Named field leaders ride with their army and project a **command radius** — brigades inside it recover morale faster, hold longer before they break, and rally from a rout sooner (the historical job of a general: rallying the line in person). And they **can be hit** — a commander who rides too long within musket range of the firing line is wounded, then falls; his command aura dies and the troops around him take a morale shock (the "the general is down!" rout trigger). **The bug-hunt earned its keep.** The first cut killed generals far too easily — the Confederate commanders who *survived* First Bull Run were dying in nearly every game, which a teaching wargame must never assert as fact. A 52-agent adversarial hunt caught it (and seven more), and the fix is now history-honest: a per-leader **historical durability** (Bee and Bartow, the real casualties, are fall-prone; the army commanders endure), accumulated risk that **decays when a general withdraws to safety**, and **cover** that shelters a commander on the reverse slope — so Jackson's stand is rewarded by position, not luck. Army commanders now survive **10 of 10** test battles; a general falling is a rare, dramatic hinge, not background noise.
