# Civil War Economy — Historical Data Digest (Phase S1 Content Layer)

> **Purpose.** Citation-grade reference for the asymmetric economy sim. Feeds the in-game codex + teaching layer. Source of truth for facts; pairs with `data/economy.json` (the machine-readable parameter set).
>
> **Provenance tags.** `[Verified]` = corroborated across the cited scholarship/primary sources. `[Inferred]` = reasonable estimate derived from verified data, flagged for designer awareness — calibrate, do not quote as hard fact.
>
> **The one sentence to remember.** The Union held *high convertible capacity* (industry, finance, rail, sea power, manpower it could actually mobilize); the Confederacy held *high wealth but low convertibility* (rich per free capita, but ~44% of that wealth was illiquid slave capital and its economy rode a single export crop strangled by blockade). The war's economic arithmetic flows from that asymmetry.

**The counterintuitive teaching hook:** free Southerners were nearly twice as rich per head as Northerners — because ~44% of Southern wealth (~$3 billion) was the market value of enslaved people. That asset was illiquid, un-mortgageable abroad, un-armable, and self-liquidating the moment emancipation became Union policy.

# 1. Union Finance

**Summary.** The Union financed its ~$3.4B war primarily by borrowing (~two-thirds via bonds), supplemented by taxation (~21%) and fiat paper (greenbacks, ~13–15%) — a balanced, credit-driven model in sharp asymmetric contrast to Confederate near-total reliance on printing. Chase + Jay Cooke mass-marketed war bonds through ~2,500 agents; the Revenue Acts created the first federal income tax + Bureau of Internal Revenue; the National Banking Acts forced national banks to hold federal bonds, creating captive demand. Result: national debt rose ~40-fold ($65M → ~$2.7–2.8B) yet inflation stayed ~80% — because legal-tender status, tax-receivability, and bond-convertibility anchored the greenback. The machinery (fiat currency, national banking, mass-market bonds, federal income/excise apparatus) outlasted the war.

### Key Facts
- Total combined direct war costs ~$3.4 billion; Union share ~two-thirds bond-financed. `[Verified]` — McPherson, *Battle Cry of Freedom*.
- US national debt: ~$65M (1860) → ~$2.7–2.8B (1865), a ~40-fold rise. `[Verified]` — US Treasury historical debt; McPherson.
- Greenbacks issued under three Legal Tender Acts: ~$447–450M (first act Feb 25, 1862 = $150M). `[Verified]` — Legal Tender Acts; Federal Reserve History.
- Union war-finance mix: loans/bonds ~66%, taxes ~21%, greenbacks ~13–15%. `[Verified]` — McPherson; Gilder Lehrman.
- Jay Cooke "5-20" bonds (6% gold interest): ~$400–500M sold. "7-30" notes (7.30%): ~$800–830M (1864–65). `[Verified]` — Larson, *Jay Cooke*; Oberholtzer.
- Cooke's nationwide sub-agents: ~2,500. `[Verified]` — Thomson, *Bonds of War*.
- Revenue Act of 1861: 3% income tax over $800. Revenue Act of 1862: 3% on $600–$10,000, 5% over $10,000 (first US progressive tax) + Bureau of Internal Revenue. `[Verified]` — Revenue Acts; IRS history.
- National banks chartered by end of 1864: 683; note issuance up to 90% of deposited bond value. `[Verified]` — Federal Reserve History.
- Northern wartime inflation ~80% total (peaks ~14% in 1862, ~25% 1863–64). `[Verified]` — Public Books; Study.com.
- Greenback gold-value low: ~39 cents (gold premium ~258:100), July 1864, before recovering with Atlanta + Lincoln re-election. `[Verified]` — Mitchell, *History of the Greenbacks*.

### Timeline
- **Aug 1861** — Revenue Act of 1861 (first federal income tax + higher tariffs). *Establishes direct-tax precedent; slow revenue forces reliance on loans.*
- **Dec 1861** — Northern banks + Treasury suspend specie. *Ends gold convertibility; clears path for greenbacks.*
- **Feb 25, 1862** — First Legal Tender Act ($150M greenbacks). *Fiat legal-tender currency funds immediate spending.*
- **Jul 1, 1862** — Revenue Act of 1862 (progressive income tax + Bureau of Internal Revenue + excises). *Durable federal tax machinery; anchors greenback value.*
- **1862–1864** — Jay Cooke markets 5-20 bonds. *~$400–500M placed; proves mass-market debt finance.*
- **Feb 25, 1863** — National Banking Act of 1863. *Captive bond demand + uniform currency.*
- **Mar 1863** — Third Legal Tender Act (total greenbacks ~$447–450M). *Feeds mid-war inflation peak.*
- **Jul 1864** — Greenback gold-value low (~39¢). *Confidence trough during Grant's stalled Overland Campaign; recovers after Atlanta.*
- **Jun 1864 → 1865** — National Banking Act of 1864 + 10% tax on state-bank notes (1865). *Drives state banks into national system; federal currency monopoly.*
- **1864–1865** — Cooke's 7-30 campaign. *~$800–830M for the costliest final year.*
- **1865** — National debt ~$2.7–2.8B. *~40-fold expansion, serviceable on industrial tax base.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`finance-greenback-vs-csa` — Why the greenback held value when the Confederate dollar collapsed.** *Consensus:* the ~80% vs ~9,000% gap traces to three Union design choices — legal-tender status, tax-receivability, bond-convertibility — anchored by a credible industrial tax base + gold customs revenue. *Debate:* McPherson/Ball stress structural/fiscal failure; Mitchell's price-of-gold data implies battlefield-confidence shocks mattered as much; monetarists (after Friedman & Schwartz) foreground sheer CS issuance quantity. *Sources:* McPherson (1988); Ball (1991); Mitchell (1903); Lerner (JPE 1955).
- **`finance-win-with-money` — Did the Union "win with money"?** *Consensus:* superior financial system gave a decisive, sustainable material edge. *Debate:* Bensel/Richardson frame it as the birth of a centralized fiscal state (causal, transformative); Gallagher warns against economic determinism (morale/leadership/contingency decided it); Ransom & Engerman stress the South's capital was locked in illiquid slaves/land. *Sources:* Bensel (1990); Richardson (1997); Gallagher (1997); Ransom & Sutch/Engerman.
- **`finance-national-banking` — The National Banking Acts: expedient or revolution?** *Consensus:* created uniform currency, captive bond market, and ended state-bank-note patchwork. *Debate:* Hammond emphasizes wartime fiscal motive; Bensel/Richardson read deliberate Republican nationalization; monetary critics note pyramided reserves + inelastic currency until the 1913 Fed. *Sources:* Hammond (1957/1970); Federal Reserve History; Bensel (1990).

# 2. Confederate Finance

**Summary.** The CSA financed its war on the printing press: ~60% of real revenue from unbacked Treasury notes, ~32% from bonds, ~8% from taxation (Ball 1991). Over-issue drove catastrophic inflation — the Lerner index rose from 100 (Jan 1861) to >9,000 (Apr 1865), ~10%/month from late 1861 to early 1864. Bonds (1861 Produce Loan, 1863 Erlanger cotton loan) raised real money but far too little; taxation came late and timid (April 1863 tax-in-kind "tithe," widely evaded). The Feb 1864 Currency Reform Act repudiated ~one-third of the money stock. By surrender a gold dollar cost >$1,000 in Confederate paper. The war's central economic morality tale: a government that prints rather than taxes destroys the wealth it claims to defend.

### Key Facts
- Cumulative inflation ~9,000% (Lerner index 100 → 9,000+, Jan 1861 → Apr 1865); ~10%/month Oct 1861–Mar 1864; cost of living ~92× prewar. `[Verified]` — EH.net (Lerner); InflationData.com; Encyclopedia.com.
- CS revenue mix: printing ~60%, bonds ~32%, taxes ~8.2%. `[Verified]` — Ball (1991) via EH.net.
- Total Treasury notes issued ~$1.7B (some est. ~$1.5B); money supply ×11.5 (Jan 1861–Oct 1864) yet prices ×90 → velocity/confidence collapse. `[Verified]` — Encyclopedia.com; EH.net.
- Erlanger cotton loan: £3M / ~$15M face, 7% coupon, 20-year; oversubscribed ~$80M; Erlanger took bonds at 77, sold ~90, +5% commission. `[Verified]` — Britannica; Tontine Coffee-House.
- Tax-in-kind ("the tithe," Apr 24, 1863): 10% of farm produce; raised only ~$62M (1863–65). `[Verified]` — DocSouth UNC.
- Currency Reform Act (Feb 17, 1864): repudiated ~1/3 of money stock; notes >$5 to 4% bonds or 2-for-3 exchange. `[Verified]` — Internet Archive; Pecquet et al.
- Gold premium (paper $ per $1 gold): $1.25 (Feb 1862) → $3 (Feb 1863) → $26 (Mar 1864) → $70–100 (Apr 1864) → $1,200 (May 1865). `[Verified]` — InflationData.com.
- Real output decline ~-40% over the war. `[Verified]` — EH.net.
- Counterfeiting (Sam Upham): ~$15M bogus notes, 1–2.5% of money supply. `[Verified]` — EH.net.
- 1861 Produce Loan: ~$50M–$100M authorized (subscribed-vs-authorized varies). `[Inferred]` — Todd, Ball.

### Timeline
- **1861** — Founded with no banking system, no tariff revenue (blockade), no specie. *Printing press is the default lever from turn one.*
- **1861–62** — Produce Loan bonds (cotton-pledged). *Cover ~32% of revenue over time but cannot scale.*
- **Feb 1862** — Dollar slips to ~$1.25/gold $1. *Inflation visibly begins.*
- **Apr 24, 1863** — Comprehensive tax act + tax-in-kind + graduated income tax. *A year late; ~$62M; breeds evasion.*
- **Mar 19, 1863** — Erlanger loan floated in Europe; oversubscribed ~$80M. *One-time foreign cash at punitive discount.*
- **Jul 1863** — Vicksburg + Gettysburg. *Erlanger bond prices slide; foreign credit window narrows.*
- **Oct 1861–Mar 1864** — Sustained ~10%/month inflation. *Flight from currency; desertion pressure.*
- **Feb 17, 1864** — Currency Reform Act repudiates ~1/3 of money. *Brief dip, then inflation resumes; confidence permanently damaged.*
- **Apr 1864** — Gold at $70–100. *Reform fails; free fall.*
- **Apr–May 1865** — Surrender; paper ~$1,200/gold $1, repudiated entirely. *Complete monetary collapse.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`csa-fin-cause-vs-symptom` — Did finance lose the war, or did the war wreck finance?** *Consensus:* Ball argues financial policy was a genuine cause of defeat — the ~60% printing reliance was a choice; the South had taxable wealth it failed to mobilize. *Debate:* Lerner and others treat inflation as a symptom of an agrarian, blockaded economy with a weak fiscal state; even flawless finance may not have overcome Union material/manpower edge. *Sources:* Ball (1991); Lerner (JPE 1955); EH.net.
- **`csa-fin-king-cotton-collateral` — King Cotton finance: brilliant collateral or fatal dependence?** *Consensus:* Erlanger showed cotton-backed bonds could attract European capital (oversubscribed). *Debate:* dependent on running cotton through the blockade + battlefield credibility; net proceeds heavily discounted; now judged a strategic miscalculation. *Sources:* Britannica; Tontine Coffee-House; Lebergott.
- **`csa-fin-tithe-state-capacity` — The tax-that-wasn't: state capacity and the tithe.** *Consensus:* taxed too little (~8%), too late; left printing to do the work. *Debate:* Bensel notes the irony of a states'-rights government building a centralized war machine yet failing to extract revenue — ideology vs administrative incapacity. *Sources:* Bensel (1990); DocSouth UNC; Ball (1991).

# 3. Industry

**Summary.** The industrial gap was the most lopsided asymmetry of the war. On the eve of war the North held ~90% of US manufacturing output, ~101,000 factories to the South's ~21,000, and ~1.1M industrial workers to ~111,000 (a 10:1 labor ratio). In war goods the gap was wider: ~93% of pig iron, ~96–97% of firearms, ~96% of locomotives. The Union turned capacity into logistics: Springfield alone made 793,434 M1861 rifle-muskets (+~882,000 from contractors). The CSA improvised an industry from nothing under Gorgas — Tredegar (~1,100 cannon, ~half of CS artillery), Augusta Powder Works (~2.75–3.1M lb world-class powder) — but made only ~107,000 shoulder arms domestically, depending on imports (~350,000–600,000 European arms, chiefly P1853 Enfields run through the blockade by Caleb Huse).

### Key Facts
- Manufacturing output ~90% North / ~10% South; ~101,000 vs ~21,000 factories; ~1.1M vs ~111,000 workers (10:1). `[Verified]` — 1860 Census; McPherson.
- Firearms ~96–97% North; pig iron ~93% North; locomotives ~96% North. `[Verified]` — 1860 Census; McPherson.
- All Southern iron furnaces combined: ~20,000 tons/year capacity. `[Verified]` — Essential Civil War Curriculum.
- Springfield M1861 production: 793,434; private contractors ~882,561. `[Verified]` — Springfield records; HistoryNet.
- Tredegar cannon: ~1,100 (~half of CS domestic artillery). `[Verified]` — American Battlefield Trust.
- Augusta Powder Works: ~2.75–3.1M lb. `[Verified]` — HistoryNet; Rains records.
- CS domestic shoulder arms total: ~107,000. European imports: ~350,000–600,000 (≥250,000 P1853 Enfields). `[Verified]` — NPS; American Rifleman; Huse records.
- Tredegar wartime workforce: ~800–2,500 (incl. enslaved). `[Inferred]` — Encyclopedia Virginia / Battlefield Trust.
- Population still in agriculture: ~40% North vs ~84% South. `[Verified]` — 1860 Census.

### Timeline
- **1860** — Census documents the ~90/10 split. *Starting asymmetry baseline.*
- **Apr 1861** — Gorgas appointed CS Ordnance Chief; Tredegar the only Southern heavy-ordnance plant. *CS improvises from near-zero.*
- **May 1861** — CS capital moved to Richmond (partly to protect Tredegar). *Ties strategic geography to its single foundry — concentration risk.*
- **1861** — Huse begins European arms procurement; first Enfields run the blockade. *CS arms supply becomes import-dependent.*
- **1861–62** — Springfield + contractors ramp; Quartermaster machine scales. *North converts capacity into mass armies.*
- **1862** — Augusta Powder Works online; Niter & Mining Bureau organized. *Powder ceases to be the binding constraint.*
- **1863** — Tredegar + armories at peak strain (iron, labor, transport shortages). *CS metal/arms output plateaus below need.*
- **Early 1864** — Gorgas's diary boast of self-sufficiency. *High-water mark of CS ordnance.*
- **1864–65** — Union output peaks; CS loses iron districts + Atlanta/Augusta corridor. *Regional production collapse.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`ind-foreordained-gap` — Was Confederate defeat foreordained by the industrial gap?** *Consensus:* the North's ~90% manufacturing + 10:1 labor edge was decisive in a long attritional war. *Debate:* Gallagher and others argue CS armies were rarely beaten for lack of weapons (Gorgas worked) — they lost on manpower, transport, food, finance, will; Beringer et al. stress internal/morale factors. *Sources:* McPherson; Gallagher; Beringer/Hattaway/Jones/Still.
- **`ind-gorgas-miracle` — Gorgas and the miracle of Confederate ordnance.** *Consensus:* Gorgas/Rains/St. John built an arms-and-powder industry from scratch; by 1864 ordnance was rarely the limiting factor. *Debate:* since only ~107k arms were made domestically (majority imported), some argue the "miracle" is overstated — the binding constraints were finance, iron, rail, food. *Sources:* Vandiver, *Ploughshares into Swords*; Encyclopedia Virginia; Ball.
- **`ind-imports-vs-domestic` — How the South actually armed itself.** *Consensus:* primarily via blockade-run imports (Huse's Enfields), not domestic production. *Debate:* "porous blockade" scholars note runners got enough through; others stress import dependence made CS supply fragile + hostage to Union naval power. Model CS arms as blockade/finance-driven, not factory-driven. *Sources:* American Rifleman; Wise, *Lifeline of the Confederacy*; Huse records.

# 4. Railroads

**Summary.** Railroads were the decisive logistical asymmetry. The North had ~22,000 track-miles to the South's ~9,000–9,500 (~70% of national mileage) plus the overwhelming share of locomotives, rolling stock, rail mills, and mechanics. The Union institutionalized military railroading (USMRR + Haupt's Construction Corps with prefab bridge trusses), operating 2,000+ miles by war's end. The CSA suffered fragmentation: ~100 small companies, incompatible gauges, unconnected lines, and no domestic capacity to replace worn rail once the blockade cut British imports. CS rail production fell to ~zero (iron diverted to ordnance), speeds collapsed from ~25 to ~10 mph by 1863, passenger service largely ceased by mid-1864. The Sep 1863 Chattanooga relief (Union: ~25,000 men, ~1,200 mi, ~11–12 days) vs Longstreet to Chickamauga (CS: ~13,000 men, ~950 mi, ~16 days, piecemeal arrival) illustrates the gap.

### Key Facts
- Mileage: ~22,000 US / ~9,000–9,500 CS (~70–71% Union share). `[Verified]` — American Battlefield Trust; Black, *Railroads of the Confederacy*.
- Equipment: ~96% of US railroad equipment in the North; CS held only ~1/5 of locomotives, ~1/3 of freight cars. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; ECWC.
- CS locomotive-manufacturing capacity ~1/24 of national; rail (iron) production ~1/8. `[Verified]` — ECWC.
- CS rail iron: ~26,000 tons/yr prewar → ~0 wartime, vs ~49,500 tons/yr needed. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; ECWC.
- ~100 separate Southern companies; predominant 5-ft broad gauge (>7,000 mi) with standard-gauge VA/NC pockets; North ~13 gauges. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; Trains Magazine.
- Train speed collapse ~25 mph (1861) → ~10 mph (1863); ~25% of CS locomotives needing repair by 1863. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust.
- USMRR at war's end: 2,000–2,100 mi, ~419 locomotives, ~6,330 cars, ~10,000 peak workers. `[Verified]` — Wikipedia USMRR; NARA Prologue.
- Haupt Construction Corps: ~650 mi track + >26 mi bridges; Potomac Creek bridge rebuilt in 9 days. `[Verified]` — Wikipedia.
- Chattanooga relief (Sep 1863): ~22,000–25,000 men, ~1,200 mi, ~11–12 days, ~7 railroads/8 states. Longstreet to Chickamauga: ~13,000 men, ~950 mi, ~16 days, ~8 transfers, ~half present for battle. `[Verified]` — Emerging Civil War; ECWC.

### Timeline
- **1861** — ~70/30 mileage advantage; CS has ~100 fragmented companies + incompatible gauges. *Structural logistics asymmetry that compounds.*
- **1862** — USMRR established; Haupt organizes Construction Corps; Potomac Creek bridge in 9 days. *Rail repair/operation becomes a renewable Union asset.*
- **1862** — Blockade chokes British rail-iron imports; CS iron diverted to ordnance. *Irreversible network decay begins (no replacement rail).*
- **1863** — CS speeds ~10 mph; ~25% locomotives need repair; Longstreet arrives piecemeal. *Operational throughput halved; CS concentration fails.*
- **1863** — Union transfers XI & XII Corps to relieve Chattanooga in ~11–12 days. *Demonstrates Union strategic mobility.*
- **1864** — Sherman's Atlanta campaign on a single rebuilt line; CS passenger service collapses. *Union projects force deep on rails.*
- **1865** — USMRR peaks; CS network near-total collapse fuels Petersburg/Richmond supply famine. *Logistics differential drives surrender.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`rail-inevitability-vs-org` — Material inevitability vs organizational failure.** *Consensus:* CS network deteriorated catastrophically (no replacement iron, collapsing speeds, aging locomotives). *Debate:* Black stresses organizational/political failure (states'-rights resistance, late nationalization); Turner leans material determinism (the South lacked iron and shops regardless). *Sources:* Black (1952); Turner (1953); Battlefield Trust.
- **`rail-decide-vs-shape` — Did railroads decide the war, or merely shape it?** *Consensus:* rails gave the Union a decisive logistical edge enabling deep penetration. *Debate:* McPherson/Nevins fold rail into broader Union dominance (caution vs single-factor determinism); Weber/Clark argue management (USMRR, Haupt, McCallum) mattered as much as mileage; others note armies still moved largely on foot/wagon (rails mattered most strategically). *Sources:* McPherson (1988); Weber; Clark (2001).
- **`rail-gauge-problem` — How decisive was the "break of gauge"?** *Consensus:* both networks were gauge-fragmented, forcing time-consuming transfers. *Debate:* one school sees gauge as second-order friction (Chattanooga: ~0–1 transfer vs ~8); revisionists say the real killer was inability to replace iron/locomotives + unconnected lines — gauge was a symptom, not root. *Sources:* Black; Trains Magazine; ECWC.

# 5. Agriculture & Provisions

**Summary.** The agricultural war was profoundly asymmetric. The North was the emerging breadbasket: in 1860 it grew ~80% of national wheat and ~50% of corn, and mechanization (>125,000 reapers by 1861, ~230,000 more sold during the war) let output rise even as ~1M men left. Northern grain became a diplomatic weapon ("King Wheat" — Britain drew ~25% of bread-grain from the US) just as "King Cotton" failed. The Confederate crisis was less production than distribution: food output actually rose in 1861–62 (Hurt), but a fragmented, decaying rail network left grain rotting while armies and cities starved. Impressment (Mar 1863) and the tithe paid ~50% below market, suppressing marketed supply, fueling hoarding, and detonating the spring 1863 bread riots (Richmond, Apr 2, 1863). Hyperinflation compounded scarcity: Richmond flour ~$30–40/barrel (autumn 1863) → ~$275 (spring 1864).

### Key Facts
- 1860 Northern share: ~80% wheat, ~50% corn, ~87.5% oats. `[Verified]` — NPS; McPherson.
- US wheat+flour exports 16M → 42M bushels (1860→1861); corn 4M → 12M. `[Verified]` — USDA/trade data summaries.
- Britain ~25% of bread-grain from US ("King Wheat"). `[Verified]` — standard diplomacy account; NPS.
- McCormick reapers: ~125,000 in use by 1861; ~230,000 more sold 1861–65. `[Verified]` — Richmond Fed; Gates.
- 1860 cotton crop ~4M bales (record); food output rose 1861–62 ("hunger amid plenty"). `[Verified]` — MDAH; Hurt (2015).
- Impressment (Mar 26, 1863) + tax-in-kind paid ~50% below market. Tithe (Apr 24, 1863): 10% of corn/wheat/sweet potatoes in kind. `[Verified]` — Encyclopedia Virginia; DocSouth UNC.
- Richmond/Lynchburg flour ~$30–40/barrel (autumn 1863) → ~$275 (spring 1864). `[Verified]` — Encyclopedia Virginia; Ball.
- Richmond Bread Riot Apr 2, 1863 (~several hundred to 1,000+ rioters). `[Verified]` — Encyclopedia Virginia; McCurry.
- 1861 US regulation ration: 12 oz pork/bacon OR 20 oz beef; ~16–22 oz bread component; CS adopted then cut from spring 1862 (cornmeal for flour). `[Verified]` — US Army Regs 1861; QM Museum.
- South less mechanized / enslaved-labor dependent → harder hit per man drafted. `[Inferred]` — reaper distribution + Gates; Ransom & Engerman.

### Timeline
- **1860** — Record cotton crop (~4M bales); North grows ~80% of wheat. *Asymmetric starting positions: US food-surplus vs CS cotton-monoculture.*
- **1861** — US grain exports to Britain surge; CS adopts US ration; ~125k reapers in Northern fields. *"King Wheat" strengthens.*
- **1861–62** — CS shifts cotton → corn; food production rises (Hurt). *Seeds of "hunger amid plenty."*
- **Spring 1862** — CS cuts ration, substitutes cornmeal; feast/famine oscillation begins. *Army morale/health hit.*
- **1862** — Drought in parts of the South; British cotton stockpile delays famine. *King Cotton diplomacy fails.*
- **Mar 26, 1863** — Impressment Act (~50% below market). *Producers hoard/evade; city supply falls.*
- **Apr 2, 1863** — Richmond Bread Riot. *Urban food access collapses amid rural plenty.*
- **Apr 24, 1863** — Tithe Act (10% in kind). *Adds collection but worsens farmer alienation.*
- **1863** — ~25% of CS locomotives need repair; speeds 25→10 mph. *Distribution craters — the decisive failure.*
- **1863–64** — Food hyperinflation (flour ~$40→$275/barrel). *Couples food to currency collapse.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`ag-hunger-amid-plenty` — Starvation or distribution failure?** *Consensus:* rail-transport + finance breakdown, not failure to grow enough, was primary (Hurt: output rose 1861–62). *Debate:* older accounts blame cotton-obsession production failure; a middle position notes labor loss + 1862 drought cut some regional output. *Sources:* Hurt (2015); Black (1952); Gates (1965).
- **`ag-king-cotton-vs-food` — Did King Cotton ever have a chance, and was food the better bet?** *Consensus:* King Cotton failed (British ~1M-bale stockpile + India/Egypt substitution; "King Wheat" cut the other way). *Debate:* Beckert stresses cotton's structural grip (reallocation harder than hindsight suggests); Owsley emphasizes diplomatic miscalculation; embargo self-imposed vs blockade-imposed disputed. *Sources:* Owsley (1931/1959); Beckert (2014); McPherson (1988).
- **`ag-impressment-tithe` — Impressment and the tithe: necessary policy or self-inflicted famine?** *Consensus:* paying ~half market value alienated farmers, encouraged hoarding, suppressed supply, helped trigger the 1863 riots. *Debate:* McCurry reads the riots as the state undone from within; some economic historians counter that with a collapsing currency, impressment-in-kind was rational — the deeper failure was monetary (Ball). *Sources:* McCurry (2010); Ball (1991); Encyclopedia Virginia.
- **`ag-reaper-mechanization` — How much did the reaper matter?** *Consensus:* mechanization let output grow even as ~1M men left, sustaining army bread + grain exports. *Debate:* some argue high prices + labor shortage merely accelerated a 1850s adoption trend (catalyst not cause); Gates stresses timing was decisive. *Sources:* Gates (1965); JEH reaper studies; Richmond Fed.

- Precise per-capita Southern corn (1860 census bushels) + year-by-year output not pinned here.
- Exact reduced CS ration weights post-spring-1862 are qualitative; consult OR Series I/IV commissary circulars.
- Richmond Bread Riot crowd size: use a range, not a point estimate.

# 6. Cotton, Blockade & Foreign Trade

**Summary.** The CSA's central gamble was "King Cotton diplomacy": Europe's dependence on Southern cotton (~77–80% of British raw cotton; ~60% of US exports by value in 1860) would force Britain/France to break the blockade and grant recognition. The 1861 self-embargo backfired — Britain entered the war with ~1M+ bales stockpiled, so withholding mainly denied the new CS government hard currency in its one year of easy export access. The Union blockade tightened from ~1-in-10 capture risk (1861) to ~1-in-3 (1864); individual runners kept ~80%+ voyage success, but aggregate cotton exports collapsed to <2M bales (vs ~17M peacetime). Europe weathered the Lancashire Cotton Famine (peak winter 1862–63, ~500,000 on relief) via stocks + Indian/Egyptian/Brazilian substitution. The 1863 Erlanger loan was the main foreign credit and collapsed worthless. Recognition never came: Trent (Nov 1861) was defused; Antietam + Emancipation (Sep 1862) made backing a slaveholding republic impossible.

### Key Facts
- Southern cotton ~77–80% of British raw cotton imports; cotton ~57–60% of total US exports (~$190–200M/yr). `[Verified]` — Liverpool/LDHI; McPherson; American Yawp.
- Prewar crop ~4–5M bales/yr (1859–60). Exports near zero by Aug 1861 (embargo + blockade). American cotton <3% of British imports during the war. `[Verified]` — ECWC; Owsley.
- Total wartime cotton exported (blockade + Union-held ports): <2M bales vs ~17M peacetime; output down ~93% by weight by 1864. `[Verified]` — Surdam (2001); Statista.
- Blockade-runner voyage success ~80–83%; capture risk ~1-in-10 (1861) → ~1-in-3 (1864); ~2,500–2,800 total attempts. `[Verified]` — Wise; Encyclopedia of Alabama.
- ~500 Union blockade ships; ~1,500 runners captured/destroyed. `[Verified]` — US Navy histories.
- Cotton arbitrage: ~3¢/lb in South vs 45¢–$1.00/lb Europe; ~$500–600/bale delivered; Banshee returned ~700% on 8 trips. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; USNI.
- British stockpile entering war ~1M+ bales (~1 yr+ buffer); Indian output +~70% over the 1860s. `[Verified]` — King Cotton synthesis; Battlefield Trust.
- Lancashire Cotton Famine peak ~500,000 on relief; ~60% of cotton-town labor idle by Nov 1862. `[Verified]` — Lancashire scholarship.
- Erlanger loan: £3M face (~$15M), 7% coupon, 20-yr, cotton-backed; net to CS ~£2.16M (~72%); defaulted/worthless at war's end. `[Verified]` — Britannica; Tontine; Ball; Weidenmier (NBER).

### Timeline
- **Apr 1861** — Lincoln proclaims blockade ("Anaconda Plan"). *Legal basis for naval strangulation; initially porous.*
- **Summer 1861** — Quasi-official cotton embargo. *Backfires: denies CS hard currency in its one easy-export year.*
- **Nov–Dec 1861** — Trent Affair. *War scare with Britain, defused — recognition crisis passes.*
- **1862** — European stocks drawn down; Lancashire famine sets in; India/Egypt/Brazil substitution begins.
- **Sep 1862** — Antietam + preliminary Emancipation Proclamation. *British cabinet shelves recognition — decisive diplomatic turning point.*
- **Winter 1862–63** — Famine peak (~500,000 on relief). *Yet pro-Union working-class sentiment + substitution prevent intervention.*
- **Mar 1863** — Erlanger loan floated. *CS nets ~£2.16M at 7%; props exchange briefly.*
- **1863** — India/Egypt/Brazil output ramps. *Permanently erodes King Cotton leverage.*
- **1864** — Blockade tightens (capture ~1-in-3); ports closing. *Exports near ~1/9 of peacetime.*
- **Jan 1865** — Fort Fisher falls, closing Wilmington (last major runner port). *Severs the ocean lifeline; Erlanger bonds collapse.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`cotton-king-chance` — Did King Cotton diplomacy ever have a chance?** *Consensus:* it failed (prewar surplus + substitution + slavery politics); the 1861 self-embargo made it worse. *Debate:* Owsley saw recognition as near-run; Jones/Blackett argue British non-intervention was overdetermined by domestic politics + anti-slavery opinion — cotton never could override it. *Sources:* Owsley (1931); Jones, *Blue & Gray Diplomacy*; Blackett.
- **`cotton-blockade-decisive` — How decisive was the blockade?** *Consensus:* it throttled aggregate exports (~1/9 of peacetime) + starved CS of foreign exchange. *Debate:* Owsley's "leaky sieve" (high per-voyage success); Surdam counters the right metric is aggregate economic effect (strategically decisive); Wise credits runners as a lifeline of inadequate volume. *Sources:* Surdam (2001); Wise; Owsley.
- **`cotton-runner-help-hurt` — Did blockade running help or hurt?** *Consensus:* supplied essential munitions, kept the CS in the fight, but volume too small + incentives distorted. *Debate:* arbitrage drew runners to luxury cargo + drained specie; defenders cite late-war cargo-quota regulation + Wilmington as a true lifeline. *Sources:* Battlefield Trust; Wise; Ball.
- **`cotton-erlanger` — The Erlanger loan: lifeline or fleecing?** *Consensus:* the principal CS foreign credit; cotton-backing attractive while CS arms looked viable. *Debate:* Ball stresses ruinous terms (~72% of face, 7%); others note the structure was innovative and the collapse merely tracked battlefield defeat; Weidenmier uses bond prices as a real-time survival barometer. *Sources:* Ball; Britannica; Weidenmier (NBER).

# 7. Manpower & Conscription

**Summary.** Manpower was the most lopsided structural advantage: ~22M loyal-state people vs ~9M Confederate, of whom ~3.5M were enslaved and could not be armed (until a futile 1865 reversal). Both began with volunteers; as 12-month enlistments expired, the CSA enacted the first national American conscription (Apr 16, 1862), followed by the Union Enrollment Act (Mar 3, 1863). The Union draft was less a direct source than a coercive stimulus to volunteering + bounties — only ~9,881 names drawn in 1863 were directly held to personal service; the real engine was ~2.1M cumulative enlistments, ~25% foreign-born, plus ~179,000–186,000 USCT (~10% of the army) after 1863. The CSA faced a deepening crisis: conscription widened to ages 17–50 by Feb 1864, class-resentment exemptions (the "Twenty-Negro Law"), an un-armable enslaved base, and ~104,000+ recorded desertions. The Union could absorb and replace casualties; the CS could not, and ran out of men before it ran out of will.

### Key Facts
- Union cumulative enlistments ~2,128,948. CS total served ~750,000–1,000,000+ (commonly ~850k–900k; records lost). `[Verified]` US / `[Inferred]` CS — Official Records; Livermore; McPherson.
- Population: ~22M North vs ~9M South (~3.5M enslaved). `[Verified]` — 1860 Census.
- Union army ~25% foreign-born (~500,000+; ~43% incl. sons of immigrants); ~200,000 German-born, ~150,000 Irish-born. `[Verified]` — Lonn.
- USCT ~179,000–186,000 (~10% of the army; ~178,000 Black enlisted + ~7,000 white officers). `[Verified]` — National Archives.
- 1863 federal draft: 292,441 names drawn; ~88,171 "drafted"; only 9,881 personally held to service; ~52,000+ paid the $300 commutation; ~26,000 furnished substitutes. `[Verified]` — Geary, *We Need Men*.
- Conscription supplied only ~6% of the Union army directly. `[Verified]` — McPherson; Geary.
- $300 commutation fee (repealed Jul 1864 except COs); $300 federal bounty + state/local up to ~$1,000. `[Verified]` — Enrollment Act; Murdock.
- Union peak ~1,000,000 on rolls; ~800,000+ present for duty May 1865. `[Verified]` — Official Records.
- First CS Conscription (Apr 16, 1862): ages 18–35. Twenty-Negro Law (Oct 11, 1862): 1 exemption per 20+ enslaved. Feb 17, 1864 expansion: ages 17–50. `[Verified]` — CS conscription/exemption acts.
- Recorded CS desertions ~104,428 (~103,400 enlisted + ~1,028 officers). `[Verified]` — Lonn, *Desertion*.
- CS Black-soldier law signed Mar 13, 1865 (GO No. 14, Mar 23): yielded only dozens (vs hoped ~200,000–300,000). `[Verified]` recruits / `[Inferred]` hoped — Richmond Whig; Levine.
- CS military-age white male pool ~1,000,000–1,100,000 (18–45). `[Inferred]` — 1860 Census; McPherson.

### Timeline
- **1861** — Both raise massive volunteer armies; first $100 federal bounty. *Manpower abundant; North's deeper pool latent.*
- **Apr 16, 1862** — CS enacts first national conscription (18–35). *Stops bleed of expiring enlistments; begins class resentment.*
- **Oct 11, 1862** — Twenty-Negro Law. *Recurring CS morale/legitimacy debuff: "rich man's war, poor man's fight."*
- **Jan 1, 1863** — Emancipation Proclamation opens Black enlistment; USCT organized. *Unlocks ~180,000 men from the South's labor base — a double-swing.*
- **Mar 3, 1863** — Union Enrollment Act (20–45; $300 commutation + substitution). *Draft mainly stimulates volunteering (~6% serve via draft).*
- **Jul 13–16, 1863** — NYC Draft Riots. *Largest civil insurrection of the war; political cost made visible.*
- **Dec 28, 1863** — CS abolishes substitution. *Closes a class loophole; deepens elite resentment.*
- **Feb 17, 1864** — CS expands conscription to 17–50. *Scrapes the bottom of the barrel — a visible desperation tell.*
- **Jul 4, 1864** — Union repeals $300 commutation (except COs). *Draft bites harder; bounty inflation.*
- **Late 1864–1865** — CS desertion accelerates as Union armies penetrate South. *Replacement ratio falls below 1; armies melt away.*
- **Mar 13/23, 1865** — Davis signs Black-soldier law (GO No. 14). *Too late: only dozens recruited.*
- **May 1865** — Union army at peak (~1M on rolls) musters out. *Decisive end-state.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`mp-draft-supply` — Did the Union draft actually supply soldiers?** *Consensus:* very few direct conscripts (~6%; 9,881 held in 1863); its real function was coercive (drove districts to bounty-paid volunteers). *Debate:* Geary quantifies the small direct yield; others say focusing on "held to service" understates the draft's structural importance to the bounty/volunteer system. *Sources:* McPherson; Geary; Murdock.
- **`mp-rich-mans-war` — How unfair were the drafts?** *Consensus:* both had class-skewed escape hatches (commutation/substitution; Twenty-Negro Law) that provoked unrest. *Debate:* older populist readings take the slogan literally; revisionists argue commutation was a price *cap*, communities pooled funds, and many wealthy did serve — grievance real but partly symbolic. *Sources:* Murdock; Geary; Encyclopedia Virginia.
- **`mp-arm-the-enslaved` — Could the CS have solved its manpower crisis by arming the enslaved?** *Consensus:* its base was capped by refusal to arm ~3.5M enslaved until the final weeks; the 1865 law yielded dozens. *Debate:* Levine argues arming/freeing them would have gutted the social order the South fought to preserve (politically impossible until total desperation); mainstream scholarship (Levine; Kevin Levin) holds Black men served overwhelmingly as impressed laborers, not soldiers. *Sources:* Levine, *Confederate Emancipation*; Kevin Levin; McPherson.
- **`mp-demographic-superiority` — How decisive was Union demographic superiority?** *Consensus:* the ~22M:9M edge (with ~3.5M un-armable) gave a deeper, replenishable pool. *Debate:* Gallagher cautions against pure determinism (numbers had to be mobilized; the North could have lost politically in 1864); Beringer et al. emphasize internal CS factors. Synthesis: demography set the odds; contingency/morale decided realization. *Sources:* Gallagher; Beringer et al.; McPherson.
- **`mp-immigrants-usct` — How important were immigrants and USCT?** *Consensus:* ~25% foreign-born + ~180,000 USCT (~10%) — substantial pools the CS could not match. *Debate:* USCT faced unequal pay (until 1864), non-combat assignments, massacre risk (Fort Pillow); nativist narratives downplayed immigrants. Contributions documented; political/social meaning is the live debate. *Sources:* Lonn; National Archives; Glatthaar, *Forged in Battle*.

# 8. Comparative Macro Snapshot (1860)

**Summary.** In 1860 the future Union held a crushing aggregate-resource advantage. ~22M free people vs ~5.5M free + ~3.5–4M enslaved (9M total) — a ~4:1 free-population edge. The North produced ~90% of US manufacturing, held ~85% of factories, ~92% of iron/steel, and dominated firearms (≈32:1), pig iron (≈20:1), textiles (≈17:1). It had ~21,000–22,000 of ~31,000 rail miles, the overwhelming majority of banking capital/specie, essentially the entire merchant marine, and the only standing navy. The South's economy was agrarian/export-driven: ~75% of the world's cotton (~60% of all US exports, ~$190–200M/yr), free population richer per capita — but ~44% of wealth (~$3B) was locked in enslaved people, illiquid and self-destructing on emancipation. The honest fork: the South was *rich* (high free per-capita wealth, profitable, growing) yet *structurally fragile* for total war. Model "high wealth, low convertibility" for CS vs "high convertible capacity" for US.

### Key Facts
- Free pop ~22M US / ~5.5M CS; total ~22M / ~9M; enslaved ~3.5M (CS) / ~3.95M (US total). `[Verified]` — 1860 Census; McPherson; Ransom (EH.net).
- Population shares ~71% Union / ~29% Confederacy (of 31.4M). `[Verified]` — 1860 Census.
- Manufacturing ~90/10; factories ~85–90% North (~101k vs ~21k); iron/steel ~92% North. `[Verified]` — NPS; 1860 Census.
- Output ratios (North:South): firearms ~32:1, pig iron ~20:1, textiles ~17:1, leather ~30:1. `[Verified]` — NPS; New Georgia Encyclopedia.
- Railroads ~21,000–22,000 mi US / ~9,022 mi CS. `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; McPherson.
- Cotton ~75% of world supply; ~57–60% of US exports (~$190–200M/yr). `[Verified]` — American Yawp; OpenEd CUNY.
- Enslaved-people capital ~$3B (1860 $); ~44% of Southern wealth (real estate only ~25%). `[Verified]` — Ransom (1989).
- Per-capita wealth, free pop: ~$1,043 South vs ~$546 North (South RICHER). `[Verified]` — Wright (1978); EH.net.
- Per-capita income: North ~$195; South ~$300 (free only) but ~$100+ if enslaved counted. `[Verified]` — Engerman & Gallman; EH.net.
- Banking: New Orleans specie ~$13.6M (> rest of South combined) vs New York ~$26.4M. `[Verified]` — Tontine; Ball.
- Navy ~42 commissioned US / ~0 CS at outbreak. Merchant marine ~95% Union-flagged (~2.4M tons; ~$926M seaborne trade). `[Verified]` — Battlefield Trust; USNI; usmm.org.
- Agriculture (aggregate): corn ~280M bu CS vs ~396M bu US; wheat 31M vs 114M bu; swine ~15.5M CS vs ~11.3M US (South ahead in hogs). `[Verified]` — 1860 Ag Census.
- Urbanization: Northeast ~36% vs South ~7%. `[Verified]` — EH.net.
- CS share of national wealth (incl. slave capital) ~30%. `[Inferred]` — derived from Ransom/Engerman.

### Timeline
- **1860** — Census documents the 4:1 free-pop gap, 90/10 manufacturing, 21k vs 9k rail miles, ~$3B slave capital. *Sets every starting parameter.*
- **1860–61** — King Cotton diplomacy / voluntary embargo. *Backfires; CS forfeits peak export revenue just when it needs hard currency.*
- **1861** — Union declares blockade (US ~42 warships vs CS ~0). *Progressively chokes the cotton engine + war-materiel imports.*
- **1861** — CS issues unbacked Treasury notes (thin banking base, no specie). *Opens the hyperinflation trajectory.*
- **1862** — Legal Tender Act, Pacific Railway Act, Homestead Act, National Banking groundwork; Jay Cooke markets bonds. *Union converts financial depth into sustainable war finance.*
- **1863** — Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1); Southern bread riots (Apr). *Detonates the ~$3B slave-capital asset; prices already ×13.*
- **1863** — National Banking Act creates uniform currency + bond market. *Cements Union financial superiority institutionally.*
- **1864–65** — CS currency collapses toward zero (~9,000% cumulative). *Low-finance start matures into total monetary failure.*

### Historiographical Debate Cards
- **`macro-rich-or-poor` — Was the South rich or poor in 1860?** *Consensus:* both true — by convertible capacity ~10% of the North, yet richest region by free per-capita wealth (~$1,043 vs ~$546) because ~44% of wealth was the ~$3B slave market value. Wealthy but illiquid, single-crop, self-liquidating. *Debate:* Wright + Ransom & Sutch stress slavery retarded development (wealth without development); Fogel & Engerman controversially argued slave agriculture was efficient/profitable — contested by Gutman, David et al. on method + morality. *Sources:* Wright (1978); Ransom & Sutch (1977); Ransom (1989); Fogel & Engerman (1974) + critiques.
- **`macro-inevitability` — Did the Union's material advantage make victory inevitable?** *Consensus:* the 1860 numbers gave durable advantages in resources that win long industrial wars; the Union's ability to CONVERT them (bonds, banking, rail, blockade) was decisive. *Debate:* McPherson + Gallagher insist contingency mattered (battlefield, leadership, 1864 election, CS resolve); the South only had to outlast Northern will. *Design rule: do NOT auto-resolve from the macro snapshot — make CS victory reachable via morale/endurance + US war-weariness levers, not GDP alone.* *Sources:* McPherson (1988); Gallagher (1997); Beringer et al. (1986).
- **`macro-csa-finance-doomed` — Confederate finance: doomed at the start, or mismanaged?** *Consensus:* the 1860 base was structurally thin (few banks, little specie, illiquid main asset + export); reliance on the press → ~9,000% inflation. *Debate:* Ball argues substantial policy failure (taxed too little/late, printed too much; earlier taxation + cotton-bonds could have prolonged the war); others see severe inflation as nearly unavoidable. *Design rule: give CS a real but narrow finance lever — early-tax / cotton-bond options that mitigate but never fully escape the inflation curve.* *Sources:* Ball (1991); EH.net; Burdekin.
