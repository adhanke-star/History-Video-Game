## First Bull Run — UNION Order of Battle

# Union Order of Battle — First Battle of Bull Run (First Manassas), 21 July 1861

## Army of Northeastern Virginia
**Commanding General:** Brig. Gen. Irvin McDowell
**Staff (Verified):** Chief of Artillery — Maj. William F. Barry; Chief Engineer — Maj. John G. Barnard; Chief Quartermaster — Capt. Otis H. Tillinghast (mortally wounded).

**Strength & casualties (contested — sources vary):**
- Total present in the army: **~35,000** (with one widely-used compilation citing **40,476** "aggregate" including the rear-area Fourth Division). *Medium confidence — the spread reflects what counts as "the army."*
- Effectives: **~28,452**; actually engaged on 21 July: **~18,000** (the four engaged divisions; Runyon's reserve never reached the field). *High confidence on the ~18,000 engaged figure.*
- Union casualties: **2,708** per the standard count (481 killed, 1,011 wounded, 1,216 missing/captured); some compilations give **2,896**. *Medium confidence — both figures appear in reputable sources; the difference is missing/straggler accounting.*

McDowell's plan: feint at the Stone Bridge with Tyler's First Division while Hunter's Second and Heintzelman's Third Divisions made a wide flank march northwest, crossing Bull Run at **Sudley Springs Ford** (~12,000 men) to roll up the Confederate left. Miles's Fifth Division guarded Centreville and the Blackburn's Ford approaches; Runyon's Fourth Division (reserve) was strung out along the road to the rear and never engaged.

## FIRST DIVISION — Brig. Gen. Daniel Tyler
*(~9,936 present; casualties ~1,012. The largest division; opened the battle at the Stone Bridge and later fed brigades across to Henry House Hill.)*

### First Brigade — Col. Erasmus D. Keyes
- 1st Connecticut Infantry (Lt. Col. John Speidel)
- 2nd Connecticut Infantry (Col. Alfred H. Terry)
- 3rd Connecticut Infantry (Col. John L. Chatfield)
- 2nd Maine Infantry (Col. Charles D. Jameson)
- *Engagement:* Late afternoon attack along the foot of **Henry House Hill** near the Robinson House / Stone Bridge; advance was checked by Confederate fire and the brigade drifted off without pressing the assault. Brigade casualties ~223.

### Second Brigade — Brig. Gen. Robert C. Schenck
- 2nd New York State Militia (Col. George W. B. Tompkins) *(later redesignated 82nd NY Vol. Inf.)*
- 1st Ohio Infantry, 3-months (Col. Alexander M. McCook)
- 2nd Ohio Infantry, 3-months (Lt. Col. Rodney Mason)
- Battery E, 2nd U.S. Artillery — 6 guns (Capt. James H. Carlisle)
- Battery G, 1st U.S. Artillery (2nd Section), part — 1 gun (Lt. Peter C. Hains)
- *Engagement:* Remained on the **Warrenton Turnpike near the Stone Bridge** as part of the demonstration; lightly engaged. Casualties ~98.

### Third Brigade — Col. William T. Sherman
- 13th New York Infantry (Col. Isaac F. Quinby)
- 69th New York State Militia (Col. Michael Corcoran — wounded & captured; then Capt. James Kelly)
- 79th New York "Highlanders" (Col. James Cameron — **killed**)
- 2nd Wisconsin Infantry (Lt. Col. Henry W. Peck)
- Battery E, 3rd U.S. Artillery — 6 guns (Capt. Romeyn B. Ayres)
- *Engagement:* Crossed Bull Run at an upper ford after seeing Hunter's column; assaulted **Henry House Hill** in piecemeal regimental waves (13th NY, 2nd WI, 79th NY, 69th NY) that were repulsed in succession. Took the heaviest brigade losses in the division — **~605 casualties** (the 79th and 69th NY were shattered).

### Fourth Brigade — Col. Israel B. Richardson
- 1st Massachusetts Infantry (Col. Robert Cowdin)
- 12th New York Infantry (Col. Ezra L. Walrath)
- 2nd Michigan Infantry (Maj. Adolphus W. Williams)
- 3rd Michigan Infantry (Col. Daniel McConnell / Lt. Col. Ambrose A. Stevens)
- Battery M, 2nd U.S. Artillery — 4 guns (Capt. Henry J. Hunt)
- Battery G, 1st U.S. Artillery (1st Section) — 2 guns (Lt. John Edwards Jr.)
- *Engagement:* Detached southeast to demonstrate against **Blackburn's Ford** (under Miles's tactical control for much of the day); not at Matthews/Henry Hill. Light casualties ~80. Covered the retreat.

- 11th Massachusetts Infantry (Col. George Clark Jr.)
- 1st Minnesota Infantry (Col. Willis A. Gorman)
- 4th Pennsylvania Infantry (Col. John F. Hartranft) — **DID NOT FIGHT**: its 90-day enlistment expired and the regiment marched to the rear on the morning of 21 July despite McDowell's personal appeal (Hartranft stayed as a volunteer aide). *Verified.*

### Third Brigade — Col. Oliver O. Howard

Howard's collapse triggered the general Union rout. *Verified.*

## Engagement summary by terrain feature

**Matthews Hill (morning, ~10 a.m.–noon — Union high-water mark):**
- Burnside's 2nd Brigade (2nd Division) — opened the infantry fight (2nd RI leading).
- Porter's 1st Brigade (2nd Division) — Sykes's regulars, 8th/14th NY Militia, 27th NY; Griffin's battery.
- Sherman's and Keyes's brigades (1st Division) crossed and joined the pursuit as Confederates fell back toward Henry Hill.
- Result: Confederate left (Evans, Bee, Bartow) driven off the hill — the moment of greatest Union success.

**Henry House Hill (afternoon, ~1–4 p.m. — decisive Confederate stand):**
- Griffin's (Porter's bde) and Ricketts's (Franklin's bde) regular batteries — advanced to the crest and lost.
- Sherman's 3rd Brigade (13th NY, 2nd WI, 79th NY, 69th NY) — piecemeal repulsed assaults.
- Willcox's 2nd Brigade (11th NY Fire Zouaves, 38th NY, 1st & 4th Michigan).
- Franklin's 1st Brigade (1st Minnesota, 11th MA, 5th MA Militia).
- Keyes's 1st Brigade (1st Division) — abortive flanking attempt along the hill's eastern foot.
- This is where "Stonewall" Jackson's Virginians held, Stuart's cavalry charged the Zouaves, and the captured batteries decided the day.

**Chinn Ridge (late afternoon, ~4 p.m. — collapse):**
- Howard's 3rd Brigade (3rd/4th/5th Maine, 2nd Vermont) — the only Union brigade to fight on Chinn Ridge; overwhelmed by Confederate reinforcements (Kirby Smith/Elzey, Early) and routed, beginning the army-wide retreat to Centreville and Washington.

**Not engaged on the main field:** Runyon's entire 4th Division (reserve); Miles's 5th Division less Blenker's rear-guard role at Centreville; Richardson's 4th Brigade and Davies's 2nd Brigade (Blackburn's Ford demonstration).

- **Strength totals (Inferred/contested):** ~35,000 present vs. 40,476 aggregate; ~18,000 engaged is the consistent operative figure.
- **Casualties (contested):** 2,708 (standard, from the Official Records tabulation) vs. 2,896 (some compilations).
- **"Bookwood's NY Battery" (Low confidence):** name appears only in the Wikipedia OOB; likely garbled or a small/unattached section. Flagged Inferred.
- **4th Pennsylvania & 8th NY Militia departure (Verified):** the 4th PA's 90-day term expired 20 July and it marched to the rear on the 21st; the 8th NY Militia's term was also expiring (ordered home 24 July). The oft-cited image of regiments marching away "to the sound of the guns" refers chiefly to the 4th PA and the battery of the 8th NY Militia / Varian's NY battery.
- Brigade-level strength/casualty splits (e.g., Sherman ~605, Porter ~464, Keyes ~223) come from the Bull Runnings compilation of the Official Records — **High confidence** but rounded.

## First Bull Run — CONFEDERATE Order of Battle

# Confederate Order of Battle — First Battle of Bull Run / First Manassas (July 21, 1861)

## Strategic Frame

The Confederate force on Bull Run was a **combination of two field armies** operating under unity of command for the day:

- **Army of the Potomac** — Brig. Gen. **P. G. T. Beauregard**, the larger force (~21,000–22,000), entrenched along Bull Run behind a string of fords from Union Mills to the Stone Bridge.
- **Army of the Shenandoah** — Brig. Gen. **Joseph E. Johnston** (~11,000–12,000), which had escaped Maj. Gen. Robert Patterson in the Lower Shenandoah Valley.

**Johnston was the senior officer** (full brigadier general by an earlier commission) and held overall command, but he deferred substantially to Beauregard's tactical dispositions because Beauregard knew the ground. Combined strength is commonly cited at **~32,000–34,000 men present**, of whom only **~18,000 were actually engaged** in the decisive combat on the Confederate left around Matthews Hill and Henry House Hill. (Strength figures are *Inferred / approximate* — period returns are imperfect; the engaged-vs-present distinction is well established.)

**The railroad pivot (Verified):** Johnston's army began transferring from Winchester via the Manassas Gap Railroad on/after July 18–19, 1861. This is frequently described as **the first time in history troops were moved to a battlefield by rail to achieve strategic concentration.** Johnston himself reached Manassas Junction on **July 20**; some of his brigades (notably Kirby Smith/Elzey's) did not detrain until the **afternoon of July 21**, arriving directly onto the battlefield — a fact decisive to the outcome.

**Note on structure:** There were **no divisions.** The Confederate army fought as ~13 independent brigades plus unattached units and cavalry. Brigade numbering ("First Brigade," etc.) belongs to each parent army separately.

## ARMY OF THE SHENANDOAH — Brig. Gen. Joseph E. Johnston

**Staff:** Chief of Artillery Col. William N. Pendleton; Chief Engineer Maj. **William H. C. Whiting**.

### First Brigade ("The Stonewall Brigade") — Brig. Gen. Thomas J. Jackson (wounded, hand)
~**2,600 men**
- 2nd Virginia — Col. James W. Allen
- 4th Virginia — Col. James F. Preston
- 5th Virginia — Col. Kenton Harper
- 27th Virginia (7 cos.) — Lt. Col. John Echols
- 33rd Virginia (8 cos.) — Col. Arthur C. Cummings
- Rockbridge (Va.) Artillery — Lt. John P. Brockenbrough

**KEY (Verified):** Jackson posted his five Virginia regiments and guns on the **reverse (eastern) slope of Henry House Hill**, screened in tall grass behind massed artillery — a disciplined defensive line onto which the broken brigades of Bee, Bartow, and Evans rallied. This is the origin of the **"Stonewall"** sobriquet (see contested wording below). The 33rd Virginia's later charge captured Union guns; Jackson's general advance helped precipitate the Federal rout. Jackson was wounded in the hand.

### Second Brigade — Col. Francis S. Bartow (KILLED) → Col. Lucius J. Gartrell
- 7th Georgia — Col. Lucius J. Gartrell
- 8th Georgia — Lt. Col. William M. Gardner (wounded)
- Wise (Va.) Artillery — Lt. **John Pelham** (later the famed "gallant" horse-artillerist)

**KEY (Verified):** Bartow led his Georgians to reinforce Evans on Matthews Hill, then fell back to Henry Hill where he was **killed** rallying the 7th Georgia — one of the highest-ranking Confederate officers to die in the battle.

### Third Brigade — Brig. Gen. Barnard E. Bee, Jr. (MORTALLY WOUNDED) → Col. States Rights Gist
- 4th Alabama — Col. Egbert Jones (mortally wounded)
- 2nd Mississippi — Col. William C. Falkner *(great-grandfather of novelist William Faulkner)*
- 11th Mississippi, Cos. A & K — Lt. Col. Philip F. Liddell
- 6th North Carolina — Col. Charles F. Fisher (killed)

## The Three Tactical Anchors (Verified narrative)

1. **Evans on Matthews Hill (morning, ~9:30 a.m.–11 a.m.):** ~1,100 men of a demi-brigade detected and delayed the main Union turning movement (Hunter/Heintzelman via Sudley Ford), reinforced to ~2,800 with Bee and Bartow, buying the time without which the army's left would have been turned.
2. **Jackson on Henry House Hill (midday–early afternoon):** the Stonewall Brigade's reverse-slope defense formed the rally point; the broken left re-formed on it; the "Stonewall" name was born.
3. **Late arrivals on Chinn Ridge (~3:30–4:30 p.m.):** Early's brigade (from the right) + Elzey's (ex-Kirby Smith, off the train) flanked the Union right; combined with Jackson's counterattack and Stuart's cavalry, the Federal line collapsed into the "Great Skedaddle" back toward Washington.

## First Bull Run — Engagement Flow, Terrain & Casualties

## First Battle of Bull Run (First Manassas) — July 21, 1861

The first major land battle of the American Civil War, fought along Bull Run creek near Manassas Junction in Prince William and Fairfax Counties, Virginia, roughly 25–30 miles southwest of Washington, D.C. It pitted **Brig. Gen. Irvin McDowell's** Union Army of Northeastern Virginia against the Confederate forces of **Brig. Gen. P.G.T. Beauregard** (Army of the Potomac) and **Brig. Gen. Joseph E. Johnston** (Army of the Shenandoah). Johnston, as the senior officer present, held overall Confederate command, though Beauregard managed most of the tactical fight. The result was a Confederate victory and a humiliating Union rout.

---

### Strategic Setup: The Railroad That Won the Battle

Under political pressure ("On to Richmond!"), McDowell marched ~35,000 men out of Washington on July 16, 1861. His plan targeted Beauregard's ~22,000 Confederates posted behind Bull Run guarding Manassas Junction — a critical rail hub where the **Orange & Alexandria Railroad** met the **Manassas Gap Railroad**.

The decisive strategic stroke was logistical. In the Shenandoah Valley, the aging Union general **Robert Patterson** (a War of 1812 veteran, ~18,000 men) was supposed to pin Johnston's ~11,000-man Army of the Shenandoah. Instead, Johnston slipped away behind a cavalry screen (J.E.B. Stuart's troopers) on July 18, marched to **Piedmont Station**, and entrained his infantry on the **Manassas Gap Railroad** to reinforce Beauregard — the **first use of railroads to move troops strategically to a battlefield in history** (high confidence, widely cited). These fresh brigades, arriving July 19–21 (some on the very day of battle), proved decisive.

A preliminary clash occurred July 18 at **Blackburn's Ford**, where Brig. Gen. Daniel Tyler's probe of the Confederate right was repulsed by Brig. Gen. James Longstreet's brigade. This convinced McDowell to shift his main effort to the Confederate **left (northwest) flank**.

**McDowell's plan for July 21:** Tyler's division would demonstrate at the **Stone Bridge** (Warrenton Turnpike crossing) to fix the Confederate left, while the divisions of **Brig. Gen. David Hunter** and **Brig. Gen. Samuel P. Heintzelman** (~12,000–13,000 men) made a wide flanking march to cross Bull Run upstream at **Sudley Springs Ford** and roll up the exposed Confederate flank. (Ironically, Beauregard had drafted a near-mirror-image plan to attack the Union left — both armies planned to turn each other's left, but McDowell moved first.)

---

### Hour-by-Hour Timeline (July 21, 1861)

**~2:30 a.m. — The flank march begins.** McDowell's columns left Centreville. Tyler's division clogged the Warrenton Turnpike, delaying Hunter and Heintzelman, whose flanking route proved to be little more than a cart path. The column fell roughly 2.5–3.5 hours behind schedule.

**~5:15–5:30 a.m. — Opening shots.** Union artillery opened the action. Most accounts credit **Lt. Peter Conover Hains** firing a **30-pounder Parrott rifle** at the Stone Bridge, signaling Tyler's demonstration. (Note: some accounts place the very first shots at ~6 a.m. when the flanking column met pickets near Sudley — minor source disagreement.)

**~8:30–9:00 a.m. — The flank is spotted.** Confederate signal officer **Capt. Edward Porter Alexander**, using wig-wag semaphore from Signal Hill several miles away, detected the dust and glint of the Union flanking column and signaled **Col. Nathan "Shanks" Evans**: *"Look out for your left, your position is turned."* This early warning is one of the first significant uses of military signal communication in the war (high confidence).

**~9:30–10:00 a.m. — Sudley Ford crossing & Matthews Hill.** Hunter's and Heintzelman's divisions forded Bull Run at Sudley Springs. Evans, with only ~1,100 men (including **Maj. Roberdeau Wheat's 1st Louisiana Special Battalion — "Wheat's Tigers"**), pulled most of his small force away from the Stone Bridge and rushed north to **Matthews Hill** to delay the Federals. Wheat's spoiling attack bought time but Wheat was severely wounded (shot through both lungs — famously survived).

**~10:00–11:30 a.m. — The Matthews Hill fight.** Evans was reinforced by the brigades of **Brig. Gen. Barnard E. Bee** and **Col. Francis S. Bartow**, raising the Confederate flank force to ~2,800. Regiments from eight states fought here (NY, RI, NH, SC, LA, AL, GA, MS). Union pressure mounted as **Col. Ambrose Burnside's** brigade and the U.S. Regulars under **Maj. George Sykes** pressed forward, and **Col. William T. Sherman** found an unguarded ford and crossed to strike the Confederate flank. The outnumbered (~4,500 vs. ~10,000+) Confederate line collapsed and retreated in disorder south across the Warrenton Turnpike and **Young's Branch** up onto **Henry House Hill**. By late morning the battle seemed a Union victory.

**~Noon–1:00 p.m. — Jackson forms his line on Henry House Hill.** **Brig. Gen. Thomas J. Jackson** posted his Virginia Brigade (~2,000 men, five regiments — 2nd, 4th, 5th, 27th, 33rd Virginia) on the **reverse slope** of Henry Hill, sheltering them from direct fire while massing ~13 guns on the crest. **Col. Wade Hampton's** Hampton's Legion and **Col. J.E.B. Stuart's** cavalry bought time on the flanks. This is the moment of the famous naming.

**The "Stonewall" Quote (contested).** As Bee's broken brigade fell back, he is said to have pointed to Jackson and exclaimed words to the effect of: *"There is Jackson standing like a stone wall! Rally behind the Virginians!"* (NPS variant: *"Yonder stands Jackson like a stone wall. Let us go to his assistance."*). **Confidence: the naming is established; the exact wording and even Bee's intent are contested.** Bee was mortally wounded shortly afterward and died the next day (July 22) without filing a report, so no firsthand account survives. Some contemporaries argued Bee spoke in frustration — that Jackson stood "like a stone wall" *instead of advancing to help*. The heroic interpretation became canonical and gave Jackson and his brigade their enduring nickname.

**~1:00–3:00 p.m. — The artillery duel & the climax.** McDowell ordered the rifled batteries of **Capt. James B. Ricketts** (Battery I, 1st U.S. Artillery) and **Capt. Charles Griffin** (Battery D, 5th U.S. Artillery) forward to within ~300 yards of Jackson's line — too close for their long-range rifled guns to be effective against Confederate smoothbores firing canister. In a pivotal confusion, the **33rd Virginia**, wearing **blue uniforms**, advanced; Griffin's gunners were ordered not to fire, mistaking them for friendly infantry. The 33rd Virginia (with Stuart's cavalry striking the 11th New York "Fire Zouaves") delivered a point-blank volley that wiped out the gun crews. The batteries were captured, recaptured, and lost again in repeated charges — their loss broke the back of the Union assault. During the fighting, **85-year-old widow Judith Carter Henry**, bedridden in the Henry House, was struck by shell fragments and killed — the battle's only civilian fatality and the namesake of Henry Hill.

**~4:00 p.m. — Chinn Ridge and the collapse of the Union right.** Fresh Confederate brigades from the Shenandoah arrived by the Manassas Gap Railroad and went straight into action on the Union right. **Col. Jubal A. Early's** brigade (shifted from the Confederate right) and the brigade of **Brig. Gen. Edmund Kirby Smith** — Smith was wounded almost immediately upon arrival, and command passed to **Col. Arnold Elzey** — struck the Union flank at **Chinn Ridge**, crushing the exposed brigade of **Col. Oliver O. Howard**. With his right enveloped and his men exhausted, McDowell's line gave way. Beauregard ordered a general advance along the whole front.

**~4:30–5:00 p.m. onward — The rout ("The Great Skedaddle").** The Union withdrawal began in fair order but disintegrated into panic. At the **Cub Run** bridge on the route back to Centreville, Confederate artillery fire overturned a wagon, blocking the span and triggering a stampede. Soldiers threw away rifles, knapsacks, and ~25–27 cannon (including a 30-pounder Parrott). Compounding the chaos, scores of Washington civilians — congressmen, sightseers, picnickers in carriages who had come to watch the battle — clogged the roads in their own flight. McDowell could not rally his men short of the Washington defenses; the army streamed back into the capital by July 22. The Confederates, themselves disorganized and exhausted (and with ~14,000 troops never engaged), failed to mount an effective pursuit — a controversy that dogged Johnston and Beauregard afterward.

---

### Terrain Features and Why They Mattered

- **Bull Run (the creek):** A meandering stream with steep, wooded banks crossable only at fords and the Stone Bridge. It formed Beauregard's defensive line and channelized all movement, making the fords (Sudley, Blackburn's, Mitchell's, Union Mills, etc.) the operational keys.
- **Stone Bridge:** Carried the **Warrenton Turnpike** over Bull Run; anchor of the Confederate left and the focus of Tyler's fixing demonstration.
- **Sudley Springs Ford:** The upstream, lightly guarded crossing that enabled McDowell's flanking march — its remoteness (and the poor approach road) both made the turning movement possible and delayed it.
- **Matthews Hill:** First high ground south of Sudley; where Evans, Bee, and Bartow fought the morning delaying action before being overwhelmed.
- **Warrenton Turnpike & Young's Branch:** The valley the Confederates retreated across; the turnpike was both the battle's axis and the rout's escape route.
- **Henry House Hill:** The dominant plateau where the battle was decided — Jackson's reverse-slope defense, the artillery duel, the Henry House, and the climactic infantry fighting. Holding it gave the Confederates time for Valley reinforcements to arrive.
- **Chinn Ridge:** West of Henry Hill; where Early/Kirby Smith/Elzey turned the Union right and broke McDowell's army.
- **Manassas Junction:** The rail hub whose strategic value drew both armies and whose railroad (Manassas Gap RR) delivered Johnston's decisive reinforcements.
- **Cub Run:** The bottleneck on the retreat where the rout became a panic.

### Casualties (sources differ slightly — see confidence notes)

**Union (McDowell):**
- Killed: **460–481**
- Wounded: **~1,011–1,124**
- Captured/Missing: **~1,216–1,312**
- **Total: ~2,700–2,900** (Wikipedia ~2,708; American Battlefield Trust/CWSAC 2,896)
- ~25–27 guns lost.

**Confederate (Beauregard/Johnston):**
- Killed: **387**
- Wounded: **1,582**
- Missing: **13**
- **Total: 1,982** (consistent across nearly all sources — high confidence)

**Forces:** Of ~35,000 Union present McDowell committed roughly **18,000**; of ~32,000–34,000 Confederates present, roughly **18,000** were also engaged — a frequently cited near-parity. Casualty rates ran ~10% on each side — shockingly high to a public that had expected a bloodless, decisive contest, and a foreshadowing of the war's cost.

**Notable individual casualties:**
- Confederate: **Col. Francis S. Bartow** (Georgia) — among the first Confederate field commanders killed in the war; **Brig. Gen. Barnard E. Bee** — mortally wounded, died July 22; **Brig. Gen. Edmund Kirby Smith** — wounded at Chinn Ridge; **Maj. Roberdeau Wheat** — severely wounded (survived).
- Union: **Col. James Cameron** (79th New York Highlanders), brother of Secretary of War Simon Cameron — killed.
- Civilian: **Judith Carter Henry**, 85 — the only civilian killed; namesake of Henry Hill.

---

### Significance
The Confederate victory shattered Northern hopes of a quick war, made Jackson and Beauregard household names in the South, gave the Confederacy a (dangerously) inflated sense of superiority, and prompted Lincoln to replace McDowell with George B. McClellan and rebuild the army as the Army of the Potomac. It demonstrated the strategic power of railroads, the danger of green troops and convergent amateur planning, and that the war would be long and bloody.

(2) Exact wording and intent of Bee's "Stonewall" remark — genuinely uncertain due to Bee's death without a report. (3) Time of the very first shot (5:15–6:00 a.m.). (4) Whether ~25, 27, or "25 guns" were lost by the Union — sources cluster around 25–27.

## Weapons — Infantry Shoulder Arms (types, ranges, RoF)

# Civil War Infantry Shoulder Arms: Types, Ballistics & Tactical Reality

## Overview

The American Civil War (1861-1865) is often called the first "modern" war because of the widespread adoption of the **rifled musket** firing the **Minié ball** — a conical, hollow-base, expanding soft-lead projectile invented by French Capt. Claude-Étienne Minié (1849). The expanding skirt let a soldier ram an undersized bullet down a fouled rifled bore quickly, then grip the rifling on firing. This combined the loading speed of a smoothbore with the accuracy of a rifle. Pre-war doctrine assumed this would push effective infantry combat out to 400-500+ yards and render the close-order frontal assault obsolete. The reality, as documented by Earl J. Hess, Paddy Griffith, Mark Grimsley, and Brent Nosworthy, was far more modest — most Civil War firefights occurred at smoothbore ranges.

Roughly **90% of Civil War combat deaths** were caused by these shoulder arms (small-arms fire), not artillery. Muzzle-loading rifled muskets remained the dominant infantry weapon on both sides for the entire war; breechloaders and repeaters, though revolutionary, were issued mainly to cavalry, mounted infantry, and sharpshooters and never displaced the muzzle-loader as the standard infantry arm.

| Spec | Value |
|---|---|
| Caliber | .69 in |
| Type | Smoothbore percussion musket, muzzle-loading, single-shot |

The Springfield **Model 1842** was the last U.S. smoothbore and the first made entirely with interchangeable parts. Many older flintlock M1816s were converted to percussion. Notably, the Irish Brigade carried smoothbores into the close-quarters fighting at battles like Antietam. This weapon is central to the modern scholarly argument (Section 9): if the rifle musket's *real* effective range was ~100 yards, the smoothbore was nearly its equal in practice. (High confidence; range figures vary by source.)

## 9. The Rifle-Musket: Theoretical vs. Real Battlefield Range — and Tactical Implications

This is the most important and most contested point, and the one most relevant to an educational game.

**The traditional ("Minié-ball revolution") interpretation:** The rifled musket's 400-500+ yard accuracy should have made the war a slaughter, multiplying defensive firepower, dooming Napoleonic close-order assaults, and explaining the war's horrific casualties. This view dominated 20th-century writing (e.g., implicit in McWhiney & Jamieson's *Attack and Die*).

**The revisionist interpretation (Paddy Griffith, *Battle Tactics of the Civil War*, 1987; Mark Grimsley; Brent Nosworthy, *The Bloody Crucible of Courage*; and most rigorously Earl J. Hess, *The Rifle Musket in Civil War Combat: Reality and Myth*, Univ. Press of Kansas, 2008):**

- Despite a *theoretical/sighted* effective range of 300-500 yards, **actual Civil War firefights typically opened at ~100-150 yards.** Griffith's and Grimsley's sampling of engagements found **average firing ranges of roughly 116-127 yards** — essentially smoothbore range.
- Hess argues the rifle musket's "first killing zone" was **~100 yards — the same as a smoothbore musket** with buck-and-ball.
- **Why the gap between theory and reality:**
  1. **Parabolic (looping) trajectory.** The slow, heavy Minié ball arced. A rifle sighted for distant targets created **two killing zones** — roughly 0-100 yards and again ~250-350 yards as the ball descended — with a **"safe zone" of ~150-250 yards where bullets sailed over advancing troops' heads.** Soldiers were almost never trained to estimate range and adjust sights for this arc.
  2. **Minimal marksmanship training.** Most soldiers received little or no live-fire practice; volley fire by command, not aimed individual fire, was the norm.
  3. **Terrain and smoke.** Wooded, broken battlefields (the Wilderness, much of the Eastern theater) limited sightlines well under 200 yards; black-powder smoke obscured targets after the first volleys.
  4. **Soldier behavior.** Troops tended to hold fire until the enemy was close, then fire fast rather than slow and aimed.

**Tactical implications:**

- The expected obsolescence of the frontal assault did **not** fully materialize *because of the rifle alone*; assaults still sometimes succeeded, and the defensive advantage Hess and others credit grew more from **field fortifications, entrenchments, and massed fire at close range** than from long-range rifle accuracy.
- The rifle musket made combat **more lethal than smoothbore-era warfare but far less than the "revolution" thesis claimed** — Hess concludes the rifle musket was "only slightly more lethal than the smoothbore" under typical battlefield conditions.
- **Game-design takeaway:** Modeling Springfield/Enfield effective range at 300-500 yards reflects the *nominal/sighted* spec, but historically accurate *combat* behavior is firefights at ~100-150 yards. A defensible design splits "sighted/theoretical range" from "effective combat range," and gives smoothbores a genuine close-range (buck-and-ball) niche rather than making them strictly inferior.

(The revisionist scholarship is well-established and peer-reviewed but remains debated; the *direction* of the argument — real ranges far shorter than nominal — is high confidence, while exact average-range figures vary by study and should be cited as ~100-130 yards.)

## Weapons — Artillery (types, range by ammunition)

# Civil War Field Artillery — Types and Range by Ammunition

Civil War field artillery split into two broad families: **smoothbore** guns and howitzers (bronze, muzzle-loading, short-to-medium range, devastating with canister) and **rifled** guns (iron, longer range, far more accurate, but with weaker close-range anti-personnel performance). Each piece fired a menu of ammunition selected for the target and distance: **solid shot** (round shot for smoothbores; elongated "bolts" for rifles) for material destruction and counter-battery; **shell** (hollow, powder-filled, bursting) against fortifications and massed troops; **spherical case / case shot** (shrapnel) as an airburst anti-personnel round at medium range; and **canister** as the close-defense, last-ditch anti-infantry weapon.

A standard Federal field battery was nominally **six guns** served by crews of about **8-9 men per piece** (gunner, two loaders/handlers at the muzzle, men at the vent and lanyard, ammunition runners), with the rest of the ~150-man battery handling the limbers, caissons, and ~110 horses. A well-drilled crew sustained roughly **2 aimed rounds per minute**, and could surge to **3-4 rounds/min** with canister in an emergency at the cost of accuracy and barrel heat. Sources vary on "crew" counts (cannoneers at the gun vs. the full detachment), so treat 8-9 as the working figure.

---

## 1. 12-pdr Napoleon (Model 1857) — the workhorse smoothbore gun-howitzer

- **Bore:** 4.62 in | **Type:** bronze smoothbore, muzzle-loading | **Tube weight:** ~1,227 lb
- **Powder charge:** 2.5 lb | **Muzzle velocity:** ~1,440 ft/s | **Crew:** ~8-9 | **Rate of fire:** ~2/min sustained, up to 3-4/min
- **Solid shot:** ~1,440 ft/s muzzle velocity; effective ~**1,600-1,680 yd at 5°** (Wikipedia cites 1,619 yd; sources also give ~1,680 yd)
- **Shell:** comparable trajectory, slightly shorter; effective roughly **1,300 yd** range band against troops/works
- **Spherical case (shrapnel):** medium-range airburst, effective to roughly **500-1,000 yd**; a 12-pdr case round carried ~**78 musket balls** over a small bursting charge
- **Canister:** decisive to **~400 yd**, lethal and preferred under ~250 yd; **double canister** (two tins, single charge) at **under 100-150 yd**

The Napoleon (named for Napoleon III, who sponsored its French original) was the most-produced and most-respected field piece of the war. As a "gun-howitzer" it combined a gun's flat-trajectory solid-shot range with a howitzer's effective shell and canister fire, which is why it displaced the older separate 6-pdr gun and 12-pdr howitzer. **Confidence: high** on bore, type, charge, and solid-shot range; **medium** on the precise shell/case range bands, which varied by fuze, charge, and source.

## 2. 3-inch Ordnance Rifle (Model 1861) — the best field rifle

- **Bore:** 3.0 in | **Type:** wrought-iron rifled (banded/welded wrought iron), muzzle-loading | **Tube weight:** ~820 lb
- **Powder charge:** ~1 lb | **Crew:** ~8-9 | **Rate of fire:** ~2/min
- **Solid shot/bolt (~10 lb):** long-range, accurate; effective ~**1,830 yd at 5°**, max ~**4,180 yd at ~16°**
- **Shell / common shell (~8-9 lb):** prized for long-range accuracy; effective well past 1,800 yd, used to ~2,000+ yd
- **Spherical case / conical case:** highly accurate at long range against troops, effective to ~**1,500+ yd**
- **Canister:** effective to **~400 yd**, but less devastating than a Napoleon's because of the narrower 3-inch bore (fewer balls per tin) and the slower-to-clear rifling

Manufactured chiefly by Phoenix Iron Company of Phoenixville, Pennsylvania, the wrought-iron 3-inch was famous for **safety** (it almost never burst, unlike the cast-iron Parrotts) and **accuracy**. A Confederate artillerist's oft-quoted complaint was that the 3-inch was so accurate "the Yankees could hit the head of a flour barrel more often than miss it." Fired Hotchkiss, Schenkl, and Dyer projectiles. **Confidence: high** on bore/type/range.

## 3. 10-pdr Parrott Rifle (Model 1861/1863) — common rifled gun

- **Powder charge:** ~1 lb | **Crew:** ~8-9 | **Rate of fire:** ~2/min
- **Solid shot/bolt (~10 lb):** effective ~**1,850 yd at 5°**; max ~**5,000 yd** at high elevation
- **Shell:** long-range bombardment to several thousand yards
- **Spherical/conical case:** anti-personnel airburst at medium-long range

## 6. 14-pdr James Rifle — early-war bronze rifle (transitional)

- **Bore/caliber:** **3.80-3.82 in** | **Type:** bronze rifled (often reamed/rifled from M1841 6-pdr smoothbores), muzzle-loading | **Crew:** ~8-9 | **Rate of fire:** ~2/min
- **Solid shot (~14 lb projectile, "James shot"):** effective ~**1,530 yd at 5°**
- **Shell / common shell:** medium-long range bombardment
- **Canister:** close defense to ~400 yd

## 7. Whitworth Rifle (12-pdr) — the precision sniper of artillery

- **Bore:** **2.75 in, hexagonal cross-section** (not conventional rifling) | **Type:** steel/iron **breech-loading** (some muzzle-loading) rifle — exceptional at a time when nearly all field artillery was muzzle-loading | **Crew:** ~8-9
- **Projectile:** elongated hexagonal "bolt" of ~**12 lb 11 oz** that mechanically conformed to the twisted hexagonal bore
- **Solid shot/bolt:** extraordinary range and accuracy — reliably effective for **counter-battery to ~1,600 yd** ("at 1,600 yards, ten shots with a lateral deviation of only 5 inches," per an 1864 engineering account); demonstrated extreme max range of ~**5-6 miles** in 1860s English trials (Southport Sands, 1863, ~4.7 miles)
- **Shell:** long-range; relatively rare and prized
- **Canister:** poor / rarely used — the narrow hexagonal bore made it a long-range precision weapon, not a close-defense piece

British-made (Joseph Whitworth, Manchester) and imported by the **Confederacy** in small numbers; the rounds' distinctive **whistle** was noted by Union troops. Drawbacks: scarce ammunition, fouling in the hexagonal bore, slow breech mechanism, and the cost/rarity that made each gun precious. **Confidence: high** on the hexagonal bore, breech-loading, and accuracy reputation; **low/contested** on the most extreme "10,000-yard" range claims, which conflate proving-ground demonstrations with battlefield-effective range.

## Ammunition explained

**Canister — the close-defense weapon.** A canister round was a thin tin cylinder packed with dozens of cast-iron or lead balls in sawdust. On firing, the tin disintegrated at the muzzle and the balls fanned out **like a giant shotgun blast**. Effective to about **400 yd**, it was devastating under ~250 yd and was the gunner's answer to an infantry charge reaching the battery. In extremis, crews fired **double canister** (two tins on a single powder charge) at **under 100-150 yd**, turning a cannon into the largest shotgun on the field — the move that broke Confederate assaults at point-blank range (e.g., the climax of Pickett's Charge at Gettysburg). **Smoothbores like the Napoleon were superior canister weapons:** the wide bore held more balls and the un-rifled tube did not snag or strip the tin, so a Napoleon's canister blast was denser than that of a narrow rifled gun.

**Spherical case / shrapnel — medium-range airburst.** Invented by British officer **Henry Shrapnel** (1784), spherical case was a hollow shell packed with musket balls around a small bursting charge, fitted with a **timed fuze** cut to burst the shell **in the air, above and ahead of the enemy line**, raining the balls down. It extended the "shotgun" effect of canister from ~300 m out to ~1,100 m. Its great weakness was **fuze unreliability**: a fuze cut too long let the round bury itself before bursting; too short and it burst harmlessly high. A 12-pdr Napoleon case round held ~78 balls.

**Solid shot.** Round shot (smoothbore) or elongated "bolts" (rifles) for smashing wagons, guns, and works, and for **ricochet/grazing fire** that bounced round shot through troop columns. Round shot ricocheted well across firm ground or water, extending its lethal depth; rifled bolts tended to bury themselves on impact and ricochet poorly.

**Shell.** Hollow, powder-filled, fuze-burst projectiles for fortifications and massed targets; the rifled guns' long range made them the bombardment/counter-battery rounds of choice.

---

## Rifled vs. smoothbore tradeoffs

- **Rifled guns (3-inch Ordnance, Parrotts, James, Whitworth):** far greater **range and accuracy**, ideal for counter-battery and long-range bombardment with shot, shell, and case. The 3-inch Ordnance Rifle was the gold standard for safety and precision; Parrotts were cheaper but **prone to bursting**; the Whitworth was a precision specialist hobbled by scarce ammunition.
- **Smoothbores (Napoleon, 12-pdr howitzer):** shorter range and less accurate at distance, **but superior at close range with canister** — the wide bore delivered a denser, deadlier blast, and round shot ricocheted effectively. The bronze Napoleon was also **safer and more durable** than cast-iron rifles and dominated close, decisive fighting in the broken terrain of many Eastern battles.

Net effect: most batteries mixed types, or the army fielded rifled batteries for long-range work and smoothbore (Napoleon) batteries for the close, terrain-limited fights where canister decided the day.

## Weapons — Cavalry Arms & Doctrine

# Civil War Cavalry: Arms, Carbines & Doctrine

Civil War cavalry was a weapon system in transition. It entered the war as a 19th-century arm built around the saber, the revolver, and the romantic mounted charge; it ended the war as something closer to modern mobile infantry — fast-moving columns that rode to the fight and dismounted to deliver fire from breech-loading and repeating carbines. Understanding the trooper's three-weapon kit (saber, revolver, carbine) and how doctrine shifted around it is the key to representing cavalry accurately in a strategy game.

---

## 1. The Carbines — The Decisive Weapon Class

The carbine was the cavalry's primary combat arm because it was short, light, and breech-loading, which a trooper could load and fire from horseback or, more usefully, dismounted. Breech-loading let the cavalryman reload lying down or behind cover — impossible with a muzzle-loader. This is the single most important technical fact about Civil War cavalry: **the carbine, not the saber, made cavalry militarily relevant by 1863.**

### Sharps Carbine (M1859 / M1863) — the workhorse
- **Action:** Falling-block, breech-loading, single-shot; combustible linen or paper cartridge ignited by a percussion cap or the Sharps pellet-primer feed.
- **Caliber:** .52 (fired a ~475-grain projectile).
- **Barrel:** ~22 in; weight ~8 lb.
- **Rate of fire:** roughly 8–10 aimed shots/minute (vs. 2–3 for a muzzle-loading rifle-musket).
- **Effective range:** generally cited ~300–500 yards for combat use (the rifle version was capable to far longer ranges in expert hands; carbine claims of "1,000 yards" reflect the rifle/marksman context, not realistic mounted-combat use — **treat long-range figures with caution**).
- **Production / cost:** roughly 80,000–90,000 carbines during the war; ~$30 unit cost in 1861. By 1863 it was the most common weapon carried by Union cavalry. Popular with both armies.

### Burnside Carbine — the third-most-issued
- **Action:** Single-shot, breech-loading, lever-actuated; fired a distinctive tapered .54 brass cartridge that sealed the breech against gas leakage. Designed by Ambrose Burnside (later the general).
- **Caliber:** .54 (Burnside cartridge); muzzle velocity ~950 ft/s.
- **Barrel:** 21 in; overall 39.5 in; ~7 lb.
- **Effective range:** ~200 yards (commonly cited).
- **Production:** ~55,000+ ordered for Union cavalry during the war (total production figures of "~100,000" span the full 1858–1870 production run). Third most popular carbine after Sharps and Spencer.

### Smith Carbine — the rubber-cartridge breechloader
- **Action:** Single-shot break-action (hinged in the middle, like a shotgun); used a self-contained **rubber (or foil/paper) cartridge** to seal gases.
- **Caliber:** .50; ~402-grain bullet, ~41-grain powder charge, ~950 ft/s.
- **Dimensions:** 39.5 in overall, 21.6 in barrel, ~7.5 lb.
- **Production:** roughly 30,000+ purchased by the U.S. during the war.
- **Weakness:** the cartridge cases fouled the chamber badly, jamming the gun after a few dozen rounds until cleaned — a meaningful reliability drawback worth modeling.

### Spencer Repeating Carbine (M1860 / M1865) — the game-changer
- **Action:** Lever-action **repeater** feeding from a **7-round tubular magazine in the buttstock**; self-contained **.56-56 Spencer rimfire metallic cartridge** (~.52 bullet, ~45 gr powder). Hammer still cocked manually between shots.
- **Dimensions:** carbine ~39.25 in overall, 22-in barrel.
- **Rate of fire:** ~14–20 rounds/minute; a full magazine (7 rounds) could be emptied in roughly 15–20 seconds. The **Blakeslee cartridge box** (tubes of 7 pre-loaded rounds each — boxes held 6, 10, or 13 tubes) let a trooper reload a full magazine in seconds, sustaining the rate of fire.
- **Effective range:** ~500 yards; muzzle velocity ~930–1,030 ft/s.
- **Production:** ~200,000 across all models, 1860–1874 (rifle + carbine); ~$40 unit cost.
- **Crucial limitation for the Confederacy:** the self-contained metallic rimfire cartridge was beyond Southern industrial capacity to manufacture, so captured Spencers were near-useless to Confederate troopers once their captured ammunition ran out. This is why repeaters were an almost exclusively **Union** advantage.

**Other carbines** in service (lesser numbers): Maynard (.50, tape-primer), Starr, Gallager, Joslyn, Merrill, Cosmopolitan, Sharps & Hankins. Confederate cavalry, chronically short of proper carbines, frequently substituted **sawed-off shotguns** and muzzle-loading musketoons — lethal at close range but badly outranged.

---

## 2. Sabers

- **Model 1840 "Heavy Cavalry Saber":** ~44 in overall, ~35-in curved blade, ~2.5 lb, brass guard, leather grip, steel scabbard. Heavy enough to earn the nickname **"Old Wrist-Breaker."** Considered the superior cutting weapon by some, but unwieldy.
- **Model 1860 "Light Cavalry Saber":** ~41 in overall, 35×1-in blade, ~2 lb 4 oz (3 lb 10 oz with scabbard). Developed in response to complaints about the 1840's weight; smaller, more maneuverable, and the standard issue for the rest of the war.

Doctrinally the saber retained huge symbolic and morale value and was decisive in **cavalry-vs-cavalry melee** (the swirling mounted fights at Brandy Station and on Gettysburg's East Cavalry Field). Against formed infantry it was nearly useless. Many practical officers — Forrest among Confederates, and increasingly Union commanders — preferred the revolver to the saber in the close fight.

---

## 3. Revolvers

The standard cavalry sidearm was a **.44-caliber, 6-shot, single-action percussion (cap-and-ball) revolver:**
- **Colt Model 1860 Army:** .44, 6-shot, 8-in barrel, ~2 lb 11 oz, ~14 in overall; effective range ~50–75 yards. ~200,000+ produced 1860–1873, the dominant Union sidearm.
- **Remington New Model Army (1858/1863):** .44, 6-shot, solid **top-strap frame** (sturdier than the Colt's open-top), allowing fast cylinder swaps for quicker reloading. ~122,000+ produced; ~$12 each. Many troopers considered it the more robust, safer revolver (hammer safety notches between chambers).

Reloading a cap-and-ball revolver in combat was impractical (loose powder, ball, and caps per chamber), so troopers often carried **two or more revolvers** and emptied them in succession during a charge — the basis of the **"revolver charge"** that men like Forrest and Mosby favored over the saber.

---

## 4. Cavalry Doctrine & Roles

Civil War cavalry performed roughly six functions, in rough order of importance:

1. **Reconnaissance / scouting** — the "eyes and ears" of the army; finding the enemy's location, strength, and movements. The most strategically vital role.
2. **Screening** — masking one's own army's movements and positions from enemy scouts (the reciprocal of reconnaissance). Stuart's failure to screen/report during the Gettysburg Campaign left Lee "blind."
3. **Flank security & pickets** — guarding the army's flanks, rear, and lines of communication.
4. **Raiding / interdiction** — deep strikes against railroads, supply depots, and communications (Grierson's Raid, Morgan's raids, Stuart's rides, Sheridan's 1864 raids, Wilson's 1865 raids).
5. **Dismounted combat (the "carbine line")** — fighting on foot as mobile infantry; the dominant combat mode by mid-war.
6. **The mounted charge** — saber/revolver shock action, used mainly against other cavalry, broken/retreating infantry, or artillery — rarely against formed infantry.

### The "Carbine Line" / Dismounted Tactics (the central late-war innovation)
Cavalry used mobility to reach the decisive point, then **dismounted the majority of the force to fight on foot** with carbines. **Every fourth trooper became a "horse-holder,"** leading four mounts to the rear while the other three fought in a skirmish/firing line. This sacrificed ~25% of firepower for the ability to relocate the whole force rapidly. Breech-loaders and especially Spencers gave a dismounted cavalry line firepower that could punch well above its numbers — the "force-multiplier" of the repeating carbine.

### Why Cavalry Rarely Charged Formed Infantry
The conventional explanation is the **rifled musket** (effective range ~300–500 yards vs. ~100 for the smoothbore), which supposedly made closing the gap suicidal. This is the standard textbook reason and is **partially true but contested**: historian **Earl J. Hess** argues infantry had reliably repelled cavalry charges with disciplined volley fire for over a century before rifling, and that the deeper reason is that **the U.S. had no tradition of heavy "shock" cavalry**, scant cavalry-vs-infantry training, and an ethos that favored mobility and firepower over the charge. Either way, the empirical record holds: well-ordered infantry in formation almost always broke a mounted charge, and successful charges came against cavalry, disordered/fleeing infantry, or guns. (Mark this causal debate as **contested**, the fact that charges against formed infantry usually failed as **established**.)

---

## 5. The War's Arc — Confederate Early Edge, Union Late Dominance

### Confederate ascendancy (1861–1862)
The South began with major cavalry advantages: a rural, horse-borne culture; men who owned and rode their own mounts; and a disproportionate share of experienced officers (over half of the antebellum Regular cavalry officers — ~104 of 176 — were Southerners, many of whom resigned for the Confederacy). 
- **J.E.B. Stuart** dominated the Eastern Theater, famously **riding completely around the Army of the Potomac** twice in 1862 (Peninsula and after Antietam) — dazzling for morale and reconnaissance, if of limited lasting strategic value.
- **Nathan Bedford Forrest** ("The Wizard of the Saddle") and **John Hunt Morgan** ran rampant in the Western Theater with raids and aggressive mounted-infantry tactics.

### The turning point — Brandy Station, June 9, 1863
The largest cavalry battle ever fought in North America (~20,500 troopers engaged). Though tactically a draw, it was the moment **Union cavalry under Alfred Pleasonton proved it could stand toe-to-toe with Stuart's vaunted horsemen** — the psychological pivot of the war's cavalry story.

### Union professionalization (1863–1865)
- **Organizational reform:** Hooker consolidated the Army of the Potomac's cavalry into a single **Cavalry Corps** (Feb. 1863) under George Stoneman. The **Cavalry Bureau** (est. July 1863) centralized remounts via depots like **Giesboro Point** (handling ~30,000 horses), giving the Union a sustainable horse-supply system the Confederacy never had.
- **John Buford at Gettysburg, July 1, 1863:** Buford's **1st Cavalry Division** fought a textbook **dismounted delaying action** west of town (Herr Ridge / McPherson's Ridge), one trooper in four holding horses, using fast-firing carbines to slow Heth's Confederate infantry (Archer's and Davis's brigades, contact ~7:30 a.m.) long enough for Reynolds and the I Corps to reach and hold the high ground. (Note: contemporary accounts and Buford's report make clear most of his troopers carried **single-shot breech-loaders**, not Spencers — the "Buford's men had repeaters" claim is a popular overstatement; **medium-low confidence on widespread Spencer use** in his division specifically.)
- **Wilder's "Lightning Brigade":** Col. John T. Wilder's **mounted infantry** brigade (incl. 17th & 72nd Indiana, 98th & 123rd Illinois) privately financed and self-armed with **Spencer repeating rifles** (each man pledging ~$35). At **Hoover's Gap (June 24, 1863)** during the Tullahoma Campaign, their firepower routed Confederate defenders and demonstrated the repeating rifle's battlefield value a week before Gettysburg.
- **Sheridan's offensive cavalry (1864):** Philip H. Sheridan took the Cavalry Corps in 1864 and reconceived it as an independent **mounted strike force**, not just the army's scouts. His raid toward Richmond brought on **Yellow Tavern (May 11, 1864)**, where **J.E.B. Stuart was mortally wounded** (shot with a pistol by a dismounted Michigan trooper) and died the next day — a blow to Lee comparable to Jackson's loss. **Trevilian Station (June 1864)** was the largest all-cavalry battle of the war (~16,000 engaged).
- **James H. Wilson** standardized Spencer issue as Cavalry Bureau chief and led massive late-war raids (Selma/Alabama, 1865), fielding thousands of Spencer-armed mounted troopers.

### Confederate decline (1864–1865)
The South's cavalry deteriorated for structural reasons:
- **Horses:** Confederate troopers supplied their **own** mounts with no replacement system — a dismounted trooper faced furlough or transfer to infantry. Union occupation of prime horse country (western Virginia, Kentucky, Missouri) and soaring horse prices (toward $3,000+ in inflated Confederate currency) made remounting impossible.
- **Weapons:** Chronic carbine shortage forced reliance on shotguns and captured arms; Confederates **could not manufacture the metallic cartridges** for captured Spencers/Burnsides.
- **Leadership/numbers:** Stuart's death and Union numerical and qualitative growth ended the South's ability to roam at will. Wade Hampton III succeeded Stuart and was arguably more methodical, but the material balance had decisively shifted.

### Forrest's mounted-infantry model
Forrest distilled his doctrine as **"Get there first with the most men"** (the famous "fustest with the mostest" is a journalistic misquote). He used horses purely for mobility — riding to the field, tying off mounts, and fighting as **dismounted infantry** with combined-arms support (he often pushed artillery forward aggressively). His emphasis on speed, surprise, flanking ("hit 'em on the end"), and dismounted firepower prefigured 20th-century mobile/mechanized warfare. (Forrest is also infamous for the **Fort Pillow massacre**, April 1864 — relevant to his historical profile, not his tactics.)

## 6. Organization & Numbers (for game scaling)
- **Union:** ~258 mounted regiments + 170 unattached companies; ~14% of the Union Army was cavalry. A regiment was authorized ~1,040–1,156 men (12 troops/companies; ~96–100 men each) but rarely fielded that. Cavalry losses: ~10,596 killed, ~26,490 wounded.
- **Confederate:** ~137 regiments, 1 legion, 143 separate battalions, 101 separate companies; ~20% of soldiers were mounted. Regiments of ~10 companies, often fielding only 160–360 effectives.
- **Mobility benchmark:** ~35 miles in an 8-hour day without exhausting horse or rider; daily forage ~10 lb hay + ~14 lb grain per horse. Raids pushed far beyond (Stuart's Chambersburg raid ~80 miles in ~27 hours).

### Confidence notes
- **High confidence:** weapon types/calibers/roles; the dismounted "horse-holder" 4:1 system; Brandy Station as turning point; Stuart killed at Yellow Tavern 5/11/1864; Confederate inability to produce metallic cartridges; Wilder's brigade self-arming with Spencers; Buford's dismounted delaying action at Gettysburg.
- **Medium/low confidence (treat with care):** exact "effective range" figures (sources conflate carbine vs. rifle and ideal vs. combat ranges — long-range Sharps/Spencer claims should be discounted for mounted combat); precise production totals (ranges given because Civil-War-only vs. full-production-run figures differ); the claim that Buford's division was broadly armed with Spencers (likely overstated — mostly single-shot breechloaders).
- **Contested (scholarly debate):** whether the rifled musket is the true reason cavalry didn't charge formed infantry — Earl J. Hess argues the cause is the absence of a U.S. heavy-cavalry tradition, not rifling.

## USCT — Formation, Structure, Scale

## United States Colored Troops (USCT): Formation, Structure, and Scale

### Overview and Significance

The United States Colored Troops (USCT) constituted the formally organized regiments of African American soldiers raised by the U.S. War Department during the American Civil War. By the war's end, roughly **178,000–180,000 Black soldiers** had served in the U.S. Army (the most commonly cited figure is ~179,000), plus an additional ~7,000 white officers, for a combined total of more than **185,000 men** in USCT formations. Counting an additional ~19,000 African Americans who served in the U.S. Navy (which was integrated and never used the USCT designation), total Black military service approaches 200,000. USCT men served in roughly **160–175 regiments**, fought in approximately **449 engagements** (about 39 of them major battles), and by 1865 comprised roughly **one-tenth (10%) of the total Union Army**. Frederick Douglass framed enlistment as the path to citizenship: "Once let the black man get upon his person the brass letters U.S. ... there is no power on the earth ... which can deny that he has earned the right of citizenship."

### Origins and the Path to Enlistment (1861–1863)

Black enlistment was legally and politically blocked early in the war by an 1820s militia-law tradition and by Lincoln's caution toward the loyal border states. The legal scaffolding was built incrementally:
- **First Confiscation Act** (August 1861) — allowed seizure of property, including enslaved people, used to aid the rebellion.
- **Second Confiscation Act and Militia Act** (July 1862) — authorized the President to employ "persons of African descent" for military service.
- **Emancipation Proclamation** (effective January 1, 1863) — explicitly authorized the enlistment of Black men into the armed forces, transforming the war into one of liberation and providing the manpower rationale for mass recruitment.

Several pioneering Black units predated formal federal organization:
- **1st Louisiana Native Guard** — Maj. Gen. Benjamin F. Butler issued a recruitment order on **August 22, 1862**; the regiment was mustered **September 27, 1862**, becoming the first Black regiment officially mustered into U.S. service. It was drawn largely from New Orleans's "free men of color." Notably, it initially included some Black line officers (e.g., Capt. André Cailloux, killed at Port Hudson; Maj. Francis E. Dumas of the 2nd Regiment) — a rare exception soon eliminated as the army standardized white-officer policy. The Native Guards distinguished themselves at the **Siege of Port Hudson (May–July 1863)**, the first major assault by Black troops. They were later folded into the **Corps d'Afrique** and then renumbered as USCT regiments.
- **1st South Carolina Volunteers** (later 33rd USCI), organized under Gen. Rufus Saxton, led by Col. Thomas Wentworth Higginson.
- **1st Kansas Colored Infantry** (later 79th USCI), which fought at **Island Mound, Missouri (October 28–29, 1862)** — often cited as the first Civil War combat by a Black unit.
- **54th and 55th Massachusetts** — state regiments raised in early 1863 (the 54th organized March 13, 1863; mustered into federal service May 13, 1863), recruited nationally with the help of abolitionists. These retained their state designations and are discussed below.

### The Bureau of Colored Troops and General Order No. 143 (1863)

The War Department issued **General Order No. 143 on May 22, 1863**, establishing the **Bureau of Colored Troops** within the Adjutant General's Office to centralize and standardize all matters relating to the organization of Black regiments — recruitment, uniforms, equipment, training, and especially officer commissioning. 
- **Maj. Charles W. Foster** served as chief of the Bureau.
- It reported to **Adjutant General Brig. Gen. Lorenzo Thomas**, who was personally dispatched by Secretary of War **Edwin M. Stanton** to the Mississippi Valley in March 1863 to recruit freedmen from contraband camps and abandoned plantations. Thomas's campaign was extraordinarily productive, yielding **as many as 70 Black regiments** — the bulk of USCT manpower came from the formerly enslaved population of the occupied South rather than the free Black North.
- General Order 143 directed that regiments be **"numbered seriatim, in the order in which they are raised,"** under the Adjutant General — creating the standardized federal numbering that distinguished the USCT from state volunteer regiments.

### Unit Organization and Structure

The USCT followed the standard Union table of organization: companies → regiments → brigades → divisions → corps. Regiments were the core building block, organized by branch under federal designations:
- **United States Colored Infantry (USCI)** — by far the largest component, roughly **135–144 numbered infantry regiments** (the single largest branch).
- **United States Colored Cavalry (USCC)** — approximately **6–7 regiments**.
- **United States Colored Heavy Artillery (USCHA)** — roughly **12–13 regiments** (large units used for garrison/fortification duty).
- **United States Colored Light Artillery (USCLA)** — about **1 regiment plus an independent battery**.
- **Engineers** — a small number of engineer regiments.

Some regiments **retained their original state designations** rather than converting to federal numbers, including the **54th Massachusetts**, **55th Massachusetts**, **5th Massachusetts Cavalry (Colored)**, and **29th Connecticut Infantry (Colored)**. Most early units were **renumbered into the USCT system**: e.g., 1st South Carolina → **33rd USCI**; 2nd North Carolina → **36th USCI**; 1st Kansas → **79th USCI**; the Louisiana Corps d'Afrique regiments were renumbered into the federal sequence.

At the highest level of organization, the **XXV Corps** (formed December 1864, Army of the James) became the **only army corps in U.S. history composed primarily of Black troops**, comprising three divisions and representing the largest concentration of African American soldiers in the war. Earlier, **Brig. Gen. Edward Ferrero's 4th Division of the IX Corps** was an all-USCT division (see below).

### White Officers and the Politics of Command

The Bureau's defining policy — pressed by Stanton — was that **commissioned officers would be white**. The rationale was a mix of contemporary racism, a belief that white officers would lend the units "legitimacy" and discipline, and political caution. The narrow exceptions were **Black chaplains and surgeons** (non-combat commissions). By war's end only about **100–110 African Americans held commissions**, almost all as chaplains or surgeons; the highest-profile combat-related commission was **Maj. Martin R. Delany** (commissioned February 1865, the first Black field officer of infantry line rank). The **2nd Kansas Colored / Independent Battery US Colored Light Artillery (Kansas)** is sometimes cited as the rare unit largely officered by Black men.

Because the USCT created thousands of officer vacancies, the Army instituted a formal **officer examination system**:
- A **Board of Examiners** chaired permanently by **Maj. Gen. Silas Casey** (author of the army's infantry tactics manual) examined all applicants for USCT commissions regardless of prior rank — a meritocratic departure from the patronage-based volunteer system, intended to ensure competent leadership of the new regiments.
- The **Free Military School for Applicants for the Command of Colored Troops** opened at **1210 Chestnut Street, Philadelphia, in December 1863**, run by the Supervisory Committee for Recruiting Colored Regiments under chief preceptor **Col. John H. Taggart**. It prepared candidates (tactics, regulations, math, history) to pass the Casey Board, then commissioned graduates from captain to colonel.

For ambitious white volunteers, USCT service offered rapid promotion (a sergeant could become a captain or colonel), which helped overcome reluctance to lead Black troops; Col. **Robert Gould Shaw** of the 54th Massachusetts is the most famous example.

### Pay Inequality and the 1864 Equalization

Despite enlisting on the promise of equal treatment, Black soldiers were paid under the **Militia Act of 1862**: **$10 per month, minus a $3 clothing deduction (net $7)**, versus **$13 per month plus a clothing allowance** for white soldiers. In protest, the **54th and 55th Massachusetts refused all pay for roughly 18 months**, declining even Massachusetts's offer to make up the difference because accepting it would concede the principle of inequality. 
- **Congress equalized pay in June 1864** (commonly dated to the act of June 15 / signed/operative figures cited as **September 28, 1864**), retroactive to **January 1, 1864** for all, but full retroactive back pay to enlistment was limited to men who had been **free as of April 19, 1861** (the "Quaker oath" workaround let some former slaves swear they "owed no man unrequited labor").
- The **Enrollment Act amendment of March 3, 1865** finally granted full and equal back pay to all Black soldiers regardless of prior status. The 54th was paid in full in **September 1864**.

### Combat Record and Notable Engagements

- **Port Hudson, LA (May 27, 1863)** — Louisiana Native Guards in one of the first major assaults by Black troops.
- **Milliken's Bend, LA (June 7, 1863)** — newly raised, barely trained Black regiments held against a Confederate attack in a brutal hand-to-hand fight, noted as among the longest bayonet engagements of the war; a key early proof of Black soldiers' fighting capacity.
- **Fort Wagner, SC (July 18, 1863)** — the **54th Massachusetts**, led by **Col. Robert Gould Shaw** (killed and buried with his men by the Confederates as an intended insult), led the doomed but heroic assault. The 54th suffered roughly **40% casualties** (commonly cited as ~270 of ~600 engaged: ~20+ killed, ~125 wounded, ~100 missing/captured; Shaw plus 272 total Union dead in the broader assault). **Sgt. William H. Carney**, who saved the flag, became the first African American to perform the deed for which he later received the **Medal of Honor**.
- **Fort Pillow, TN (April 12, 1864)** — Confederate troops under **Maj. Gen. Nathan Bedford Forrest** overran the garrison (~557–600 men, including ~262 Black soldiers); many surrendering Black soldiers were killed. Black units suffered roughly **64% killed** versus far lower white losses. "Remember Fort Pillow!" became a USCT battle cry; the episode is widely characterized as a massacre.
- **New Market Heights / Chaffin's Farm, VA (September 29, 1864)** — two USCT brigades carried fortified Confederate lines outside Richmond; **14 of the ~16–18 USCT Medals of Honor** were earned here. Recipients included **Christian Fleetwood (4th USCI)**, **Powhatan Beaty (5th USCI)**, **Edward Ratcliff (38th USCI)**, **James Gardiner** and **Miles James (36th USCI)**, and **Charles Veal (4th USCI)**.
- **Battle of the Crater, Petersburg, VA (July 30, 1864)** — **Brig. Gen. Edward Ferrero's 4th Division, IX Corps**, an all-USCT division, had trained specifically to spearhead the assault after the mine explosion. On the eve of battle, **Gen. George G. Meade** (backed by **Gen. Grant**) ordered **Maj. Gen. Ambrose Burnside** to substitute a white division to lead, partly to avoid the political appearance of sacrificing Black troops. Ferrero's division attacked late, became trapped in and around the crater, and suffered heavy casualties; many were killed after attempting to surrender. Ferrero's two brigades: the **27th, 30th, 39th, and 43rd USCT** (1st Brigade, Lt. Col. Joshua K. Sigfried) and the **19th, 23rd, 28th, 29th, and 31st USCT** (2nd Brigade, Col. Henry Goddard Thomas). *(Corrected at D465 per the D459 adversarial pass and the OR-derived Union order of battle: the prior "four companies of the 30th [31st] Connecticut" line was wrong — the four-company 30th Connecticut had been consolidated into the 31st USCT before the battle; the old moderate-confidence flag is discharged.)*
- **Battle of Nashville, TN (December 15–16, 1864)** — USCT regiments fought prominently. Also **Petersburg, Richmond, Fort Fisher, and the Appomattox campaign**, where USCT units were among the first to occupy Richmond in April 1865.

### Casualties and Recognition

- Approximately **38,000–40,000+ USCT soldiers died** (one widely cited figure is ~40,000; combat deaths were comparatively few — about **2,751 killed in action** — with the overwhelming majority dying of **disease**, reflecting poor camp conditions, garrison/fatigue assignments, and limited medical care).
- The mortality rate (~**20%** of enrolled men) ran roughly **35% higher** than that of white Union troops (~15.2%).
- **Medal of Honor**: approximately **16–18 USCT Army soldiers** received it (sources vary between 16 and 18; an additional handful of Black sailors received the Navy Medal of Honor). The first were awarded for New Market Heights (action September 29, 1864; medals awarded April 6, 1865).

### Confederate Policy and Federal Retaliation

The Confederacy refused to treat captured Black soldiers as legitimate prisoners of war. The Confederate War Department's **General Order No. 60 (August 21, 1862)** and later Confederate Congress resolutions threatened to **enslave or execute captured Black soldiers and execute their white officers**. In response, Lincoln issued the **Order of Retaliation (General Order No. 252, July 30, 1863)**, pledging to execute one Confederate prisoner for every USCT soldier murdered and to sentence one to hard labor for every one enslaved. This standoff also helped stall prisoner exchanges in 1863–1864.

### Recruitment Geography and Composition

USCT soldiers came from every Union and border state and the occupied Confederacy. The largest contributions came from heavily enslaved states once Union armies penetrated them — **Louisiana, Kentucky, Tennessee, and Mississippi** were major sources — meaning the majority of USCT soldiers were **formerly enslaved freedmen** rather than free-born Northern Black men. Recruitment served dual purposes: filling Union ranks amid declining white enlistment (and the unpopular 1863 draft) and advancing emancipation by drawing enslaved men into U.S. service. By the fall of 1865, in the occupation South, Black regiments reportedly made up **as much as one-third of the Union forces** still in service, as white volunteers mustered out faster.

- **Medium confidence**: exact total regiment counts by branch (sources range 160–175+ total; ~135–144 infantry); exact Medal of Honor count (16 vs. 18); exact regimental composition of Ferrero's brigades; precise total death figure (38,000–43,000).

## USCT — Battles, Valor & Historiographical Criticism

## United States Colored Troops (USCT): Battles, Valor, and Historiographical Criticism

### Overview and Foundational Facts

The United States Colored Troops (USCT) was the segregated branch of the U.S. Army organized to administer African American regiments. The **Bureau of Colored Troops** was established within the War Department by **General Order No. 143 on May 22, 1863**. The same order codified the policy that USCT regiments would be commanded by **white commissioned officers** (the bureau screened officer applicants for competence, partly to counter accusations that Black units would fail).

By war's end, roughly **179,000–180,000 African American men** served as soldiers (about **10% of the Union Army**), plus approximately **19,000 in the U.S. Navy** (a non-segregated service). USCT regiments numbered roughly **175** by the end of the war. Total Black military deaths approached **~40,000**, of which the large majority — on the order of **~30,000 — died of disease and infection** rather than combat. USCT soldiers died at a rate roughly **35% higher** than white Union troops, attributable largely to poor camp conditions, inferior medical care, and frequent assignment to fatigue/labor duty. **Sixteen Black soldiers earned the Medal of Honor** during the war (additional sailors and a later total are sometimes cited; the figure of 16 soldiers is standard for army recipients during the conflict).

---

### The Battles

#### 1. Port Hudson, Louisiana — May 27, 1863
During the Union assault on the Confederate works at Port Hudson (part of the broader May–July 1863 siege), the **1st and 3rd Louisiana Native Guard** advanced on the extreme right of the Union line. The **1st Native Guard** included free Black line officers from New Orleans; the **3rd** was composed largely of former slaves under white officers. **Captain André Cailloux**, a free Black officer who reportedly boasted of being the "blackest man in New Orleans," led his company forward shouting commands in French and English until killed by artillery fire; his July 29, 1863 funeral became a major public commemoration. The assaults failed against strong works, but the conduct of the Native Guards was among the **first widely publicized demonstrations** that Black troops would fight under fire. *(Note: casualty tallies vary by source and conflate the May 27 assault with later attacks; specific Native Guard losses are reported in ranges and should be treated with moderate confidence.)*

#### 2. Milliken's Bend, Louisiana — June 7, 1863
A Confederate brigade under **Brig. Gen. Henry E. McCulloch** attacked a Union garrison defending a supply point during the Vicksburg Campaign. The defenders were the largely raw **"African Brigade"** — the **9th and 11th Louisiana Infantry (African Descent)** and the **1st Mississippi (African Descent)**, mostly recently freed slaves — supported by white troops and Union gunboats. The fighting was brutal hand-to-hand combat. Total Union losses were about **492 (119 killed, 241 wounded, 132 missing)**; the overwhelming majority were USCT. The **9th Louisiana suffered the highest casualty percentage of any Black regiment in the war** (commonly cited as ~45% killed-and-mortally-wounded, with some sources giving up to ~68% total strength loss). Confederate losses were roughly **185**. Assistant Secretary of War Charles A. Dana reported the bravery of the Black troops to Washington, reinforcing the case for Black enlistment.

#### 3. Fort Wagner (Second Assault), Morris Island, S.C. — July 18, 1863
The **54th Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry** — the North's celebrated free-Black regiment — led the doomed evening assault on Battery/Fort Wagner. Its commander, **Col. Robert Gould Shaw**, was killed atop the parapet shouting "Forward, Fifty-Fourth!" and was buried by the Confederates in a mass grave with his men (intended as an insult, later embraced as honor). **Sgt. William H. Carney** retrieved the national colors after the bearer fell, was wounded multiple times, and famously declared "the old flag never touched the ground" — for which he became the **first African American to be cited for Medal of Honor–worthy action** (his medal was formally awarded in 1900). The 54th lost about **281 of ~600 engaged** (roughly 54 killed/mortally wounded, dozens captured/missing). Total Union casualties in the assault were approximately **1,515**. The battle, though a tactical defeat, became the most famous symbol of Black valor (basis of the 1989 film *Glory*).

#### 4. Olustee (Ocean Pond), Florida — February 20, 1864
Florida's largest battle and a clear Confederate victory. Three Black regiments fought: the **54th Massachusetts**, the **8th USCT** (mostly free Black Pennsylvanians), and the **35th USCT** (formerly the **1st North Carolina Colored**, mainly ex-slaves). The green **8th USCT** was thrown in piecemeal and shattered, losing **310 of 565 engaged — 49 killed, 188 wounded, 73 missing** (ABT's explicit breakdown; corrected at D465 per the D459 adversarial pass — the prior "≈87 killed" figure was unverified and dies); its commander, **Col. Charles W. Fribley, was killed**. The 54th and 35th fought a disciplined rearguard that prevented a Union rout — Union commander **Brig. Gen. Truman Seymour** praised them as fighting "like veterans." Total Union casualties were about **1,861 (203 killed, 1,152 wounded, 506 missing)** of ~5,500 engaged. Reports indicate wounded Black soldiers left on the field were murdered by Confederates afterward.

#### 5. The Crater, Petersburg, Virginia — July 30, 1864
After Union miners detonated a massive charge under Confederate works (the blast killed ~278–352 Confederates and created a crater ~170 ft long), the assault collapsed into chaos. **Brig. Gen. Edward Ferrero's 4th Division (USCT)** of the IX Corps had trained to lead the attack, but **Gen. George G. Meade — endorsed by Gen. Grant — ordered a white division to go first** the day before, fearing political fallout if Black troops were "sacrificed" on a forlorn hope. The USCT division went in last, into the deadly bowl. Confederates under **Brig. Gen. William Mahone** counterattacked; enraged by Black soldiers and cries of "No Quarter! Remember Fort Pillow!", many Confederates **massacred surrendering and wounded USCT soldiers** — estimates of those killed after attempting to surrender run from **~200 into the hundreds** (figures are contested). Total Union casualties were **3,798 (504 killed, 1,881 wounded, 1,413 missing/captured)**; Confederate **1,491**. Grant called it "the saddest affair I have witnessed in this war."

#### 6. New Market Heights / Chaffin's Farm, Virginia — September 29, 1864
USCT brigades of the **XVIII Corps**, under the overall command of **Maj. Gen. Benjamin F. Butler**, assaulted entrenched Confederate works north of the James River outside Richmond. The attack fell on two Black brigades of just over 2,000 men, who took **800+ casualties including 130+ killed**, repeatedly closing on the works after color bearers fell. **Fourteen USCT soldiers earned the Medal of Honor** — the **largest number of Black Medals of Honor from a single engagement**. Recipients: **William H. Barnes, Powhatan Beaty, James H. Bronson, Christian A. Fleetwood, James Daniel Gardiner (Gardner), James H. Harris, Thomas R. Hawkins, Alfred B. Hilton, Milton M. Holland, Miles James, Alexander Kelly, Robert A. Pinn, Edward Ratcliff, and Charles Veal.** **Sgt. Maj. Christian A. Fleetwood** (4th USCT, born free in Baltimore July 21, 1840), seized the national colors when the bearers were shot, recording in his diary that he "saved colors"; his medal was awarded April 6, 1865. (Recipients came from regiments including the 4th, 5th, 6th, 36th, and 38th USCT.)

#### 7. Fort Pillow Massacre, Tennessee — April 12, 1864
Confederate cavalry under **Maj. Gen. Nathan Bedford Forrest** (later the first Grand Wizard of the KKK) attacked the Mississippi River fort. The garrison of roughly **585–605 men** was about half Black, comprising the **6th U.S. Colored Heavy Artillery**, a section of the **2nd U.S. Colored Light Artillery (Memphis Battery)**, and **Bradford's Battalion, 13th Tennessee Cavalry (white Unionists)**. Union commander **Maj. Lionel F. Booth** was killed by a sniper early; **Maj. William F. Bradford** took over. After the fort fell, Confederates **killed many soldiers attempting to surrender**, disproportionately the Black troops: only about **58 Black soldiers (~20%) were taken prisoner versus ~168 white soldiers (~60%)**. Total Union dead are reported between **~277 and ~297** (some later tabulations cite up to ~350 killed/mortally wounded); Confederate losses were about **14 killed, 86 wounded**. The **U.S. Congressional Joint Committee on the Conduct of the War** investigated and (May 1864) concluded a massacre of surrendered men had occurred — its report became Union propaganda. **"Remember Fort Pillow!" became a rallying cry** for USCT soldiers. *(Historians debate Forrest's precise culpability — whether he ordered, condoned, or merely failed to halt the killings — making intent the contested element; that a massacre of surrendering Black soldiers occurred is well established.)*

#### 8. Nashville, Tennessee — December 15–16, 1864
In Gen. George H. Thomas's decisive destruction of John Bell Hood's Army of Tennessee, USCT regiments under **Maj. Gen. James B. Steedman** (two brigades, seven regiments) anchored the Union left and assaulted **Peach Orchard Hill / Overton Hill** on December 16. The principal Black regiments included the **12th, 13th, and 100th USCT** (plus others such as the 14th, 17th, 18th, 44th). The **13th USCT** — mostly recently freed "contrabands" — lost heavily in its first combat: about **55 killed and 165 wounded (≈40% casualties)**, with five color bearers shot. Though their frontal assault on Overton Hill failed bloodily, it pinned Confederate forces and helped enable the breakthrough at Shy's Hill that shattered Hood's army. Thomas, surveying the integrated dead, reportedly said, "Gentlemen, this settles it; the Negro will fight."

### The Criticism, Debate, and Historiography (to teach)

#### Unequal Pay (1863–1864)
USCT soldiers were initially paid under the **Militia Act of July 17, 1862** as laborers: **$10/month minus a $3 clothing deduction = $7 net**, versus **$13/month (no clothing deduction) for white privates**. This was a profound grievance. The **54th and 55th Massachusetts refused all pay for ~18 months** rather than accept discriminatory wages — even after Massachusetts offered to make up the difference, they refused on principle, demanding the U.S. government treat them equally. **Cpl. James Henry Gooding** of the 54th wrote a celebrated September 1863 letter directly to President Lincoln protesting the inequity (Gooding was later captured and died at Andersonville in 1864). **Sgt. William Walker** of the 3rd South Carolina (later 21st USCT) led his company to stack arms in protest, was court-martialed for mutiny, and was **executed by firing squad on February 29, 1864** — the starkest illustration of the stakes. **Congress equalized pay on June 15, 1864**, retroactive to enlistment for those free before the war (and, by an 1864 Attorney General opinion and subsequent action, ultimately for others).

#### White-Officer Policy
War Department policy (codified May 1863) reserved commissioned officer ranks for white men, on the prevailing racist assumption that Black men lacked capacity for command. The **Free Military School for Applicants for the Command of Colored Regiments** opened in Philadelphia (operating ~December 1863 through 1864) to train *white* officers; Black men were not admitted to its officer track. Roughly **~100–110 Black men nonetheless received commissions** during the war, overwhelmingly as **chaplains and surgeons** (e.g., **Maj. Martin R. Delany**, commissioned as the first Black line field officer in early 1865; **Lt. Stephen A. Swails** of the 54th Massachusetts, commissioned by Gov. John A. Andrew March 1864, whose mustering as a line officer the War Department resisted for months over his race). The policy was a structural insult that limited Black advancement and remained a live grievance.

#### Confederate Refusal to Treat Captured USCT as POWs
On **December 23/24, 1862, Jefferson Davis proclaimed** that captured Black soldiers be turned over to state authorities (to be enslaved or punished under state slave codes) and that their white officers be treated as inciters of servile insurrection. The **Confederate Congress, on May 1, 1863** (signed by Davis), formalized this: captured Black soldiers to be delivered to the states (enslavement/death), white officers to face military tribunals and potential execution for "inciting insurrection." This Confederate refusal to exchange or protect captured USCT was a primary cause of the **breakdown of the prisoner-exchange cartel**. **Lincoln responded with the Order of Retaliation (General Order No. 252, July 30, 1863)**, threatening to execute or place at hard labor a Confederate prisoner for every Union soldier killed or enslaved in violation of the laws of war. The threat was largely not carried out, but the policy framed the lethal stakes for USCT soldiers, who knew capture could mean death or re-enslavement (e.g., the massacres at Fort Pillow, the Crater, Poison Spring, and Saltville).

#### The Lost Cause Erasure and Modern Reassessment
In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the **Lost Cause** interpretation — promoted by Confederate veterans' organizations, the United Daughters of the Confederacy, and sympathetic historians — recast the war as a noble defense of states' rights divorced from slavery, and the **Reconciliationist** memory privileged sectional (white) reunion. Both **largely erased Black soldiers and the emancipationist meaning of the war** from popular memory (historian **David W. Blight**'s *Race and Reunion*, 2001, is the landmark analysis of this erasure). For decades USCT contributions were minimized in textbooks, monuments, and film (e.g., *The Birth of a Nation*, 1915; *Gone with the Wind*, 1939). The **1989 film *Glory*** (depicting the 54th Massachusetts and Fort Wagner) marked a popular-culture turning point, reintroducing Black soldiers into mainstream Civil War memory and helping shift it toward the **Emancipation Cause**. Modern scholarship — including **Joseph T. Glatthaar's *Forged in Battle* (1990)**, **Dudley Taylor Cornish's *The Sable Arm* (1956)**, **Noah Andre Trudeau's *Like Men of War* (1998)**, **John David Smith (ed.), *Black Soldiers in Blue* (2002)**, and the NPS/American Battlefield Trust public-history programs — has restored the USCT to a central place, documenting both their combat record and the discrimination they endured. Battlefield preservation efforts (e.g., at New Market Heights and Milliken's Bend) and the recovery of soldiers' graves are part of this ongoing reassessment.

## Teaching the War at PhD Level — Historiography & Criticism Framework

## Teaching the Civil War at PhD Level Inside a Game: Historiography, Criticism, and a Provenance-Disciplined Pedagogy

This brief synthesizes the major scholarly debates a graduate-level player must encounter and prescribes a concrete in-game pedagogical architecture built on a "Verified vs. Inferred" provenance discipline, multiple-interpretation framing, and primary-source confrontation. The governing principle: the game must teach *historical thinking* (Sam Wineburg's triad of **sourcing, contextualization, corroboration**), not deliver settled conclusions. It must avoid both hagiography (the bronze-statue "great captains" mode) and Lost Cause apologia, while honestly modeling where consensus exists and where genuine scholarly contestation remains.

---

### I. THE CENTRAL CAUSE: Slavery vs. the Lost Cause Myth

**Scholarly consensus (HIGH confidence):** Slavery was the central, precipitating cause of secession and thus of the war. This is not a "both sides" question among professional historians; it is the modern consensus, endorsed by figures including James M. McPherson (Pulitzer, *Battle Cry of Freedom*, 1988). The primary-source record is unambiguous:

- **Alexander H. Stephens's "Cornerstone Speech"** (Savannah, GA, Athenaeum, **March 21, 1861**), delivered as Confederate Vice President: the new government's "foundations are laid, its cornerstone rests, upon the great truth that the negro is not equal to the white man; that slavery — subordination to the superior race — is his natural and normal condition." This is the single clearest contemporaneous statement that secession's goal was the preservation of slavery.
- **Declarations of Causes**: Of the 11 seceding states, four issued formal "Declarations of Causes" (South Carolina, Mississippi, Georgia, Texas). **South Carolina** seceded first (ordinance **Dec. 20, 1860**; declaration **Dec. 24, 1860**), citing Northern states' refusal to enforce the Fugitive Slave Act. **Mississippi's** declaration (**Jan. 9, 1861**; some sources give final adoption Jan. 29, 1861) opens: "Our position is thoroughly identified with the institution of slavery — the greatest material interest of the world." **Georgia's** cited "numerous and serious causes of complaint against our non-slaveholding confederate States with reference to the subject of African slavery." Primary texts are hosted at Yale's **Avalon Project** and the **American Battlefield Trust**.

**The Lost Cause (its origins, as a historiographical object — HIGH confidence on facts of origin):** A post-war mythic narrative that romanticized the Confederacy and *denied* slavery's centrality. Key facts to teach as *historiography* (the myth itself is to be analyzed, not endorsed):
- The term was coined by **Edward A. Pollard** (Richmond Examiner editor) in *The Lost Cause: A New Southern History of the War of the Confederates* (**1866**).
- **Jubal A. Early** became its most influential propagandist; he co-founded the **Southern Historical Society (1869)**, which built the narrative through its papers.
- Tellingly, Stephens himself recanted in his postwar *A Constitutional View of the Late War Between the States* (1868–70), reframing the war as constitutional principle, not slavery — a documentable pivot that lets players watch myth-making happen.
- Institutional vectors: **United Daughters of the Confederacy** (monuments, textbooks), **United Confederate Veterans** (consolidated 1889), *Confederate Veteran* magazine (1893–1932).
- Six tenets to teach as claims-to-be-tested: secession as constitutional/non-slavery; benevolent slavery and "faithful slaves"; defeat by overwhelming numbers/industry, not on the field; gallant soldiers; **Lee as sainted "Christian soldier"** (intensified after his 1870 death); women's memorial sacrifice.

**Pedagogical handling:** Present the Cornerstone Speech and a secession declaration side-by-side with a Lost Cause text (Pollard or Early). The player's task is *corroboration and sourcing* — to detect the contradiction. The "states' rights" counter-argument should be surfaced as the historical Lost Cause position and then tested against the documents, not pre-dismissed. The contested residue worth flagging at LOW-MED confidence: *ordinary soldiers'* motivations were more heterogeneous (honor, locality, conscription) — distinguish soldier motivation from *secession's institutional cause*.

---

### II. EMANCIPATION AS MILITARY + MORAL TURNING POINT

- **Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation: Sept. 22, 1862**, issued five days after **Antietam (Sept. 17, 1862)** — the bloodiest single day in American history, ~**22,700–23,000** killed/wounded/missing. The battle's strategic-draw-as-Union-victory gave Lincoln the political footing to act and helped forestall European (esp. British) recognition of the Confederacy.
- **Final Emancipation Proclamation: Jan. 1, 1863.** Critical nuance to teach (a classic "myth vs. document" lesson): it freed slaves *only* in Confederate-held territory in rebellion, explicitly **exempting the loyal border states** (Delaware, Maryland, Kentucky, Missouri) and Union-occupied areas. It was a war measure under presidential war powers, not a universal abolition — that came with the **13th Amendment** (passed Congress **Jan. 31, 1865**; ratified **Dec. 6, 1865**).
- Military transformation: it authorized Black enlistment. **United States Colored Troops (USCT)**: ~**179,000** served (≈**10%** of the Union Army by war's end); roughly **37,000–40,000** died. The **54th Massachusetts Infantry** assault on **Fort/Battery Wagner (July 18, 1863)** is the iconic engagement (the 54th lost ~281 of ~600); **Sgt. William H. Carney** became the first African American Medal of Honor recipient (awarded 1900) for saving the colors.

**Pedagogical handling:** The "did the Proclamation free anyone immediately?" question is a perfect Verified-vs-Inferred exercise — the *document text* (Verified) vs. *popular memory* (the Inferred myth of instant universal freedom). Frame the historiographical debate: Lincoln-the-reluctant-pragmatist vs. Lincoln-the-principled-emancipator vs. the "self-emancipation" thesis (enslaved people forced the issue by fleeing to Union lines — associated with the Freedmen and Southern Society Project / Ira Berlin).

---

### III. HARD WAR / TOTAL WAR (Sherman)

- Core debate: was Sherman's **March to the Sea (Nov.–Dec. 1864)** "total war"? **Mark Grimsley**, *The Hard Hand of War: Union Military Policy Toward Southern Civilians, 1861–1865* (1995), argues NO — he rejects the anachronistic "total war" label in favor of "**hard war**" (a term Union policymakers actually used) and "**directed severity**": targeting of war-supporting infrastructure (railroads, factories, farms producing for the army) rather than systematic killing of civilians. Grimsley stresses restraint tempered by a sense of moral justice.
- The counter-current: **Daniel E. Sutherland** and others warn against "sanitizing" the war — destruction and dislocation take many forms and must be measured by victims' experience (esp. enslaved people and Southern civilians). This is a live, unresolved scholarly tension (MED confidence on where balance lies).

**Pedagogical handling:** Make the *terminology itself* the lesson — let the player choose a label ("total war" / "hard war" / "directed severity") and surface which historians use which and why. Avoid the spurious WWII analogy (the "Sherman anticipated Hitler" trope appears in popular sources and should be explicitly flagged as ahistorical).

---

### IV. COMMAND CONTROVERSIES (model multiple interpretations, never a single verdict)

- **George B. McClellan / Peninsula Campaign (1862):** Standard view — chronic slowness, timidity, vast overestimation of enemy strength (fed by Pinkerton intelligence); poor relations with Lincoln. Removed as general-in-chief March 1862; removed from Army of the Potomac command **Nov. 5, 1862** (he learned of his first demotion via newspaper). The pro-McClellan minority blames Lincoln's withholding of McDowell's corps and political interference. Recent scholarship (e.g., critiques of the "blame-Lincoln" thesis) keeps this contested.
- **Gettysburg (July 1–3, 1863):** The richest controversy cluster. Day 3 **Pickett's Charge** was Lee's decision over **James Longstreet's** vigorous objection ("I believe it will fail," Longstreet told his artillery chief E.P. Alexander; he preferred a strategic move around the Union left). Day 1–2: **Richard S. Ewell's** failure to seize Cemetery/Culp's Hill "if practicable" — the war's most argued discretionary order. Compounding factors: **A.P. Hill** ill, **J.E.B. Stuart's** cavalry absent (depriving Lee of intelligence). Crucial caution: much "Longstreet-as-scapegoat" framing is itself a *Lost Cause artifact* — Early and the Southern Historical Society deflected blame from Lee onto Longstreet after Lee's death. Teach the controversy AND its historiographical contamination.
- **Braxton Bragg / Army of Tennessee:** Among the most-debated. Won **Chickamauga (Sept. 1863)** with Longstreet's help but failed to exploit it; notorious feuds with subordinates (esp. **Leonidas Polk**); after Stones River he circulated a "round-robin" demanding subordinates affirm confidence in him. **Earl J. Hess** (*Braxton Bragg: The Most Hated Man of the Confederacy*, 2016) places more blame on insubordinate corps commanders; others (Samuel Martin) split it differently. Genuinely unresolved (MED confidence) — ideal for an "assign-the-blame" interpretive panel.

**Pedagogical handling:** Each controversy = a multi-card "interpretation panel": Interpretation A / B / (C), the named historians and works behind each, the primary evidence each cites, and an explicit flag where one interpretation is itself a *memory artifact* (Longstreet, McClellan apologetics) rather than evidence-driven.

---

### V. MEDICINE AND THE SCALE OF DEATH

- **Death toll — a teachable historiographical revision (HIGH confidence on the revision; the number itself is a range):** The traditional figure was ~**620,000**. **J. David Hacker** (Binghamton Univ.), using newly digitized census microdata (Minnesota Population Center / IPUMS), published a census-based count in *Civil War History* (**Dec. 2011**) revising the toll to ~**750,000**, with a wide plausible range of **650,000–850,000**. McPherson called it plausible. This is a model "how do we know what we know" lesson — the number is *contested and estimated*, not counted.
- **Cause of death:** roughly **two-thirds died of disease** (dysentery, diarrhea, typhoid, measles, malaria, pneumonia), one-third from battle/wounds — overturning the romantic battlefield-death image.
- **Medical practice:** ~**60,000 amputations**; anesthesia (chloroform in ~75%, also ether) used in ~95% of major operations — countering the "biting-a-bullet" myth. **Jonathan Letterman** built the first systematic military evacuation/triage chain (aid stations → field hospitals → general hospitals; ambulance corps) for the Army of the Potomac (1862), the ancestor of modern combat medicine. The **U.S. Sanitary Commission** and figures like **Clara Barton** and **Dorothea Dix** belong here.

**Pedagogical handling:** Use the death toll to model *epistemic humility*: present 620k → 750k as a worked example of revision via new evidence and method. Mark the number LOW-MED confidence with an explicit range, never a false-precision single figure.

---

### VI. RECONSTRUCTION AND MEMORY

- **The amendments:** 13th (ratified Dec. 6, 1865, abolition); 14th (passed Congress June 13, 1866; ratified July 9, 1868 — birthright citizenship, due process, equal protection); 15th (ratified March 30, 1870 — Black male suffrage).
- **The historiographical war (HIGH confidence on the arc):** The **Dunning School** (William A. Dunning, Columbia, 1857–1922, and students) dominated for ~half a century, portraying Reconstruction as a tragic era of "Negro rule," corrupt carpetbaggers and scalawags, and vindictive Radical Republicans — a narrative that, as **Eric Foner** argues, functioned as intellectual *justification for Jim Crow and Black disenfranchisement*. **W.E.B. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935)** was the great early counter, largely ignored at the time. The revisionist turn (1960s: Kenneth Stampp, John Hope Franklin) culminated in Foner's ***Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution, 1863–1877* (1988)** — the modern standard, framing Reconstruction as a real if defeated democratic experiment.
- **The end:** The disputed 1876 election and **Compromise of 1877** withdrew federal troops, enabling "Redemption," Jim Crow, and disenfranchisement (poll taxes, literacy tests, eventually *Plessy v. Ferguson*, 1896).

**Pedagogical handling:** Present Dunning vs. Du Bois vs. Foner as a *literal sequence of interpretations over time* — the player sees that "history" changed because *historians and society changed*, the deepest historiographical lesson. Connect directly back to Section I: Lost Cause + Dunning School were a single memory regime.

### VII. RECOMMENDED IN-GAME PEDAGOGICAL STRUCTURE

**1. The "Verified vs. Inferred" provenance discipline (the core mechanic).** Every factual assertion in the game UI carries a provenance tag:
- **VERIFIED** — directly attested by a primary source or settled consensus, with the citation one tap away (e.g., the date of Antietam; the text of the Cornerstone Speech).
- **INFERRED** — a reasonable reconstruction/estimate the game makes for playability or where evidence is thin (e.g., exact regimental positions at a given hour; a "what-if" outcome). Visually distinct (color/icon).
- **CONTESTED** — a genuine scholarly dispute (e.g., Bragg's blame; "total war" labeling; the 750k toll). Tapping opens the multiple-interpretation panel.
This trains the player to ask Wineburg's *sourcing* question reflexively and inoculates against the game's own fabrications being mistaken for fact.

**2. Optional deep-dive panels ("Historian's Desk").** Non-blocking, opt-in overlays attached to events/units. Each contains: (a) 1–3 **primary-source quotes** with full citation; (b) the **named historians and works** on each side; (c) a **confidence meter**; (d) an explicit **"is this a memory artifact?"** flag where relevant (Lost Cause/Dunning contamination). Keeps casual play fast; rewards graduate-level depth.

**3. Multiple-interpretation framing as default for any controversy.** Never a single authorial verdict on McClellan, Gettysburg Day 3, Sherman, Bragg, or Lincoln's motives. Present Interpretation A / B / (C), the evidence each marshals, and let the player weigh it — modeling *corroboration*.

**4. Primary-source confrontation events.** At key nodes, drop the actual document (Cornerstone Speech, Mississippi declaration, the Proclamation's border-state exemption, a Letterman field-hospital order) and pose a sourcing/corroboration question. This is the anti-Lost-Cause vaccine: the documents do the arguing.

**5. Explicit anti-hagiography / anti-apologia guardrails.** No reverential "great captain" framing of Lee or Sherman without the countervailing record (Lee the enslaver; Sherman's destruction from the victims' view). No "states' rights" framing presented as settled truth — surfaced as the historical Lost Cause claim, then tested. Confederate perspectives are *represented and analyzed*, never *endorsed*.

**6. Historiography-over-time module (capstone).** A timeline showing how interpretation itself changed: Lost Cause/Dunning (1866–1950s) → Revisionists (1960s) → Foner/modern consensus (1988–) → the 620k→750k revision (2011). Teaches that historical knowledge is *produced and revised*, the defining PhD-level insight.

**7. Confidence-tiered fact economy.** Tie every fact card to a confidence tier (high/med/low) with a visible range for estimates (death toll, casualty counts) — never false precision. Where the game must invent unit positions for gameplay, label them INFERRED.

This architecture delivers depth without lecturing, respects the documentary record, refuses both bronze-statue worship and Lost Cause apologia, and — most importantly — teaches the player to *think like a historian* rather than to *memorize a verdict*.

## Public-Domain Image Sources for Civil War Units, Weapons, Flags, and USCT

A fetch-pipeline guide for sourcing rights-clear images of weapons, flags, USCT soldiers/units, and uniforms. Organized by (1) the canonical repositories and their rights mechanics, (2) the two machine APIs your pipeline can hit (Wikipedia REST summary + Commons license API), and (3) concrete, verified file/article titles per subject.

---

### 1. The Five Canonical Repositories (and how to verify PD status)

**A. Wikimedia Commons** — `commons.wikimedia.org`
The single best pipeline target because every file carries a machine-readable license. Files are NOT uniformly PD — Commons hosts public-domain, CC0, CC BY, and CC BY-SA. **You must check each file's license programmatically** (see Section 2B). Acceptable for a game: `Public domain`, `PD-US`, `PD-old`, `CC0`. Requires attribution (still usable): `CC BY`, `CC BY-SA`. The bulk of period photographs (1861–1865) are PD by age (PD-US-expired, published pre-1929). Modern photographs of artifacts/battlefields (a cannon at a park, a museum musket) are usually CC BY-SA from the photographer OR PD if the uploading institution (e.g., Smithsonian) released them.

**B. Library of Congress (LoC)** — `loc.gov` and Prints & Photographs Online Catalog (PPOC, `loc.gov/pictures/`)
The richest source of period Civil War photographs (Mathew Brady studio, Alexander Gardner, Timothy O'Sullivan; the ~7,000-image "Civil War Photographs, 1861–1865" collection, item 94837685). Verify PD via the **"Rights Advisory"** field. The phrase to look for is **"No known restrictions on publication."** Note LoC's caveat: it does not own rights and the label means LoC has found no restrictions, not a copyright clearance — but for period works pre-1929 this maps cleanly to PD. JSON access: append **`?fo=json`** to any `loc.gov` item page to get structured data (rights, image resource URLs). Image Services microservice returns IIIF-style derivatives.

**C. National Archives (NARA)** — `catalog.archives.gov`
Holds federal Civil War records: official photographs, USCT muster/service records, signal corps imagery. Rights rule: works **created by U.S. federal employees in their official duties are public domain** (not copyrightable, 17 U.S.C. §105). NARA's catalog has an "Access Restrictions" / "Use Restrictions" field — most general holdings read "Unrestricted." Caveat: NARA's *Special Media* holdings may contain donated third-party material with residual copyright; check the per-record restriction field. No written permission required for PD items; credit "National Archives and Records Administration" is requested, not required. NARA also has a public API (catalog.archives.gov/api).

**D. Smithsonian Open Access** — `si.edu/openaccess`, search at `si.edu`
Launched February 25, 2020; released ~2.8 million (now 4.5M+) images and metadata as **CC0** (public-domain dedication, no attribution required). Includes National Museum of American History (NMAH) weapons (the Springfield/Colt muskets), National Portrait Gallery, and the National Museum of African American History and Culture (NMAAHC) — relevant for USCT material. API: register a free key at `api.data.gov`; query the Open Access API. Many Smithsonian objects are ALSO mirrored to Wikimedia Commons (file names contain `-NMAH-` or `-NPG-`), where they appear as `Public domain` — meaning your Commons pipeline picks them up automatically.

**E. American Battlefield Trust / NPS / scholarly** — `battlefields.org`, `nps.gov`
Use these for fact-checking unit designations, dates, and battle context — NOT as image fetch targets. Their site imagery is often licensed/copyrighted. Treat as citation sources, not pipeline inputs.

**Other useful PD-rich corpora on Commons:** the *West Point Atlas* maps; *Harper's Weekly* and *Frank Leslie's Illustrated* wood engravings (PD by age); the *Photographic History of the Civil War* (Miller, 1911, PD); Gardner's *Photographic Sketch Book of the War* (1866, PD).

### 2. The Two Machine APIs Your Pipeline Hits

**A. Wikipedia REST Summary API** (for the lead image + extract per topic)
Endpoint: `https://en.wikipedia.org/api/rest_v1/page/summary/{ARTICLE_TITLE}` (underscores for spaces; URL-encode).
Returns JSON with: `title`, `extract` (plain-text lead), `thumbnail.{source,width,height}`, and **`originalimage.{source,width,height}`** — the latter is the full-resolution lead image URL on `upload.wikimedia.org`.
**Verified live example** — `GET /page/summary/Springfield_Model_1861` returns `originalimage.source` =
`https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/Colt_Model_1861_Special_Musket-NMAH-ET2012-13948.jpg` (1000×750).
**Critical nuance (high confidence, verified):** the Springfield 1861 article's lead image is actually a *Colt Special Model 1861 contract musket* from the Smithsonian, not an arsenal-made Springfield. They are near-identical .58-cal rifle-muskets, but if exactness matters, pull from `Category:Springfield Model 1861` instead of the article lead.
**Pitfall:** the REST summary response does NOT include license data. You must take the image filename and hit the Commons API (below) to confirm rights before using it.

**B. Commons License API** (the mandatory rights gate)
Endpoint: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/api.php?action=query&prop=imageinfo&iiprop=extmetadata|url|mime&format=json&titles=File:{FILENAME}`
Key fields inside `extmetadata`: **`LicenseShortName`** (e.g., "Public domain", "CC0", "CC BY-SA 4.0"), `License` (machine code, e.g., `pd`, `cc0`, `cc-by-sa-4.0`), `UsageTerms`, `Artist`, `Credit`, `Attribution`.
Decision logic: accept if `License` ∈ {`pd`, `cc0`, codes starting `cc-by`}; for any `cc-by*` capture `Artist`/`Credit` and render attribution.
**Verified live example** — querying `File:Colt_Model_1861_Special_Musket-NMAH-ET2012-13948.jpg` returns `LicenseShortName`="Public domain", `License`="pd", `Artist`="The Smithsonian Institution", `Credit`=link to NMAH object nmah_417404, direct URL on `upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/...`. Confirmed PD.
Tip: add `iiextmetadatafilter=LicenseShortName|License|Artist|Credit|UsageTerms` to shrink payloads (max 50 keys / 500 for bots).

#### 3.4 Flags — USCT (regimental)
- **David Bustill Bowser flags** — Bowser, a Black Philadelphia artist, painted the regimental colors for ~11 USCT regiments mustered at **Camp William Penn** (near Philadelphia) in 1863. Article: `David Bustill Bowser`. Documented designs/mottoes: **3rd & 127th USCT** "We Will Prove Ourselves Men" / "Rather Die Freemen Than Live To Be Slaves"; **24th USCT** "Let Soldiers in War, Be Citizens in Peace"; **45th USCT** "One Cause, One Country"; **6th USCT** (CDV images of its flags survive). High confidence on the artist, Camp William Penn, and these mottoes; the originals were destroyed (reportedly by West Point's museum, 1940s) but ~7 survive in photographs. **CDV/period photos of these flags are PD by age** — search Commons and LoC for "Bowser USCT flag."
- For generic USCT colors, also check NMAAHC (Smithsonian Open Access, CC0) and the Pennsylvania Capitol Preservation Committee flag collection (citation only).

#### 3.5 USCT Soldiers / Units (photographs)
- **54th Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry** — Article: `54th Massachusetts Volunteer Regiment` (note: `54th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment` redirects). The 2nd Black regiment raised in the North (after the 1st Kansas Colored); famed for the July 18, 1863 assault on Battery/Fort Wagner, Morris Island SC, where Col. Robert Gould Shaw was killed and Sgt. William H. Carney earned the Medal of Honor (the regimental colors were saved). NPS: `nps.gov/articles/54th-massachusetts-regiment.htm`.
- **55th Massachusetts Infantry** — Article: `55th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment` (the 54th's sister regiment).
- **United States Colored Troops** (umbrella) — Article: `United States Colored Troops`. ~175 regiments, ~178,000–186,000 men; Bureau of Colored Troops established May 22, 1863 (General Order No. 143). Period photographs of USCT soldiers are abundant and PD at LoC (search "United States Colored Troops" with "No known restrictions") and NARA (federal photos, PD).
- **Commons categories:** `Category:United States Colored Troops`; `Category:54th Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry`.

#### 3.6 Uniforms by branch
- **Overview articles (with PD plates):** `Uniform of the Union Army`, `Military uniforms of the Confederate States` (a.k.a. uniform of the Confederate States Army). Branch trim colors — Union: **infantry sky-blue, cavalry yellow, artillery red** (corps/branch piping). Confederate (1861 regs): **infantry blue, cavalry yellow, artillery red** facings on cadet-gray.
- **Best PD plate set:** the U.S. War Department *Uniform Regulations* / Quartermaster plates, and the modern but PD-released **U.S. Army Center of Military History** "American Soldier" series (federal work, PD) — search Commons `Category:Uniforms of the Union Army`, `Category:Uniforms of the Confederate States of America`.
- Period studio portraits at LoC (Brady/Gardner) show actual uniforms per branch and are PD ("No known restrictions").

### 4. Recommended Pipeline Flow
1. For each game subject, hit **Wikipedia REST summary** → grab `originalimage.source` and the filename.
2. Pass that `File:` title to the **Commons imageinfo/extmetadata** call → read `LicenseShortName`/`License`.
3. Accept if PD/CC0/CC-BY*; for CC-BY* capture `Artist`+`Credit` and render attribution; reject otherwise and fall back to the relevant `Category:` (enumerate members via `list=categorymembers`) re-running step 2 until a PD/CC0 file passes.
4. For period photographs (USCT, soldiers, flags-in-the-field) where Commons is thin, query **LoC `?fo=json`** filtering Rights Advisory = "No known restrictions," or **Smithsonian Open Access API** (all CC0), or **NARA catalog API** (federal = PD).
