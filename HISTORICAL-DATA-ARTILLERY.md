## 2. Organization — raising a battery, building a Cannon Corps

**The battery is the recruitable unit.** [Verified] The three-tier hierarchy: **battery** (captain) > 2-gun **section** (lieutenant) > **piece/platoon** = gun + caisson under a sergeant ("Chief of the Piece"). The project OOB already records section-level detachments (HISTORICAL-DATA.md L53: "Battery G, 1st U.S. Artillery (2nd Section)…1 gun, Lt. Peter C. Hains"), so the section is the natural smallest detachable unit and the battery the natural purchase atom.

**Battery sizes (the core asymmetry).** [Verified] Federal doctrine set the field battery at **six guns**, usually all one caliber. Confederate batteries typically fielded **four** (often fewer, often mixed-caliber) from chronic gun, horse, and production shortages. In game: `batteryGunsUnion: 6`, `batteryGunsCS: 4`.

**A battery is a logistics object, not a firepower token.** [Verified] A full Union 6-gun battery ≈ **150 men**, 6 guns + 6 caissons + battery wagon + traveling forge (~14 carriages), and **~110 horses** (HISTORICAL-DATA.md L818). Each gun and each caisson rolled on a 6-horse limber team in three pairs (wheel / swing / lead), one postilion driver per pair riding the **left** horse. Horseflesh — not gun losses — was the binding mobility constraint: a battery short of teams abandoned guns. **Caveat:** Confederate gun teams were frequently **four** horses from shortage, so CS team-horse counts run below a straight Union scale-down.

**The 1863 battalion reform.** [Verified, with timing correction] Until 1863 both armies **"penny-packeted"** batteries one-per-infantry-brigade under infantry officers, preventing massed fire. The corrective:
- **Army of the Potomac:** Brig. Gen. **Henry J. Hunt** (Chief of Artillery since Sept 1862 — *after* Malvern Hill, **not** via the 1863 reorg) gained a free hand post-Chancellorsville to pool batteries into **per-corps artillery brigades** plus a large army-level **Artillery Reserve** (5 brigades / 21 batteries under Brig. Gen. Robert O. Tyler). Cross-check: Hunt commanded "Battery M, 2nd U.S. Artillery — 4 guns" as a captain at First Bull Run (HISTORICAL-DATA.md L69).
- **Army of Northern Virginia:** Lee **approved the battalion system Feb 16, 1863 — BEFORE Chancellorsville**, not after. Battalions held **four OR six** batteries (not "about 4"), assigned to corps under chiefs of artillery and army chief **William N. Pendleton**. **Project-file correction:** HISTORICAL-DATA.md L341 lists Pendleton only as "Chief of Artillery **Col.** William N. Pendleton" — **not** as commanding the Rockbridge Artillery, and as **Colonel**, not Brig. Gen. (his BG rank and ANV-chief role came later). What followed Chancellorsville was **abolition of the ANV army-level reserve**, its batteries pushed down to the corps battalions.

**Why massing mattered.** [Verified] Converging fire from 20–40 guns on one sector was decisively more destructive than the same guns dispersed, and it let a chief of artillery ration ammunition and shift fire to the decisive point — the lesson of Malvern Hill (1862) and Gettysburg (1863).

**The Artillery Reserve is a Union, not symmetric, feature.** [Corrected per verify] The AoP Reserve was a major army-level command; the ANV **dropped** its general reserve after Chancellorsville. Model "+reserve" as a Union strength / Confederate gap.

**Horse ("flying") artillery.** [Verified] Every cannoneer individually mounted, fast enough to maneuver with cavalry — elite, horse-intensive, scarce. Templates: Tidball's Battery A, 2nd U.S. (HISTORICAL-DATA.md L147) and Pelham's Stuart's Horse Artillery (organized Dec 1861). Model as a mobility upgrade bound to cavalry, costing extra horses.

**Build stack (Inferred design synthesis):** gun → section → battery (6 US / 4 CS, consumes men + ~50–110 horses + a caliber choice) → ~4–6-battery battalion/brigade (1863 massing unlock) → corps artillery + Artillery Reserve (Union strength) → horse artillery (cavalry-bound mobility upgrade). Union: larger single-caliber batteries (firepower/resupply bonus). Confederacy: smaller, often mixed-caliber (resupply malus), horse-attrition mobility malus, no army reserve.

## 3. Ammunition & range bands (the four families + the fuze problem)

All field guns drew on the same four projectile families; the smoothbore Napoleon is the reference case. [Verified unless noted]

| Round | Fuze? | Effective band | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Solid shot** | No | ~600–1,700 yds | battering, counter-battery, ricochet ("grazing") | Smoothbore fires *spherical* round shot — **not** a "bolt" (a bolt is the rifled-gun solid projectile). Ricochet bonus on firm dry ground. |
| **Common shell** | Yes (Bormann / paper time) | ~600–1,500 yds | area effect vs. cover, structures | Fragments into a *small number* of large irregular pieces — low fragment count vs. case. |
| **Spherical case (Shrapnel)** | Yes (time) | ~500–1,200 yds | mid-range air-burst antipersonnel | 12-pdr case ≈ **78 musket balls** + small burster; lethality depends entirely on correct fuze timing (burst ~50–75 yds short and above). |
| **Canister** | **No** | ~100–400 yds (devastating inside ~250) | close defense — a giant shotgun | 12-pdr canister ≈ **27 cast-iron balls, ~1.48–1.5 in / ~4–4.5 oz each** (cast iron) in sawdust. **[Corrected:** earlier "~1.06–1.5 oz" was musket/case-ball weight, off by ~3–4×.] No fuze = cannot fail like a time-fuzed round. |
| **Double canister** | No | point-blank (~150–250 yds) | emergency assault-stopper | Two rounds over a single/reduced charge against charging infantry — Antietam, Gettysburg (Cushing's at the Angle), Stones River. |

**Rifled-bore caveat:** [Verified] canister patterns *poorly* through a rifled bore (the spin disperses it), so the 3-inch Ordnance and Parrott are weaker at point-blank infantry defense than the Napoleon — keep a smoothbore for the close work.

**The Confederate fuze problem.** [Verified, with correction] This is documented manufacturing/metallurgy history, **NOT** Lost Cause framing. The Confederacy did **not** "lack the Bormann fuze" — it **made an unreliable domestic copy** (soft white-metal alloy) that leaked flame into the burster cavity, causing premature muzzle/air bursts; the CS added a rebate/gasket modification and **largely abandoned the Bormann by early 1863** for irregular paper and wooden time fuzes. The result: fuze-dependent **shell and spherical case** burst short, long, or not at all, while **solid shot and canister (no fuze) were comparatively immune**. **Powder caveat:** the Augusta Powder Works produced world-class powder from 1862, so by mid-war powder ceased to be the binding constraint — **fuze reliability** and **resupply timing/quantity** were the sharper problems. **Framing caveat (Hess 2022):** long-range fuze unreliability was a problem **neither** side fully solved; the CS deficit is one of *degree*. The genuinely uniquely-CS handicaps are scarcity, captured-piece dependence, copper/bronze shortage, and import fragility.

**Game model (Inferred):** apply a per-shot dud/premature probability **only** to CS shell and case; leave shot and canister unaffected. *The commonly-cited 20–40% failure band is a game-balance interpolation, NOT a tabulated historical measurement — keep the disclaimer attached wherever the number appears.*

## 4. The Confederate gap (asymmetry model)

[All Verified unless noted; the 4-modifier model is Inferred design.]
1. **SUPPLY / COUNT.** Manufacturing rested on a single dominant works — **Tredegar (Richmond), ~1,100 cannon, ~half of all CS domestic artillery** (HISTORICAL-DATA-ECONOMY.md L111, American Battlefield Trust) — plus a thin tier (Bellona, Macon, Columbus, Augusta, Selma, Charleston). The South leaned heavily on **captured Federal guns** and deliberately matched Federal calibers (3-inch, 10-pdr Parrott) to use captured ammunition; a 3-inch gun could fire 10-pdr Parrott rounds in a pinch. CS gun strength **partly scaled with Union battlefield losses** (First Manassas, Harpers Ferry, 2nd Manassas, Winchester). *Note: the CS could not reliably manufacture the 3-inch Ordnance Rifle — "adopted Federal calibers" means fielding captured tubes, not domestic production.*
2. **METALLURGY.** Copper/tin scarcity (worsened by the **loss of the Ducktown TN copper mines, Nov 1863**, ~90% of CS copper) forced Tredegar to switch from **bronze Napoleons to banded iron** field pieces by early 1864 — comparatively riskier than Northern wrought-iron (the 3-inch "almost never burst").
3. **FUZES & POWDER.** §3 above — the sharper of the war-long tactical handicaps.
4. **IMPORTS.** Blockade-run **Whitworth** and **Blakely** British rifles — superb but **single-digit to low-dozens**, hobbled by proprietary ammunition that could not be locally made to spec. A Whitworth out of ammunition is a useless precision instrument. *Bore correction: 2.75 in is the bore measured across the flats, not the projectile; the bolt weighed ~12 lb 11 oz. Produced in both breech- and muzzle-loading forms.* (The project REVIEW-QUEUE flags a 3-pdr-vs-12-pdr source-citation mismatch in HISTORICAL-DATA.md to fix.)
5. **HORSEFLESH (1864–65 phase modifier).** Starved, broken-down teams immobilized the guns; batteries were abandoned for want of horses in the Appomattox campaign. Distinct from the war-long manufacturing/quality gaps.

**Union contrast.** [Verified] The U.S. Ordnance Department (Ripley → Dyer) mass-produced and standardized around the Napoleon and the wrought-iron 3-inch Ordnance Rifle (Phoenix Iron Co., famous for near-zero bursts), with standardized Bormann fuzes, reliable friction primers, consistent powder, ~93% of national pig iron, and a deep remount/forage system. *Minor caveat: Union long-range fuzes were better than CS but not perfect; Parrotts (both sides) still burst.*

## 5. Famous employments (teaching anchors)

- **Malvern Hill (July 1, 1862)** [Verified, corrected]: massed Union guns broke piecemeal Confederate assaults; **~30,000 Confederates engaged, ~5,100–5,600 casualties** vs. ~2,100 Federal. **Correction:** Federals engaged ≈ **54,000–57,000**, not 27,000. D. H. Hill's **"It was not war, it was murder"** is a *retrospective* characterization (written ~20+ years later), not a battlefield-moment quote.
- **Antietam / Sharpsburg (Sept 17, 1862)** [Verified]: **"Artillery Hell,"** coined by Confederate artillerist **Stephen D. Lee** ("Pray that you may never see another Sharpsburg. Sharpsburg was artillery Hell," to E. P. Alexander). The phrase describes the **punishment Confederate gunners absorbed** — not Confederate dominance. Lee's battalion sat on the Dunker Church plateau (modern Visitor Center site).
- **Fredericksburg (Dec 13, 1862)** [Verified, hedged]: Maj. **John Pelham**'s horse artillery delayed the Union left, fighting on with as few as one gun for **as long as two hours** before withdrawing; Lee's report named him **"the gallant Pelham"** and credited him with "It is glorious to see such courage in one so young." *The one-gun framing is partly romanticized — he used more than one piece overall and delayed, not stopped, the advance.* Separately, **E. P. Alexander**'s sited guns swept the ground before the stone wall at Marye's Heights; his **"a chicken could not live on that field when we open on it"** was a pre-assault **assurance to Longstreet** (Alexander later doubted he actually said it) — a **defensive emplacement** advantage, not general Confederate superiority.
- **Gettysburg, July 3, 1863 — Pickett's Charge** [Verified, hedged]: Hunt **husbanded ammunition**, deliberately slowing/ceasing fire during Alexander's ~150–170-gun cannonade to reserve canister; the staged silencing also created the **illusion of guns destroyed**, which (with smoke and his own dwindling ammunition) helped lead **Alexander** to send the infantry forward. *Two nuances:* (1) the **Hunt–Hancock–McGilvery** clash was a **partial** win for husbanding — Hancock got his own II Corps batteries firing and expended most non-canister rounds; McGilvery refused chiefly because he was **not under Hancock's command** and to avoid wasting irreplaceable ammunition. (2) The repulse owed to **both** defective CS fuzes (sending shells long) **AND** Hunt's active choices — neither Southern bad luck alone nor Union triumphalism alone (Hess, *Pickett's Charge*, 2001).

**Doctrinal arc** [Inferred synthesis, Naisawald/Hess]: Malvern Hill + Fredericksburg + the repulse of Pickett's Charge teach that **massed defensive artillery firing canister, backed by infantry in prepared positions, made frontal assault across open ground lethal by mid-war.** *Hess caveat:* the rifle-musket's distinctive effect is sometimes overstated (most fighting was at short range) — but the artillery-plus-prepared-defense lethality of these specific actions is not in dispute.

## 6. Historiography & the Lost Cause counter

[Inferred framing on Verified facts.] Claims of **general Confederate artillery superiority are not supported.** Confederate successes were situational and largely **defensive** (Pelham's delay, Alexander at Marye's Heights). The Union held the material and organizational edge: superior ordnance manufacturing, more reliable ammunition and fuzes, more rifled guns, and — critically — **centralized artillery command under Hunt with a real army-level Reserve.** A teaching card framing Confederate artillery as *inherently* superior reproduces a Lost Cause distortion; the accurate frame is **Union systemic/organizational advantage vs. occasional Confederate tactical brilliance.** Equally, attributing Pickett's Charge's failure **solely** to fuze defects or bad luck understates Hunt's deliberate success — a balanced account credits **both** defective Confederate ordnance **and** Union artillery doctrine.
