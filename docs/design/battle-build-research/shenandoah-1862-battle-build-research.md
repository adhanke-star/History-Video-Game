# Shenandoah 1862 (Jackson's Valley Campaign) - Battle-Build Research Packet

**Status:** durable battle-build research packet (docs only; no data/runtime/registry change).
**Lane:** shenandoah-1862
**Verdict:** READY_FOR_SPEC

Jackson's March-June 1862 Valley Campaign is a masterpiece of OPERATIONAL MANEUVER: with roughly 17,000 men he used interior lines, the Massanutten screen, and forced marches to beat three separate Union columns (Banks, Fremont, Shields) in detail. The campaign's genius is exactly the layer this land, objective-hold engine CANNOT represent - marching, interior lines, the operational feint - so the campaign as a whole belongs in a STRATEGIC TEACHING MODULE, not a battle. But two of the six actions stand cleanly as brigade objective-hold fights: Cross Keys (Ewell defends high ground vs a larger Fremont) and Port Republic (Jackson attacks Tyler's brigades to seize The Coaling), a natural June 8-9 two-phase pair. Kernstown is a valuable "attacker historically loses" single-phase teaching build; Front Royal and McDowell are teaching-only or trap-laden.

## 2. Recommended Playable Shape

**Cross Keys + Port Republic (top candidate) - T8 two-phase, roles FLIP between phases.**
- **Phase 1 "Cross Keys - Ewell Holds the Ridge" (Jun 8, scoreWeight 1):** attacker = US ("US") Fremont; defender = CS ("CS") Ewell. Ewell's ~5,800 hold the high ground south of Mill Creek astride the Port Republic Road and repulse Fremont's ~11,500 (only part of which Fremont actually committed). Direction: CS defender holds; US fails to seize. The edge is terrain + a cautious attacker, not fudge.
- **Phase 2 "Port Republic - The Coaling" (Jun 9, scoreWeight 3, DECISIVE):** attacker = CS ("CS") Jackson; defender = US ("US") Tyler/Carroll. Jackson's ~6,000 attack Tyler's ~3,500 astride Lewiston Lane; the fight turns on seizing The Coaling (the Union artillery emplacement) via Taylor's Louisiana flank attack through the woods. Direction: CS attacker seizes The Coaling; US withdraws. This is the harder, army-saving fight, so it carries the decisive weight.
- **scoreWeights sum: 1 + 3 = 4** (two-phase convention; NOT 5). Aggregate winner CS by weighted score; the decisive phase carries the teaching.
- **Fog:** OFF for both phases. The historically probable results emerge from terrain (Cross Keys high ground; the wooded flank route to The Coaling) and concentration, NOT from fog cover for either attacker. Port Republic opened in early-morning haze, but the objective turned on the woods-covered flank move, so encode that as cover/terrain, not a fog toggle.

**Kernstown (secondary) - single-phase attacker-FAILS-to-seize (teaching centerpiece).**
- Roles: attacker = CS ("CS") Jackson; defender = US ("US") Kimball. US holds Pritchard's Hill and the stone wall along the Middle Road; Jackson's 3,800 cannot break Kimball's 8,500 and are repulsed off the field.
- Direction: US defender HOLDS; CS attacker repulsed. Casualties roughly even (CS ~718 / US ~590) - NOT a count-forcing guard. The strategic payoff (Lincoln holding McDowell's corps back to cover Washington) is an end-note TEACHING CARD, not a battle mechanic; it is operational and cannot be scored inside the fight.

## 4. OOB And Rank Traps

- **Thomas J. Jackson - RANK TRAP (the lane's #1 trap, DATE-SCOPED).** He was **Major General** throughout the March-June 1862 Valley Campaign (promoted Maj. Gen. Oct 1861 and assigned the Valley District). For EVERY battle in THIS lane he is a MAJOR GENERAL - do NOT write "Lt. Gen. Jackson" on any 1862 Valley map; the CSA lieutenant-general grade was not even authorized until the Act of Sept 18 1862. CORRECTION (adversarial pass): the prior draft said Jackson was "never a lieutenant general" and to reject "Lt. Gen. Jackson" for 1862 OR 1863 - that is WRONG. Jackson WAS promoted **lieutenant general on Oct 10 1862** (commanding the Second Corps, Army of Northern Virginia) and held that grade at Fredericksburg (Dec 1862) and Chancellorsville, where he was **wounded by his own men on the night of May 2 1863 and died of pneumonia on May 10 1863** (do not conflate the wounding date with the death date). So the rank-guard is SCENARIO-SCOPED to the Valley dates (Maj. Gen.), NOT a blanket "Jackson is never Lt. Gen." across all years - a future Chancellorsville/Fredericksburg build must label him Lt. Gen. (Corroborated: NPS/ABT bio confirm the Oct 10 1862 Lt. Gen. promotion; the repo's own `data/generals.json` Longstreet entry names Jackson "the senior officer of that grade" behind Longstreet, and `data/ratings.json` labels him "Thomas J. Jackson".)
- **Richard S. Ewell - Major General** (promoted Jan 24 1862), commanding a division under Jackson. ABT Cross Keys page confirms "Maj. Gen. Richard S. Ewell." Do not encode him as a brigadier.
- **John C. Fremont - Major General**, US, Mountain Department. ABT Cross Keys confirms "Maj. Gen. John C. Fremont."
- **Nathaniel P. Banks - Major General**, US (a political major-generalcy). Matches `data/ratings.json` (`us-banks`, "Major General"). Do not demote him.
- **James Shields - Brigadier General**, US, division commander - but **WOUNDED Mar 22 1862**, so at Kernstown (Mar 23) the field command was **Col. Nathan Kimball**. Do NOT put Shields on the Kernstown map as the tactical commander; encode Kimball as the defender-hold commander.
- **Robert H. Milroy - Brigadier General** and **Robert C. Schenck - Brigadier General** at McDowell (Schenck ranking / overall). **Edward "Allegheny" Johnson - Brigadier General** (CS, held Sitlington's Hill). If McDowell is ever built, all three are brigadiers - do not inflate.
- **Turner Ashby - RANK TRAP (date-dependent).** He was a **Colonel** at Kernstown (Mar 23) and McDowell (May 8); **promoted Brigadier General May 23 1862**, so Brig. Gen. at Front Royal (May 23), First Winchester (May 25), Cross Keys (Jun 8) - but he was **KILLED Jun 6 1862**, so he is ABSENT from Cross Keys and Port Republic. ## 6. Teaching Cards And Anti-Lost-Cause Framing

- **"A Victorious Defeat" (Kernstown).** Jackson LOST the tactical fight at Kernstown - repulsed by a force he badly underestimated - yet the defeat frightened Washington into holding Banks in the Valley and pulling McDowell's corps off the march to Richmond. Teach the whole point: a battlefield loss that served the strategy. NEVER let the build fudge Kernstown into a Jackson win; the honesty IS the lesson.
- **"The Foot Cavalry - Evidenced, Not Deified."** Jackson's men earned the "foot cavalry" name by real, punishing marches (roughly 350-650 miles over the campaign, e.g. the ~30+ mile days that let him strike Front Royal and Winchester before Banks could react). Present the MILES, the straggling, and the exhaustion - not a mythic aura. Flag the historiographic problem directly: the Lost Cause turned Jackson into a saintly military genius; the game should teach a demanding, secretive, sometimes harsh professional whose method was interior lines and speed, not divine favor.
- **"Three Armies, One Valley."** The map card that carries the operational teaching the battle engine cannot: how Jackson used the Massanutten Mountain to screen his moves and beat Banks, Fremont, and Shields separately because they never combined. This is where the campaign's meaning lives.
- **"Two Marylands" (Front Royal).** The 1st Maryland (US) surrendered largely to the 1st Maryland (CS) - the war as civil war, brother against brother, same state and same regimental number. Teaching card; the mass surrender is treated with dignity (soldiers taken prisoner in a fair fight), never gamified as a scored capture objective.

## 7. D74 No-Fudge Risks

- **The Kernstown temptation:** hard-coding a Jackson win, or forcing his repulse with a per-battle `lossMult`/`killMult`. FORBIDDEN both ways. The historical result (Jackson REPULSED) must emerge from the true 3,800-vs-8,500 disadvantage, the Union artillery on Pritchard's Hill, and Kimball's defender-hold posture. Do not invert history to flatter Jackson, and do not script his loss - encode the true inputs and let the universal model repulse him.
- **The McDowell trap (why it is NOT a build):** the CS WON the field but lost MORE men (~532 vs ~259) because Milroy attacked uphill and Johnson counter-pressed. A casualty-DIRECTION guard keyed to "the winner bleeds less" would be FALSE here. Rather than tune around it, keep McDowell teaching-only; no combat params exist to fudge.
- **The Port Republic direction subtlety:** US (the DEFENDER, 3,500) lost MORE than CS (1,002 vs 816). That is a legitimate raw "US losses > CS losses" direction guard, but note it is NOT an "attacker bleeds more" pattern - the CS attacker took The Coaling relatively cheaply. Keep the guard as a simple side-total direction, never a count-forcing gate.
- **The Cross Keys temptation:** hard-coding "Ewell holds." FORBIDDEN. Encode the high ground south of Mill Creek, the defender posture, and Fremont's limited commitment; the hold emerges. Fremont historically pressed only part of his 11,500 - represent that as his committed OOB, not as a firepower nerf.
