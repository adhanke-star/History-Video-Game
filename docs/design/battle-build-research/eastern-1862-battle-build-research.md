## 2. Recommended Playable Shape

**Gaines' Mill (top candidate) - single-phase defender-hold, Malvern-Hill-inverse pattern (RECOMMENDED primary).**
- Roles: attacker = CS ("CS"), defender = US ("US"). US is the objective-holder on the Watt House plateau behind Boatswain's Swamp.
- Objective: US holds the Watt House plateau / Boatswain's Swamp line against Lee's afternoon-into-evening assaults; CS must seize the plateau. Historical lean: CS seizes (the line breaks at dusk).
- Engine-grain fit: objective-hold favors bodies-on-objective + cover. Give US the swamp/wooded-slope cover + strong artillery (Porter fought a textbook defensive), and give CS the true ~57k vs ~34k strength edge plus the concentrated evening mass. The historic CS breakthrough then EMERGES from numbers overcoming cover - no per-battle switch. This is Malvern Hill run in reverse: same universal model, opposite result because the inputs (strength ratio, terrain, massed vs open guns) are opposite.
- Fog OFF: this was a see-the-enemy frontal fight where the defender's edge was the swamp and slope, not concealment; leaving fog off keeps the CS attacker from being artificially aided and makes the win rest on honest mass.
- **Optional T8 two-phase enhancement** (teaches the piecemeal-vs-coordinated lesson):
  - Phase 1 "The Piecemeal Afternoon Assaults" (scoreWeight 1): A.P. Hill's and then Longstreet's divisions go in unevenly and are bloodily repulsed; US defender-hold. Historical lean US.
  - Phase 2 "The Evening Grand Assault - Hood's Breakthrough" (scoreWeight 3, DECISIVE): the coordinated ~32k-man assault (Whiting's division, Hood's Texas Brigade spearhead) breaks the plateau. CS attacker-seize; concentration + strength edge. **Weights sum to 4 (1 + 3 DECISIVE).** Aggregate winner CS via the decisive phase.

**Second Bull Run (second candidate) - T8 three-phase, roles switch by phase.**
- Phase 0 "August 29 - Jackson's Line at the Unfinished Railroad" (scoreWeight 1): US attacker (Pope) hammers Jackson's concealed line on Stony Ridge along the unfinished railroad; repulsed/inconclusive. attacker="US" / defender="CS".
- Phase 1 "August 30 - Porter's Assault on the Deep Cut" (scoreWeight 1): US attacker; the Deep Cut assault is shattered by Jackson's line + S.D. Lee's massed artillery. attacker="US" / defender="CS".
- Phase 2 "August 30 - Longstreet's Wing: Chinn Ridge to Henry Hill" (scoreWeight 3, DECISIVE): Longstreet's ~28k-30k wing counterattacks the exposed Union left; attacker="CS" / defender="US". The CS numerical mass on the open left + timing sweep the field. **Weights sum to 5 (1 + 1 + 3 DECISIVE) - three-phase convention.** (Alternative two-phase: Aug-30 only, Deep Cut repulse weight 1 + Longstreet's wing weight 3 DECISIVE = sum 4.)
- Aggregate: CS victory via decisive Phase 2. Fog OFF; the CS edge is Longstreet's concealed arrival + the Union left being in the air, encoded as OOB position + timing, not a fog aid.

## 4. OOB And Rank Traps

- **James Longstreet - RANK TRAP (source-label conflict).** At **Second Bull Run (Aug 28-30 1862) he was a Major General commanding a "wing"** (~28k-30k), NOT a lieutenant general and NOT "First Corps." His Lt. Gen. promotion was **Oct 9 1862**, six weeks AFTER the battle; the CSA lieutenant-general grade did not exist before Oct 1862 and the corps were not formalized until Nov 1862. The **ABT Second Manassas page labels him "Lt. Gen." - that is anachronistic and WRONG for the battle date.** Repo `generals.json` and NPS ("Longstreet's wing") confirm. SAFER CHOICE: encode "Major General, commanding a wing."
- **A.P. Hill - RANK TRAP (source-label conflict).** At Beaver Dam Creek and Gaines' Mill he was a **Major General** commanding the Light Division (promoted MG **May 26 1862**, a month before the Seven Days). The **ABT Beaver Dam Creek page labels him "Brig. Gen." - anachronistically LOW and WRONG.** SAFER CHOICE: Maj. Gen. (Do not repeat a stray secondary-source "Lt. Gen. A.P. Hill" reading either - no such grade existed in June 1862.)
- **Fitz John Porter - RANK TRAP (date-dependent).** Promoted **Major General of Volunteers July 4 1862**. So at **Beaver Dam Creek (Jun 26) and Gaines' Mill (Jun 27) he was a Brigadier General**; at **Second Bull Run (Aug 28-30) he was a Major General**. Encode the grade that matches the battle date; ABT labels both correctly on their respective pages.
- **John Bell Hood - RANK TRAP.** At Gaines' Mill he was a **Brigadier General commanding the Texas Brigade** (1st/4th/5th TX, 18th GA, Hampton's SC Legion) within Whiting's division. Do NOT backdate his Major General grade (Oct 10 1862). His breakthrough is teaching flavor, not a scripted event.
- **Thomas J. "Stonewall" Jackson - Major General** throughout this lane (MG Oct 7 1861; Lt. Gen. Oct 10 1862). At Second Bull Run he commanded a "wing," not a corps.
- **Robert E. Lee - General**, commanding the Army of Northern Virginia (took field command Jun 1 1862). **D.H. Hill - Major General** (MG Mar 26 1862) at the Seven Days. **George B. McClellan - Major General**, Army of the Potomac (overall US at the Seven Days but SOUTH of the Chickahominy at Gaines' Mill - Porter ran the field; do not place McClellan on the Gaines' Mill map). **John Pope - Major General**, Army of Virginia. **George A. McCall - Brigadier General**, Pennsylvania Reserves division (the unit smashed at both Beaver Dam Creek and Glendale).
- **V Corps designation trap:** Porter's "Fifth Provisional Army Corps" was created by McClellan's field order **May 18 1862** and formalized by the War Department in July 1862 - so "V Corps" in late June is a provisional designation; note it as provisional if the label matters.

## 6. Teaching Cards And Anti-Lost-Cause Framing

- **"The Seven Days: Costly Even in Victory."** Gaines' Mill was Lee's clearest tactical win of the campaign, yet the CS took MORE casualties (killed + wounded) than the US it drove off the field (~7,900 CS vs ~6,800 US, with the Union edge in men captured). Teach that Lee's offensive drove McClellan away from Richmond at a manpower cost the Confederacy could less afford - a strategic reversal bought with irreplaceable blood, not a costless triumph. Reject the "Lee's masterpiece" gloss that hides the butcher's bill.
- **"Fitz John Porter Was the Scapegoat, Not the Culprit."** At Second Bull Run, Pope blamed Porter's failure to attack Longstreet's wing on Aug 29 for the defeat; Porter was court-martialed and cashiered in 1863, then exonerated and reinstated (1879/1886) after boards confirmed he had faced Longstreet's ARRIVING wing, not an open flank - his caution was militarily sound. This is Union-on-Union scapegoating adjacent to Lost-Cause myth-making; teach it as a warning about blaming a subordinate for a commander's own misreading. Do NOT encode a scripted "Porter fails to attack" event - that would launder the smear into a mechanic.
- **"Command Failure, Not Enemy Genius: Glendale."** The Confederate near-victory that got away is a story of Huger's and Jackson's non-arrival - a coordination failure, not Union prowess. Teach it as the recurring Seven Days pattern (piecemeal attacks) rather than as fate.
- **"A.P. Hill's Piecemeal Assaults."** Both Mechanicsville and the first hours of Gaines' Mill show Hill attacking before support was up and paying in blood - the case study for why the optional Gaines' Mill two-phase separates piecemeal from coordinated assault.

The direction guards must be split: (a) CS seizes the objective (outcome direction), and (b) CS killed+wounded >= US killed+wounded (the attacker paid in blood). Never a count-forcing gate on either.

FORBIDDEN and the reason Glendale is low-priority: the non-arrival is an off-map operational fact, not a combat parameter. If built, model only the forces actually present (Longstreet + A.P.
