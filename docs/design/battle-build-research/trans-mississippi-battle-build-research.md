# Trans-Mississippi Theater - Battle-Build Research Packet

**Status:** D328-adjacent durable battle-build research packet (docs only; no data/runtime/registry change).
**Lane:** trans-mississippi
**Verdict:** READY_FOR_SPEC

The Trans-Mississippi is where the war's edges get complicated: a state-force army (the Missouri State Guard) that was never Confederate, five Native nations pulled into the war on both sides, a New Mexico invasion decided by a burned wagon train, and a Red River campaign that half-belongs to a naval engine this project does not have. Exactly one action fits the land brigade objective-hold engine cleanly: Pea Ridge / Elkhorn Tavern (Mar 7-8 1862), a two-day fight that maps onto a T8 two-phase scenario. Wilson's Creek is a defensible second candidate. Glorieta Pass and the Red River river-war are teaching-only. A live design fork (D183's bar on Native OOB extrapolation) must be resolved by Aaron before any Leetown-sector Native units are encoded - this packet honors that bar and does NOT override it.

## 2. Recommended Playable Shape

**Pea Ridge / Elkhorn Tavern (top candidate) - T8 two-phase, roles flip between phases.**
- Phase 1 "Elkhorn Tavern, March 7" (scoreWeight 1): attacker = CS ("CS"), defender = US ("US"). Van Dorn's army, having marched the Bentonville Detour around Curtis's rear via the Telegraph Road, attacks south into the Union rear and drives Carr's division back, seizing Elkhorn Tavern by nightfall. Historical lean: CS seizes. Fog ON for this phase - the whole CS gambit was a night-march surprise appearing in the Union rear; fog models that the defender did not see the envelopment forming.
- Phase 2 "Curtis's Counterattack, March 8" (scoreWeight 3, DECISIVE): attacker = US ("US"), defender = CS ("CS") holding Elkhorn Tavern / Pea Ridge. Curtis re-forms his line facing north, masses artillery, and drives Van Dorn - by now critically low on ammunition and rations, his wagons left behind on the detour, and his right wing leaderless after McCulloch's death the day before - off the ridge. Historical lean: US seizes decisively. Fog OFF (a methodical daylight artillery-backed assault the defender could see coming). The aggregate winner is US because the DECISIVE phase is US and the true inputs (Union ammunition/supply intact vs. a CS army that outran its own trains) carry it.
- **scoreWeight sum = 4** (1 + 3 DECISIVE), the two-phase convention. NOT 5 (that is the three-phase convention).
- Engine-grain fit: objective-hold favors bodies-on-the-objective + cover; give the Day-2 US side intact supply/ammo and massed guns and the historic result emerges. The CS numerical edge (16,000 vs 10,500) is real but is offset in P2 by the supply collapse - encode the collapse as accurate low ammunition/fatigue inputs, NEVER as a casualty multiplier.
- **D183 DESIGN FORK (surface to Aaron; do not silently override) - TWO-TIER GATE:** D183's load-bearing source note reads verbatim: "this is not a license to create a playable Trans-Mississippi battle, OOB, unit roster, or Confederate-diversity claim. **M8 battle-build still needs Aaron go/no-go.**" Read it exactly: the gate is TWO tiers, not one. **Tier 1 (whole-lane):** the ENTIRE Trans-Mississippi M8 battle-build - including the Native-free Elkhorn Tavern axis recommended here - needs Aaron's explicit M8 go/no-go before ANY spec is written. This packet does NOT clear that gate; it only assembles the evidence for it. **Tier 2 (Native-OOB, higher bar):** the historic Day-1 morning fight at **Leetown** - where Ben McCulloch was killed and where Albert Pike's Indian Brigade and Stand Watie's Cherokees fought and captured Union guns - is the sector that would additionally require encoding Native units as a playable OOB, which D183/D178 **expressly BARRED from playable-battle/OOB extrapolation**. The recommended build **centers on the Elkhorn Tavern axis (Price's Missourians + Van Dorn vs Curtis's right), which had NO Native units**, so it honors the Tier-2 Native bar with Leetown/Pike/Watie kept as citation-grade teaching cards only. But it still sits behind the Tier-1 whole-lane M8 gate - do not read "no Native units" as "free to build." Both tiers are Aaron decisions; this packet resolves neither.

**Wilson's Creek (second candidate) - single-phase defender-hold.**
- Roles: the cleaner encoding is US defender-hold on Bloody Hill, CS attacker (Price's Missouri State Guard + McCulloch's Confederates) must seize. Lyon's dawn attack has already carried the hill; the modeled fight is the three CS counterattacks. Historically the US repulsed all three but withdrew from exhaustion and low ammunition after Lyon was killed - so lean the phase near-even and let the CS strength edge (11,000 vs 5,400) plus US ammunition exhaustion produce the historic CS-victory-by-withdrawal without scripting it. Fog OFF. Caution: this is a subtle outcome (the loser held the ground tactically) - a near-50/50 hold is bistable; document the intended lean.

## 4. OOB And Rank Traps

- **Nathaniel Lyon - RANK TRAP RESOLVED.** **Brigadier General of U.S. Volunteers** at Wilson's Creek (commissioned May 17 1861, a jump from Captain, 2nd U.S. Infantry), KILLED on Bloody Hill Aug 10 1861. ABT confirms "Brigadier General Nathaniel Lyon (killed during battle)." Do not leave him a captain; do not inflate to major general.
- **Sterling Price - THE STATE-FORCE TRAP (load-bearing).** At Wilson's Creek he was **Major General of the MISSOURI STATE GUARD - a state force, NOT the Confederate Army.** ABT's Wilson's Creek page lists "Maj. Gen. Sterling Price" on the Confederate side; the Missouri-State-Guard status of his command (as distinct from Confederate service) is well-established history, not a verbatim parenthetical I could re-confirm on the ABT summary in this pass - state it as fact, do not stamp it as an exact ABT quote. His men were not Confederate soldiers in Aug 1861; the Guard fought alongside McCulloch's Confederates but under Missouri's secessionist state authority. This distinction is a teaching point, not a footnote. (Price accepted a Confederate major-generalcy later, in 1862.)
- **Ben McCulloch - Brigadier General, C.S.A.** at both Wilson's Creek (Aug 1861) and Pea Ridge, where he was **KILLED at Leetown, Mar 7 1862** (ABT-confirmed). His death leaderless-ing the CS right is a real Day-2 input, not a scripted event.
- **Earl Van Dorn - Major General, C.S.A.**, overall CS commander at Pea Ridge (ABT-confirmed).
- **Samuel R. Curtis - RANK TRAP.** **Brigadier General** at Pea Ridge (ABT-confirmed "Brigadier General Samuel R. Curtis"); promoted **Major General** on March 21 1862 FOR the victory. Do not backdate the major-generalcy onto the battle OOB.
- **Franz Sigel - CONFLICT, RECORD IT.** ABT's Wilson's Creek page calls him **"Colonel Franz Sigel"** (colonel of the 3rd Missouri); his brigadier-general-of-volunteers appointment is dated Aug 7 1861, three days before the battle. Sources split on whether to style him Colonel or Brig. Gen. on Aug 10. Safer choice: "Colonel (brigadier-general appointment Aug 7 1861)" at Wilson's Creek. At **Pea Ridge (Mar 1862) he was firmly Brigadier General**, commanding two divisions - use Brig. Gen. there. (Repo ratings.json labels him Major General as a career label; that is his eventual grade, not his rank at these battles.)
- **Albert Pike - Brigadier General, C.S.A.**, commanding the Indian Brigade (~900 men), Leetown sector (ABT-confirmed).
- **Stand Watie - RANK TRAP, DO NOT BACKDATE.** **COLONEL** at Pea Ridge, commanding a Cherokee Mounted Rifles regiment (ABT-confirmed "Colonel"). He was NOT promoted **Brigadier General until 1864**; he was the last CS general to surrender (June 1865) - but that rank belongs to 1864-65, not to March 1862. **REGIMENT-NUMBER CONFLICT:** the ABT article says Watie led the "2nd Cherokee Mounted Rifles"; other scholarship (Shea and Hess, *Pea Ridge*) assigns Watie the 1st and John Drew the 2nd, or vice versa - the designations are genuinely muddled in the records. Safer choice: "Col. Stand Watie, Cherokee Mounted Rifles" without pinning the number until a second scholarly source settles it. **James M. McIntosh - Brigadier General, C.S.A.**, killed at Leetown the same day (ABT-confirmed he captured Elbert's guns; his death is separately documented).
- **Henry Hopkins Sibley - Brigadier General, C.S.A.**, overall Confederate campaign commander in New Mexico (Glorieta), who **remained in Albuquerque during the battle** (ABT-confirmed). Field command fell to **Lt. Col. William R. Scurry** and **Maj. Charles Pyron**. Union: **Col. John P. Slough**, **Maj. John M. Chivington**.
- **Nathaniel P. Banks - Major General** (Red River, 1864). **Richard Taylor - Major General, C.S.A.** (Red River). Both confirmed in the Mansfield search; Taylor won Mansfield, Banks retreated.

## 6. Teaching Cards And Anti-Lost-Cause Framing

- **"A State Army, Not a Confederate One."** Price's Missouri State Guard was a state secessionist force, not the Confederate Army - Missouri never legally seceded, and the war in the state was a civil war within a border state. Teach the State Guard / Confederate distinction rather than flattening Wilson's Creek into "Confederates won."
- **"Native Nations on Both Sides - Agency, Not Exotica."** Pike's alliances were won by exploiting sovereignty fears after the U.S. Army abandoned Indian Territory's forts (May 1861), AND because Five Tribes slaveholding elites (Watie's planter faction) were protecting human property - this does NOT make the Confederacy "diverse." Native loyalties split violently (Cherokee and Creek fought on both sides; the slaveholder Opothleyahola led Unionist refugees to Kansas). Present Cherokee, Creek, Seminole, Choctaw, and Chickasaw participants as historical actors with their own politics, never as spectacle. (This card reproduces the D183 `native-nations` thread's exact framing - reuse it, do not re-invent it.)
- **"The Leetown Scalping and Its Weaponization."** From sources: a few of Watie's men scalped at least eight of Trimble's Iowa cavalrymen and mutilated others at Leetown; Pike was horrified and issued orders against it; the Northern press then pilloried Pike and "excoriated his Indian troops," and a Union officer wished extermination - the ABT article notes the perpetrators were never identified. Teach BOTH the atrocity AND its propaganda use (the North used it to justify anti-Native hatred). The atrocity is teaching content, never a scored objective. Do not launder it as either Native savagery OR Union innocence.
- **"Glorieta and Sand Creek - the Same Man."** IF Glorieta is mentioned, Maj. John Chivington is named as the Union flanking officer who burned the wagons - and named, in the same breath and as a teaching card, as the man who two years later commanded the **Sand Creek Massacre (Nov 1864)** of Cheyenne and Arapaho. Never a heroic figure. (The massacre is teaching-only; it is not, and cannot be, a scenario.)
- **"The Wagon Train, Not the Battle."** Glorieta teaches that logistics, not tactical valor, decided the West - a corrective to romantic battle narratives.
