## 2. Chapter I — Fort Pillow (April 12, 1864)

### 2.1 The record in brief
Confederate cavalry under Maj. Gen. Nathan Bedford Forrest — McCulloch's brigade of Chalmers's division plus Bell's brigade of Buford's division, both under Brig. Gen. James R. Chalmers's field command for the operation (Forrest's own OR report) — stormed the Mississippi River fort at the mouth of Coal Creek. The garrison of roughly 557-605 engaged was about half Black: four companies of the 6th U.S. Colored Heavy Artillery, a section of Battery D, 2nd U.S. Colored Light Artillery, and Bradford's Battalion, 13th Tennessee Cavalry (white Unionists). Maj. Lionel F. Booth was killed by a sharpshooter in the morning; Maj. William F. Bradford took command and refused Forrest's mid-afternoon surrender demand; the storm carried the works in minutes. What followed is one of the war's best-documented atrocities: surrendering soldiers, disproportionately Black, were shot down. The Joint Committee on the Conduct of the War concluded the killings were "the results of a policy deliberately decided upon"; Confederate Sgt. Achilles Clark's private letter two days later described the slaughter; Forrest's own hasty April 15 dispatch reported the river "dyed with the blood of the slaughtered for 200 yards." Modern scholarship (Castel, Ward, Fuchs, Cimprich) is settled that a massacre of surrendered men occurred; Forrest's precise culpability — ordered, condoned, or failed to halt — remains genuinely debated, and the packet teaches that debate honestly without softening the fact.

### 2.2 Recommended playable shape
Single-phase, attacker = CS, defender = US, historical lean = CS seizes. Objective: the inner fort on the bluff. True inputs, no fudge:
- CS strength 1,500-2,500 (a genuine source range: Cimprich/Tennessee Encyclopedia 1,500; NPS battle-summary database 2,500; the spec pass pins one figure with its source note and ships the rest of the range as an Inferred note).
- US garrison 557-605 engaged (NPS 557 = 295 white + 262 USCT; the 585-605 band is the present-for-duty accounting; Forrest's own 700 is an interested-party outlier — never encode it).
- Terrain does the historical work: ravines and the barracks rows (~150 yards south, captured about 11 a.m.) give the attacker covered approaches; knolls give sharpshooter positions (the Booth kill); the bluff and the river bound the defense. The specific mechanism that the defenders' artillery could not depress enough to bear on close attackers is a SINGLE-FAMILY claim (Wikipedia) and ships Inferred pending a second family (candidate resolver: JCCW testimony). Encoded as cover/approach geometry, never as a combat multiplier.
- Timing: Forrest arrived about 10 a.m.; truce demand about 3:30 p.m. with 20 minutes granted; the final storm breached in about five minutes. A single-phase hold-to-limit shape with a late-battle assault window models this honestly.
- The USS New Era: the gunboat's failure to intervene is taught; the claim that it "did not fire a single shot" is single-source (Wikipedia) and ships Inferred unless the spec pass finds a second family; its post-battle truce role (with Silver Cloud and Platte Valley, receiving wounded) is primary-confirmed (JCCW).
**Both sides playable (D455 R3/R5):** as US, hold the works against the storm; as CS, carry them. Either way the battle ENDS at the works: the post-surrender choice belongs to the no-quarter machinery (§2.5), never to battlefield scoring.

### 2.3 OOB and rank traps
- **Forrest — Maj. Gen.** on Apr 12 1864 (promoted Dec 1863). Never Brig. Gen., never Lt. Gen. here.
- **Chalmers — Brig. Gen.**, field command of the assault force.
- **Brigade organization trap (Forrest's own OR report, load-bearing):** McCulloch's brigade belonged to Chalmers's division; Bell's brigade belonged to BUFORD's division and was ATTACHED to Chalmers for this operation. Wikipedia's flat "Chalmers's Division: McCulloch's + Bell's" is a simplification — encode the attachment honestly. Bell held the CS right anchored on Coal Creek; McCulloch the left toward the river.
- **Booth — Major**, killed by sharpshooter fire in the morning; **Bradford — Major**, succeeded him. Neither is ever a colonel.
- Garrison units exactly: 6th U.S. Colored Heavy Artillery (four companies), one section Battery D 2nd U.S. Colored Light Artillery, 13th Tennessee Cavalry (Bradford's Battalion). No other Union formation was present.
- **6th USCHA designation trap (adversarial-pass find):** the garrison regiment carried the name "6th U.S. Colored Heavy Artillery" ONLY from March 11 to April 26 1864 — formerly the 1st Alabama Siege Artillery (African Descent), redesignated the 7th USCHA two weeks after the battle. "6th USCHA" is therefore CORRECT for the battle date — but the modern NPS unit-database page titled "6th USCHA" (battleUnitCode UUS0006RAH0C) describes a DIFFERENT regiment (the ex-2nd Mississippi Heavy Artillery) and must never be cited as the Fort Pillow unit.

## 3. Chapter II — The Crater (July 30, 1864)

### 3.1 The record in brief
Lt. Col. Henry Pleasants's 48th Pennsylvania dug a 511-foot approach shaft (the ~586-ft figure includes the lateral magazine galleries) under Elliott's Salient and fired 8,000 lbs of powder at 4:44 a.m., killing about 278 South Carolinians (the 352 figure is the ABT article's outlier) and opening a breach. Meade — with Grant's concurrence, on July 29 — had ordered Burnside NOT to lead with Ferrero's trained 4th Division (USCT); Ledlie's division, chosen by lot, led instead, and Ledlie and Ferrero sheltered in a bomb-proof sharing rum while their men piled into the crater bowl. Mahone's division counterattacked in waves (Weisiger's Virginians with part of Wright's Georgians about 9 a.m., the rest of Wright's about 11 a.m., Sanders's Alabamians about 1-1:30 p.m.) and retook the salient. USCT soldiers attempting to surrender were killed in numbers no source pins precisely — the record is qualitative (Confederate letters: "all would have been killed had it not ben for Gen. Mahone"; Pegram's chilling approval) plus Suderow's disproportionate killed-to-wounded analysis. CAUTION (adversarial-pass find): the McClellan letter must never function as Mahone-exculpation — the "Mahone the humane rescuer" reading is historiographically contested, and any teaching card quoting it pairs it with the Suderow ratio evidence, never standalone. Union casualties 3,798 (504/1,881/1,413), the 4th Division's ~1,327 including ~450 captured; Confederate ~1,491. Grant: "the saddest affair I have witnessed in this war," and to the Joint Committee: had the Black division led, "I believe... it would have been a success." The court of inquiry censured Burnside, Ledlie, Ferrero, Willcox, and Col. Bliss; the 1865 Joint Committee instead blamed Meade's substitution — teach both findings.

### 3.2 Recommended playable shape
Single-phase, attacker = US, defender = CS, historical lean = CS holds. Objective: Cemetery Hill beyond the breach (~500 yards). True inputs: the blast opens the assault with the CS first line suppressed (the ~15-minute silence is a fair fog/setup framing, not a scripted buff); the crater bowl encodes as terrain that TRAPS — steep interior with no exit cover — never as attacker cover toward the objective; Wright's battery north and Davidson's battery south encode as fielded guns with real enfilade geometry; Mahone's waves are the reinforcement schedule at their sourced hours (the three-wave shape is multi-family; the specific part-of-Wright-at-9/rest-at-11 sub-split is the beyondthecrater specialist family alone and ships Inferred). Ferrero's division fields honestly as the late-committed formation (Thomas's 2nd Brigade historically swung right into a separate trench lane — a distinct approach axis worth encoding). Leadership dysfunction (Ledlie/Ferrero absent) is teaching content, and at most the EXISTING leader-system inputs — never a new mechanic. The historical repulse must be allowed to happen at a fair baseline; the teaching is why it failed: the substitution, the hesitation, the bowl.

## 4. Chapter III — Olustee / Ocean Pond (February 20, 1864)

### 4.1 The record in brief
Brig. Gen. Truman Seymour advanced ~5,500 men west from Jacksonville — against standing instructions and toward the Florida, Atlantic & Gulf Central Railroad — and met Brig. Gen. Joseph Finegan's ~5,000 Confederates (ABT and Wikipedia both give 5,000 — 4,600 infantry, under 600 cavalry, 12 guns; the ~5,400 variant appears only on battleofolustee.org and ships as that named single family) — Colquitt's and Harrison's commands — near Olustee Station. Finegan had entrenched east of the station, but the battle was fought FORWARD of the works in open longleaf-pine flatwoods on a corridor of dry ground between Ocean Pond and the southern swamps. Seymour committed piecemeal: the 7th New Hampshire broke; the green 8th USCT — under Col. Charles W. Fribley, killed on the field, his body never recovered — was shattered losing 310 of fewer than 600 (ABT breakdown 49/188/73); the line collapsed by dusk. The 54th Massachusetts, the 35th USCT (the 1st North Carolina Colored Volunteers, redesignated Feb 8 1864; Lt. Col. William N. Reed mortally wounded), and the 7th Connecticut fought the rearguard that saved the column — and afterward the 54th manually hauled a disabled train of wounded about three miles by rope (about ten miles total with horses, over 42 hours) toward Jacksonville — VERIFIED by direct fetch in the adversarial pass, no longer a flagged story. Union losses 1,861 of ~5,500 (~34%, among the worst Union percentage losses of the war); Confederate 946. Black wounded left on the field were killed in the aftermath — the scale is genuinely contested (ABT's own two pages disagree: "killed most" versus "killed a few"; Penniman's eyewitness reminiscence describes pervasive killing; ~70 Black prisoners reached Andersonville) — and the packet presents that contest honestly while naming the killings plainly. Lincoln's Florida project (John Hay's loyalty-oath mission, the Ten-Percent Plan, the 1864 electoral calculus) died with the defeat.

### 4.2 Recommended playable shape
Single-phase meeting engagement, attacker = CS, defender = US, historical lean = CS breaks the line (a Union DEFEAT — the engine must be allowed to produce it). Objective: the Union line's junction on the wagon road/railroad axis. True inputs: the corridor terrain (Ocean Pond north, swamps south) funnels both sides — no works at the fight line (Finegan came forward); Union piecemeal arrival is the reinforcement schedule (7th NH and 8th USCT early and exposed; Barton's New Yorkers; Montgomery's brigade late as the rearguard force); CS commitment likewise staged (Colquitt forward, Harrison extending, the reserves Finegan fed in). The 8th USCT's greenness is its true experience input, never a penalty multiplier. The rearguard is the teaching heart: valor in defeat, per the family law and the D382 women-arc precedent that dignity does not require victory.

## 6. Teaching Cards And Anti-Lost-Cause Framing (family)

1. **The massacre named plainly (Fort Pillow).** A massacre of surrendering soldiers, disproportionately Black, is the established record — the JCCW's conclusion, Clark's Confederate letter, and Forrest's own river-dyed dispatch converge from three hostile directions. Forrest's precise culpability is genuinely debated (ordered vs condoned vs failed to halt); the debate is taught AS a debate, and no framing softens the fact. Refuses every Lost-Cause deflection ("garrison never surrendered", "drunk garrison") by teaching what the primary record actually supports.
2. **"Remember Fort Pillow."** The massacre's consequence ran through the war: a USCT battle cry, the cartel's collapse over equal treatment (the shipped prisoner-exchange reprisal chain), General Order No. 252 — the game's existing committed corpus carries all three; the scenarios compose them.
3. **The Crater injustice, both layers.** Layer one: the substitution — a trained USCT division pulled the day before over political optics, an unprepared division led by lot, commanders in a rum-stocked bomb-proof; the court censured the generals, the Joint Committee blamed the substitution, and Grant testified the Black division would have carried it. Layer two: the massacre of surrendering USCT by counterattacking Confederates, documented in their own letters. Both are taught; neither is played.
4. **Valor in defeat (Olustee).** The 8th USCT's green regiments were shattered doing what was asked; the 54th Massachusetts and 35th USCT saved the column and then hauled the wounded out by hand. Dignity does not require victory, and the game must not invent one.
5. **The aftermath record, honestly contested (Olustee).** Confederate letters document the killing of Black wounded; the scale is contested in the sources — including inside a single institution's own pages — and ~70 Black prisoners reached Andersonville. Teaching the contest IS the anti-Lost-Cause move: the record's existence is beyond dispute, its arithmetic is not, and the game never pretends to precision the sources lack.
6. **Why these three are one family.** The Confederate policy climate (Davis's December 1862 proclamation, the May 1863 congressional act) made every USCT engagement carry execution-or-enslavement stakes — Fort Pillow, the Crater, and Olustee are the documented consequence. The family teaches the policy chain once, sourced, and each battle composes it.
