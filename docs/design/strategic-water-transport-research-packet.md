## 2. Purpose

This packet supplies dated historical evidence for a future territory-scale conquest law to reason about strategic water reach. It asks which river, sound, harbor, and coastwise links connected which candidate endpoints between 1861 and 1865; where landings, ports, forts, batteries, bars, bends, canals, water level, capture, closure, reopening, and intermodal transfer changed that reach; and which army-transfer cases can test a later design law.

It does not select territories or mechanics. A water node, edge, chokepoint, or case below is a research candidate, not a promised gameplay object. “Verified” describes the narrow historical claim in its row; it never converts that claim into guaranteed, year-round, unlimited, or safe movement.

## 3. Scope

- **Dates:** 1861-1865, with opening-, mid-, and late-war conditions separated where the evidence permits.
- **Western and border feeders:** the Ohio only where it materially fed the war corridors; the Mississippi, Tennessee, Cumberland, lower Arkansas/White cutoff, and Red River.
- **Eastern systems:** Hampton Roads, the Chesapeake/James/Potomac approaches, the York River, and the North Carolina sounds/Neuse approach.
- **Coastwise systems:** Atlantic and Gulf military approaches, ports, sound bases, landings, and transfers for which actual passage or a bounded limitation is sourced.
- **Evidence grain:** candidate territory-scale endpoints, links, passage limits, and control changes. Hydrographic geometry, vessel-by-vessel movement, and harbor engineering detail are deferred.
- **Modes kept distinct:** navigable water, an actual military passage, port control, transport availability, blockade status, and rail-water interchange.
- **Claim status:** `Verified` requires at least two independent non-tertiary source families; `Inferred` marks one credible family, a bounded negative finding, or an unresolved connection; `Disputed` requires a credible conflict rather than ordinary uncertainty.

## 4. Explicit exclusions

- No final territory list, boundary, adjacency graph, hex, or map projection.
- No movement points, turn scale, capacities, throughput, probabilities, repair or reopening times, ownership rules, bonuses, penalties, combat effects, objective rewards, or forced outcomes.
- No ship-vs-ship or fleet tactical engine, ship classes or statistics for gameplay, fleet construction, naval AI, commerce-raider system, or foreign procurement.
- No rewrite of blockade economics, runner/capture rates, port-count formulas, recognition, cotton revenue, imports, diplomacy, or save/UI behavior.
- No second D159 logistics system and no replacement for the Western Theater readout.
- No assertion that a river drawn on a period map was navigable in every season, by every vessel, in both directions, or under enemy fire.
- No conversion of a fort, battery, mine, obstruction, bar, or bend into a combat formula.
- No tactical re-authorship of Fort Donelson, Fort Henry, Hampton Roads, Island No. 10, Vicksburg, or Mobile Bay. Their strategic passage evidence is cross-referenced only.
- No Fort Pillow spectacle, scored atrocity treatment, or D74 output gate.
- No runtime, data, probe, save, manifest, package, asset, schema, suite, generated-output, or frozen-base change.

## 6. Research method and source-independence law

1. Begin with actual military passage, transfer, or a documented failure/limit. Use maps to locate or corroborate; never let a map alone prove navigability or military use.
2. Separate six questions in every row: water present, passage made, endpoint controlled, transports available, blockade state, and transfer ashore. Evidence for one does not answer the others.
3. Treat two pages repeating one underlying report as one family. NHHC narrative pages and NHHC vessel histories are not counted as two independent families merely because their URLs differ; the same rule applies within NPS.
4. `Verified` requires two independent non-tertiary families that support the row's narrow claim. A primary map plus an institutional narrative can verify a route actually represented and used only when the narrative supplies the use; the map contributes no capacity claim.
5. Use exact dates only where the source supplies them. Otherwise use bounded intervals such as `opening 1862`, `after July 1863`, or `January 1865`.
6. Record direction, draft, water-level, seasonal, bar, bend, obstruction, mine, battery, canal, or landing limits when sourced. Silence is not permission to invent a limitation or erase one.
7. Treat capture of an entrance fort, closure to blockade runners, occupation of a port city, and reopening of a through corridor as different state changes.
8. Preserve exact ship and troop quantities as historical evidence. No quantity in this packet is a movement value or capacity.
9. Use D497 rail IDs only as explicit cross-references to underlying evidence. They never count as a second independent historical family and never authorize a joined schema.
10. Where the core source floor fails, retain a labelled limitation or omit the edge. The upper White River, upper Arkansas, Potomac transport capacity, and portions of the Texas coast are deliberately not filled by inference.

## 12. Opening-, mid-, and late-war evidence summary

### Opening war: 1861-62

- The strongest western evidence is not “rivers were open”; it is a sequence. Cairo/Paducah concentration reached Fort Henry, a control change opened a bounded Tennessee reach to Muscle Shoals, Fort Donelson changed the Cumberland/Nashville relationship, and Island No. 10 required separate gunboat and transport solutions.
- The lower Mississippi likewise changed in stages: delta-channel entry, passage of Forts Jackson and St. Philip, New Orleans surrender/occupation, subsequent ascent, and a continuing interruption at Vicksburg/Port Hudson.
- Eastern coastwise capability was substantial but conditional. Fort Monroe received a large army by naval transport, York/James reach depended on withdrawal and passage control, Roanoke required a shallow-water route choice, and Port Royal needed channel survey before it became a base.

### Mid-war: 1863-64

- The Vicksburg case proves that an enemy-held chokepoint could be passed by selected vessels without being controlled. The downstream rendezvous and Bruinsburg landing were necessary additional steps; through Mississippi control followed only after Vicksburg and Port Hudson fell.
- Lower Arkansas reach is evidenced to Arkansas Post, while the same operation records failure toward Little Rock. The source set therefore supports a bounded lower corridor and rejects a silent upper edge.
- The Red River case is the packet's strongest warning against calendar-free navigability. The force reached Alexandria/upper waters, then historically abnormal low water stranded vessels and required temporary engineering for withdrawal. Shreveport remained an objective, not an accomplished large-force water endpoint.
- In the east, the 1864 James/City Point case supports protected landing and depot reach, while Drewry's Bluff still prevented simple passage to Richmond.

### Late war: 1864-65

- Mobile Bay demonstrates three different states: Federal passage/control of the entrance, closure of the port to blockade runners, and continued Confederate control of Mobile city until 1865.
- Fort Fisher demonstrates another staged sequence: transport availability and joint landing, capture of the approach fort, closure of Wilmington's runner access, then later capture of Wilmington city.
- Dahlgren's Savannah-to-Beaufort observation supports coastwise transfer but explicitly rejects instantaneous mass movement. This is qualitative evidence for bounded availability, not a capacity number.
- Late-war Union control expanded water reach, but harbor clearance, local mines/obstructions, remaining defenders, weather, and transfer ashore did not disappear when a map color changed.

## 16. D74, source-integrity, anti-Lost-Cause, and dignity risks

1. **D74 absolute:** water reach may constrain strategic options only through a separately authorized conquest law. It cannot hard-code battle outcomes, firepower, damage, surrender, morale, or “historical winner” gates.
2. **Successful passage is not a probability table.** Vicksburg, New Orleans, Mobile Bay, and Red River were contingent operations. Deriving reusable success percentages from them would fabricate a lever.
3. **Control language must remain political and military, not celebratory.** Confederate river defenses served a slaveholding rebellion. Union advances do not erase Union racism, coercion, civilian harm, disease, displacement, or the agency of Black people who supplied intelligence, labor, military service, and claims to freedom.
4. **No Lost Cause framing:** forts are not romantic “guardians” detached from what they protected. Confederate logistics and ports supported the preservation of slavery; technical competence does not confer moral equivalence.
5. **Labor provenance matters.** Fortifications, obstructions, wharves, and supply systems could rely on enslaved or coerced labor. A future law must not turn that exploitation into a neutral productivity bonus or anonymous scenery.
6. **No Fort Pillow spectacle:** river geography may locate a corridor, but massacre/no-quarter history remains under its shipped dignity law and never becomes a scored water-route event.
