## 2. The election-system scope (adjudicated evidence)

Stamps: **[V]** Verified (≥2 independent non-tertiary families) · **[I]** Inferred

### 2a. The 1862–63 midterm cycle (the first pressure valve)

- **[V] Democrats made substantial House gains; the Republican–Unionist coalition kept
  control.** Both surviving families agree (HistoryNet; relay: Wikipedia's 98–73-of-183
  table, re-verified verbatim at the top loop). HistoryNet's framing: "the smallest net
  loss of House seats in twenty years" for a president's party. Exact swing varies by
  compilation (**+28 to +31** — display as a range; pin from Dubin/Martis at spec time).
  Border-state Union candidates salvaged the majority.
- **[V] The preliminary Emancipation Proclamation (Sept 22 1862) was a central campaign
  driver, attacked in explicitly racialized terms** (HistoryNet + relay-corroborated;
  the New York Day Book's "Shall the Working Classes be Equalized with Negroes?" slogan
  is single-sourced to HistoryNet — display the quote attributed, not as a second fact).
- **[V] Inconclusive fall-1862 outcomes (Antietam ~23,000 combined casualties;
  Perryville; Corinth) fed a stalemate perception Democrats exploited** (HistoryNet +
  relay; the casualty figure corroborated by the repo's Verified Antietam data).
- **[V] Habeas suspension, military arrests, and conscription fueled civil-liberties
  charges and disillusioned War Democrats** (HistoryNet + the habeas primary-source rows
  in §2c).
- **[V] New York fell: Horatio Seymour (D) won the governorship Nov 4 1862 and publicly
  denounced the draft and the Proclamation** (NGA + Empire State Plaza).
- **[V] New Jersey fell: Joel Parker (D) won Nov 4 1862** (NGA + relayed returns
  ~56.8%); his War-Democrat posture (pro-war, anti-Lincoln-policy) **[I]** single-relay.
- **[I] The Union coalition itself split on emancipation** (Indiana/Ohio Unionists
  downplayed slavery; Kentucky's Union Democrats disavowed the Proclamation) —
  single-family; display Inferred.

**Game reading:** 1862 is a **bounded setback with an explicit floor** — the historical
outcome is "opposition surges, administration keeps the gavel." Any 1862 cycle card must
teach BOTH halves; a wipeout render or a no-consequence render are both wrong.

### 2b. The 1864 presidential cycle (the grand hinge)

- **[V] Lincoln was renominated by the National Union coalition at Baltimore (convention
  opened June 7 1864), Andrew Johnson replacing Hamlin; the platform pledged
  no-compromise war to unconditional surrender AND a constitutional amendment abolishing
  slavery** (Miller Center + the American Presidency Project platform text, both
  re-fetched at the top loop; relay: Wikipedia).
- **[V] The Democratic convention (Chicago, Aug 29–31 1864) nominated McClellan/Pendleton
  on the Vallandigham-written "after four years of failure… immediate efforts… for a
  cessation of hostilities" peace plank; McClellan repudiated the plank in his acceptance
  letter, insisting reunion precede ceasefire** (American Presidency Project verbatim
  text + Project MUSE + Miller Center; relay: Wikipedia; a period anti-peace broadside
  corroborates via a dealer catalog).
- **[V] The Blind Memorandum (Aug 23 1864):** "it seems exceedingly probable that this
  Administration will not be re-elected"; the cabinet signed it sight-unseen; opened only
  after the victory (House Divided/Dickinson + Guelzo/Friends of the Lincoln Collection).
- **[V-interpretation] August 1864 was the low-water mark; McPherson's judgment is that
  an August election would likely have been lost** (The Conversation-citing-McPherson +
  Brookings — both secondary commentary; display as attributed historiographic
  judgment, not as a counterfactual fact).
- **[V] The reversal was military: Mobile Bay (Aug 5), Atlanta (Sept 2), and Sheridan's
  Valley run (Third Winchester Sept 19 · Fisher's Hill Sept 22 · Tom's Brook Oct 9 ·
  Cedar Creek Oct 19) gutted the "failure" plank in the nine weeks before the vote**
  (Miller Center + NPS; corroborated by the repo's shipped Atlanta and Cedar Creek
  packets).
- **[V] The result (Nov 8 1864): 212–21 electoral; McClellan carried only Delaware,
  Kentucky, New Jersey; ~55% of the popular vote** (National Archives official record +
  Miller Center, both re-fetched; relay: Wikipedia's ~2.21M/~1.81M popular figures —
  display popular totals as approximate).
- **[V] Louisiana and Tennessee cast no counted electoral votes** (NARA's official
  no-votes-cast record + relay); the finer framing — Unionist governments held elections
  whose electors Congress refused to count — **[I]** single-relay; pin the Feb 1865
  joint resolution at spec time if a card wants it.
- **[V] Under the landslide, the pivotal states were close: New York by under 1 point,
  Pennsylvania by under 4, Illinois/Indiana single digits** (HistoryNet + Civil War
  Months).
- **[V-interpretation] The election functioned as a referendum on war-to-victory plus
  abolition vs negotiated peace — the vote itself was Union grand strategy** (Miller
  Center + the McPherson-family commentary; this is the standard framing).
- **[V] The anti-Lincoln press ran sustained racialized opposition; the word
  "miscegenation" was coined in an 1864 hoax pamphlet by New York World staffers Croly
  and Wakeman to inflame emancipation fears** (The Daily Beast + relay; already modeled
  in the repo's Verified `data/press.json` ny-world row — the press system is the
  natural carrier).

**Game reading:** the 1864 verdict is **weariness vs battlefield news**, exactly the
shape the shipped clock formula already encodes (§3). The research adds calibration
anchors (August nadir → September–October reversal), the press channel, and the
referendum framing for teaching text.

### 2c. War-weariness inputs (the meter's historical anatomy)

- **[V] The Vallandigham arc (1863):** the Mount Vernon speech against Burnside's
  General Order 38 (May 1) → arrest (May 5) → five-member military commission conviction
  → Lincoln commutes to banishment through Confederate lines → Canada → runs for Ohio
  governor in absentia and loses to Brough → slips back in 1864 and writes the Chicago
  peace plank (Bill of Rights Institute + UMN commission document + Britannica + OSU
  eHistory + HistoryNet).
- **[V] The New York City draft riots (Jul 13–16 1863):** triggered by the first
  Enrollment Act lottery and the $300 commutation (~a laborer's year); the violence
  turned racially murderous — the Colored Orphan Asylum burned (200+ children escaped);
  troops from Gettysburg restored order (HISTORY.com + relay-with-named-authority
  (Bernstein)). **[D] The death toll is itself disputed:** official ~119; scholarly
  estimates range to ~1,200 — **display the dispute; it is the record.**
- **[V] Habeas suspension came in three stages:** regional 1861 Maryland (Merryman) →
  Proclamation 94 nationwide (Sept 24 1862) → the Habeas Corpus Suspension Act (Mar 3
  1863) (Gilder Lehrman primary + HISTORY/NCC + relay).
- **[I] Arrests without civil trial swept up editors, judges, and minor politicians** —
  the pattern is real (JALA); every specific tally the gather offered was unsupported
  and is DROPPED (§7); if a card wants numbers, pin Neely (*The Fate of Liberty*,
  ~13,000+ military arrests, mostly border-zone and non-political) at spec time.
- **[V] Early's raid reached the District (Jul 1864):** ~10,000–14,000 crossed the
  Potomac July 5, won Monocacy July 9, hit Fort Stevens July 11–12 before VI Corps
  arrived by steamship; Lincoln stood on the parapet under fire — the only sitting
  president under direct enemy fire; the Holmes "get down" line is legend (NPS + ABT;
  the claim's own legend-flag preserved).
- **[V] Press reaction split by distance:** national editors were nearly blasé ("the
  annual invasion of Maryland…"), while D.C. itself genuinely panicked (NPS +
  Smithsonian/Warfare History Network).
- **[V] The greenback–gold premium was a real-time war-confidence barometer** —
  non-convertible greenbacks made the gold price track expected war cost and victory
  probability, spiking on defeats and falling on victories (NBER W5381 + popular
  corroboration). **[I]** gold touched ~285 (a greenback worth ~35¢) on Jul 11 1864
  with Early at the gates — directionally consistent, exact figure Inferred pending
  Mitchell's tables.

**Game reading:** the weariness meter's historical analogue is the gold premium — a
CONTINUOUS confidence index moved by battle news, draft policy, and civil-liberties
shocks. The draft riots and the Vallandigham arc are the canonical weariness *event*
cards; both already have press-system condition hooks (`the-draft`,
`habeas-suspension`).

### 2d. Emancipation politics (the axis both cycles turned on)

- **[V] Sequence:** compensated-emancipation offer to the border states (Mar 1862) →
  rejected (Jul 1862) → preliminary Proclamation held on Seward's advice until a victory
  → issued Sept 22 1862, five days after Antietam, effective Jan 1 1863 (National
  Archives + Constituting America + JALA + Lincoln Institute).
- **[V] Both versions exempted the loyal border states and occupied areas** — a
  deliberate border-state retention choice (NARA primary text + secondary).
- **[V] Kentucky's backlash was statutory:** the legislature condemned the Proclamation
  and in early March 1863 barred Proclamation-freed people from the state (KY
  Legislative Research Commission + Lincoln Institute + Emerging Civil War).
- **[V] The Proclamation opened Black enlistment; GO 143 (May 22 1863) created the
  Bureau of Colored Troops** (NARA + National Museum of the U.S. Army + the repo's
  Verified USCT section).
- **[V] USCT pay discrimination was policy, then reversed under pressure:** $10 minus $3
  clothing = $7 net vs a white private's $13; Sgt. William Walker's pay strike ended in
  his execution (Feb 29 1864); Congress equalized June 15 1864 retroactive to Jan 1
  1864, full retroactivity Mar 3 1865 (Freedmen and Southern Society Project + Military
  Times + NARA lesson).
- **[I] Douglass's "Men of Color, to Arms!"** — the "brass letters U.S." passage is
  genuine (NMUSA + the repo's Verified quote row), but the gather's March 2 1863 date
  pin was defectively sourced; display "March 1863" (broadside date Mar 21 vs Mar 2
  variance) — the date is Inferred.
- **[V] The 13th Amendment passed the House Jan 31 1865, 119–56 (8 not voting), barely
  clearing two-thirds, after Senate passage (Apr 1864) and a failed House vote (Jun
  1864)** (NARA + HISTORY.com). **[I]** passage mechanics — lame-duck Democratic
  switchers and patronage (the Yeaman Denmark appointment) — core is real (Lincoln
  Institute + HISTORY.com) but every precise count the gather offered was internally
  inconsistent and is DROPPED (§7). **[I]** the Rollins lobbying episode ("the next
  Congress passes it anyway") — uncontested but effectively single-family.

**Game reading:** emancipation is BOTH a weariness input (1862 backlash, Copperhead
racial politics) AND the 1864 platform's forward pledge. The shipped press system
already carries the `emancipation` condition and `emancipationReacted` flag; the 13th
Amendment is an endings/teaching hook keyed to the election verdict, not a new lever.

### 2e. The soldier vote (1864's most game-legible subsystem)

- **[I] ~19 Northern states enacted field/absentee soldier voting by Nov 1864**
  (Smithsonian single-family; pin the exact table from Benton at spec time).
- **[V] Scale and lean: ~150,000 of ~1M eligible soldiers voted in the field; the
  soldier share ran ~78% for Lincoln vs ~54% civilian** (Smithsonian verbatim + NPS
  direction: "over 70%" military vs 55% popular). **Display as a range (75–80%).**
- **[D] The counted tally is disputed BY NATURE:** ABT's compilation counts 40,247
  field votes (30,503 Lincoln, 75.8%) vs the ~150,000 estimate — partial tallies,
  mixed-home-count states, and lost records make a single true count unrecoverable.
  **The dispute is the teaching row.**
- **[V] Five states (Indiana, Illinois, Delaware, New Jersey, Oregon) had NO field-vote
  law — furlough was the only route** (Emerging Civil War + U. Illinois STORIED +
  Friends of the Lincoln Collection).
- **[V] Lincoln to Sherman, Sept 19 1864 (primary, verbatim): "Indiana is the only
  important State voting in October whose soldiers cannot vote in the field," asking
  that sick/wounded be furloughed home** (Lincoln Institute family carrying the letter
  text + Emerging Civil War/Smithsonian corroboration of the Morton furlough tie).
  **[V] Indiana's October result: Morton won by ~21,000**, with furloughed soldiers
  home (relayed official returns + the ECW/Smithsonian-sourced Walters "old Abe a
  hoist" quote — attribute the quote).
- **[I] Mechanics rows (single-family NPS, re-fetched verbatim at the top loop — honest
  single-source flavor):** PA voted at the company captain's HQ with under-10 groups
  mailing sealed proxy ballots; OH opened company-HQ polls (artillery/senior officers at
  the most convenient company); CT sent commissioners with printed ballots; the 29th
  Maine near Cedar Creek went 175–41 Lincoln; the Vermont Brigade a large majority.
  **[I]** NY's proxy law + Depew's express-company distribution (Lincoln Institute
  family). **[I]** seven Seymour-appointed NY agents arrested for forging soldier
  ballots (relay core only; the named-agents/Doubleday specifics are DROPPED, §7).
- **DROPPED:** the "~30,000 PA furloughs," the PA 5,712/14,363 decisive-margin pair
  stays **[I]** single-family, and the six-state "soldiers supplied Lincoln's margin"
  attribution (§7).

**Game reading:** the soldier vote is a *documented lean* (75–80% vs 54%) plus a
*state-law variance table* plus a *furlough channel* — perfect card/teaching material.
Whether it becomes a bounded formula term is an ARC 5 spec decision (§3.5); this packet

### 2f. Confederate politics (the mirror WITHOUT an election hinge)

- **[V] The Confederacy had no organized parties; historians classify members
  retroactively as pro-/anti-administration** (standard consensus across the fetched
  corpus: Civil War Months + the Yearns/NCpedia scholarship; relay: Wikipedia).
- **[V-with-pin-owed] The 1863 congressional elections swung hard against Davis:**
  anti-administration House strength 26 → 41 of 106 (pro 80 → 65); the Senate stayed
  narrowly pro-administration; the swing concentrated in AL/FL/GA/NC/SC/TX (32 of the
  41), with nine of ten Georgia incumbents defeated — frame the GA figure as INCUMBENT
  DEFEATS, not a party count (Civil War Months + relay re-verified verbatim; **the two
  surviving publishers agree exactly but neither is scholarly — pin from Martis at spec
  time before any data row ships**).
- **[V] Davis vs the governors — Vance (NC):** fought conscription/impressment/habeas,
  read exemptions expansively, and in late 1863 refused Davis 7,000 troops, raising a
  Home Guard instead (NCpedia + Yearns/JSH + relay). **[V]** NC reportedly kept civil
  courts and habeas functioning throughout — preserve the "reportedly" framing
  (NCpedia + Yearns + relay).
- **[V] Davis vs the governors — Brown (GA):** called conscription unconstitutional
  ("a consolidated despotism"); his March 1864 extra-session message declared the CS
  habeas suspension "a fell blow at the liberties of these States" (DocSouth primary +
  HathiTrust; Wikipedia relay).
- **[V] The CS Congress suspended habeas three times — Feb 27 1862, Oct 1862, Feb 1864
  (to Aug 1 1864) — each delegating designation to Davis** (DocSouth primary House Bill
  267 + Essential Civil War Curriculum + relay). **[V]** Davis's Feb 3 1864 request
  ("citizens of well-known disloyalty… accomplish treason under the form of law")
  (ECWC + Civil War Months). **CORRECTED:** Henry Foote's "never again… unless the
  enemy is within sight of the city" belongs to the **1862** debate, not 1864 — the
  gather welded it forward ~1.5 years; the 1864 committee-narrowing (specific threatened
  towns/districts) is separately accurate (§7).
- **[V] The North Carolina peace movement:** Holden's Raleigh *Standard* backed Vance in
  1862, then ran ~100 peace meetings by summer 1863; Confederate soldiers sacked the
  paper's office (Sept 1863); Holden ran as the 1864 Peace candidate and **Vance
  crushed him Aug 4 1864 (~58,070 to 14,491, ~80/20)** — Vance ran as the true
  civil-liberties man while attacking Holden's secessionist past (HistoryNet + NCpedia +
  relayed returns; exact figures relay-pinned, scholarly pin at spec time).
- **[I — top-loop adjudicated; the one row the refute layer never processed]**
  CS dissent — the 1863 swing AND the 1864 peace movement — ran on conscription (and
  its class-skewed exemptions), impressment, inflation/shortage, and habeas/martial
  law, **not** on abolition; both factions were committed to slavery. The refuter
  returned no verdict on this synthesis row; carried Inferred because every component
  grievance is independently Verified above and the negative half states the standard
  consensus. **This row is load-bearing for §4:** rendering CS dissent as proto-Union
  or anti-slavery sentiment would be a Lost-Cause-adjacent mischaracterization.

**Game reading:** the CS side gets weariness/dissent WITHOUT an election hinge — Davis
served a single six-year term; there was no wartime CS presidential election. The 1863
congressional swing, the governors' obstruction, and the NC peace movement are the CS
pressure cards; the shipped `src/81-divergence.js` side-gate (US election card vs CS
"home-front breaks" card off the same clock state) is exactly the right precedent.
