## The five teaching cards (multi-voice: consensus · scholarly dissent · named-and-countered Lost Cause · primary sources)
1. **`cotton-king-chance` — Did King Cotton diplomacy ever have a chance?** Consensus (Owsley→Surdam/Jones): it failed and was structurally unlikely — Britain's ~1M+ bale 1860-61 glut, India/Egypt/Brazil substitution, Northern "King Wheat" grain leverage, slavery politics, war-risk. Dissent (Foreman/Jones): autumn-1862 mediation came genuinely close (Gladstone's Newcastle "made a nation" speech). Lost Cause countered: "cotton was King, only Yankee force denied recognition" inverts the evidence.
2. **`cotton-self-embargo` — coercion or self-sabotage?** Consensus (Owsley/McPherson/Ball): a committee/planter-driven, non-statutory embargo that forfeited the one blockade-free export year + the specie/arms-credit it would have bought. Dissent (Wise/Lebergott): the easy-1861-export-year is partly counterfactual (blockade + insurance + shipping limits would have throttled exports anyway). **Game takeaway:** withholding cotton in 1861 should *feel tempting* but quietly bleed specie while coercion leverage stays ~0 — the historical self-sabotage, not a reward.
3. **`cotton-blockade-decisive` — how decisive was the blockade?** Consensus (Surdam): decisive in the AGGREGATE despite high per-voyage success — a *selection effect* (the blockade deterred big slow merchantmen onto small fast low-capacity steamers; counting captures measures the wrong thing). Dissent (Owsley/Price's "leaky sieve"): porous, never legally binding, most runs got through. **Game takeaway:** per-voyage odds stay high (~5-in-6 early → ~1-in-3 by 1864) yet aggregate cotton revenue collapses — optimizing single runs can't rescue the aggregate.
4. **`cotton-runner-help-hurt` — lifeline or leak?** Consensus: both/and — indispensable for ordnance (Gorgas/Huse imported most CS small arms) BUT profit-driven distortion toward luxury cargo forced the late-1863/64 New Plan cargo regulation. Dissent (Beringer et al.): blockade overstated; defeat was will/land. **Game takeaway:** the lifeline and the distortion are the same pipe — luxury cargo earns far more than rifles (returns into the hundreds of %), so leaving cargo to the profit motive starves the armies.
5. **`cotton-erlanger` — lifeline or fleecing?** Consensus (Gentry/Lester/Weidenmier): expensive credit on hard terms with genuinely innovative cotton-convertibility; £3M, 7%, taken at 77, public-offered at 90; slid after Vicksburg/Gettysburg; the CS spent its own proceeds propping the price → worthless at surrender. Dissent (Ball): part of a broader pattern of Confederate financial mismanagement. Lost Cause + antisemitic-conspiracist fringe both named and countered.

## The numbers audit — 9 sim claims (6 CONFIRMED, 3 CORRECTED, 0 disputed)
- **CONFIRMED:** capture prob 0.10(1861)→0.33(1864); runner success 0.90→0.67 (the complement — treat as one coin, not two params); Britain's 1860-61 cotton glut + Lancashire famine peak winter 1862-63 (~500k on relief, ~60% idle); Indian/Egyptian/Brazilian substitution (~75% of British cotton from India by 1862; Egypt exports ~tripled); cotton 3¢/lb South vs 45¢-$1/lb Europe, runner returns up to ~700% (Banshee, 8 runs); recognition collapsed after Antietam + Emancipation (Sep 1862), clearly dead after winter 1862-63.
- **CORRECTED:** (3) the "~11% / 17M-bale" export figure is muddled — true blockade-run cotton to Europe ≈0.5M bales, ~3-4% of a normal ~3.5M/yr export baseline (the ~95%-collapse teaching point holds; drop the specific numbers; the sim doesn't read that field). (4) ~500 Union ships is fine, but you can't divide ~1,500 runner *vessels* lost by ~2,650 *voyages* — different denominators (one steamer made many trips); derive per-voyage odds only from the capture schedule. (5) **Erlanger: 77 was Erlanger's BUY price, 90 the public offer; net realized ~£1.4M of £3M (~45% of face), NOT 72%** — applied to `data/economy.json` foreignLoan (D19).
- **Honesty notes carried into the model:** Owsley overstates cotton's diplomatic leverage (the glut is exactly why the embargo backfired); the recognition window *narrowed* after Sep 1862 rather than slamming shut — the S1c sim models a 1862 window that forecloses in 1863, which the audit explicitly endorsed.

## How the sim uses this (S1c mechanics, `src/60-blockade.js`)
The blockade tick runs each strategic turn BEFORE production. CS: 1861 embargo → ~0 cotton revenue (trap) → auto-lifts 1862 → runner success × open-ports sets exports → `C.funds`; a dynamic `importFactor` (0.85 × runnerSuccess × portFactor) gates CS small arms in `prodOnResolve`; recognition rises in the 1862 famine window, forecloses 1863, and pulls `clock.intervention` toward its low reality. Blockade-depth is a realism toggle (full/flat/off, R14). US: no cotton, full imports, Anaconda + King-Wheat suppress intervention risk. Probe-verified arc (16 turns): rev≈4 (1861 trap) → recog→25/intervention→19 (1862 window) → recog→0 (1863 foreclosed) → ports 2 / importFactor 0.28 / arms 2-vs-6-off (1864).
